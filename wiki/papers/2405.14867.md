# Improved Distribution Matching Distillation (DMD2)

## TL;DR (project lens)

DMD2 is the cleanest **score-distillation + real-data GAN** recipe published, and the one to copy when distilling a pretrained raw-audio flow-matching / diffusion teacher into a 1- or 4-step student. Three load-bearing tricks distinguish it from the original DMD and from pure score distillers like SiD-DiT (`papers/2509.25127.md`):

1. **Drops DMD's expensive regression-loss dataset.** No more pre-rendering millions of (z, y) teacher pairs (~700 A100-days for SDXL-scale).
2. **Two-time-scale update rule (TTUR)** — 5 fake-critic updates per generator update — stabilises pure distribution matching after the regression crutch is removed.
3. **GAN loss against real images** plugs the hole that reverse-KL distillation leaves: the reverse KL `KL(p_fake ‖ p_real)` is **mode-seeking**, so removing the regression term invites mode collapse. The real-data discriminator restores diversity *and* lets the student exceed the teacher (teacher's score is itself approximate).

A 4-step student beats the SDXL 100-NFE teacher in user studies (24% wins on quality, comparable text alignment); ImageNet-64 FID 1.28 with a single forward pass. For us, this is the *baseline* recipe to apply on top of any pretrained audio FM teacher we want to compress for CPU inference.

## Why this matters here (raw audio on CPU)

Two competing distillation families currently lead the diffusion-acceleration race:

- **Pure score distillation** (DMD, SiD, SiD-DiT). Theoretically clean: minimise an approximate KL between student and teacher distributions via the *difference of two score functions*. No real data needed. But reverse-KL is mode-seeking; with no real-data anchor and a noisy fake critic, training collapses or oscillates (DMD2 §4.2 documents the pixel-brightness drift; SiD avoids it by using a Fisher-style objective instead of KL).
- **Pure adversarial distillation** (ADD, LADD, SDXL-Turbo, SDXL-Lightning, UFOGen). Anchors the student to real data but throws away the teacher's gradient information; struggles with text alignment, CFG, and stability.

DMD2 says **use both**: KL-divergence score gradient *as the main signal*, GAN loss *as the diversity / quality anchor*. The ablations in Tab. 3–4 are the cleanest empirical case for this hybrid we have: score-distillation alone (with TTUR) gets FID 2.61 on ImageNet-64; adding the GAN drops it to 1.51. GAN-alone is FID 2.56; combining is strictly better. The two objectives are complementary, not redundant.

For audio, this matters because **ADD/LADD-style approaches are the natural baseline** people will reach for (it's how Stable Audio Open / Riffusion-Turbo-style fast audio gen has been done) and they have a known failure mode: timbral mode collapse, over-saturation, loss of texture diversity. DMD2 is the publication that gives a principled recipe to keep the score-distillation gradient *while still anchoring on real data* — exactly what an audio CPU student needs.

## Background: original DMD in one paragraph

DMD trains a 1-step generator `G_θ` by descending the gradient of an expected reverse-KL between the diffused real and diffused fake distributions. The trick: the gradient is the **difference of two score functions** at the diffused student output:

```
∇L_DMD = -E_t,z [ ∫ (s_real(F(G_θ(z), t), t) - s_fake(F(G_θ(z), t), t)) · (dG_θ(z)/dθ) dz ]
```

where `F` is forward diffusion (noise injection at level `t`), `s_real = (-x_t + α_t µ_real)/σ_t²` comes from the **frozen teacher** denoiser `µ_real`, and `s_fake` comes from a **dynamically-trained fake denoiser** `µ_fake` updated on-line via standard denoising score matching against student samples. DMD then adds a **regression loss** `L_reg = E_{(z,y)} d(G_θ(z), y)` (typically LPIPS) on a precomputed set of teacher noise→image pairs `(z, y)` to stabilise training. This regression term is the bottleneck that DMD2 removes.

## The three DMD2 changes, what each one does, what fails without it

### 1. Remove the regression loss

Two reasons: (a) it costs ~700 A100-days to build the (z, y) dataset at SDXL scale (already 4× DMD2's total training compute), and (b) it caps the student at teacher quality, defeating the point of distribution matching. Removing it naively → FID 3.48 on ImageNet (vs 2.62 with regression), and visible **drift of generated-sample statistics over training** (pixel brightness oscillates without converging — Appendix C, Fig. 8).

The diagnosis: the **fake critic `µ_fake`** is dynamically optimised on a non-stationary target (the student keeps moving), so it lags. A lagging fake critic gives biased score-difference gradients, which push the student in the wrong direction, which makes the fake critic lag more.

### 2. Two-time-scale update rule (TTUR)

Inspired by Heusel et al.'s TTUR for GANs (the same paper that defined FID). **Update `µ_fake` 5 times per `G_θ` update.** This keeps the fake critic locked to the student's current output distribution and breaks the lag feedback loop.

Empirical ablation on the update frequency: `1 update` is the unstable baseline; `5` is the sweet spot for ImageNet (FID 2.61, matches DMD-with-regression); `10` is stable but slow; an asynchronous-learning-rate alternative (5× larger LR on fake critic) is strictly worse. Also converges **faster** than the original DMD-with-regression — TTUR is not just a substitute, it's an improvement.

### 3. GAN loss against real images

Even after TTUR fixes stability, the student is still bottlenecked by teacher score approximation errors (the teacher itself isn't perfect). Fix: **train a discriminator `D` to distinguish real images from generator outputs** (both diffused to a random noise level `t`).

```
L_GAN = E_{x ~ p_real, t} [ log D(F(x, t)) ] + E_{z, t} [ -log(D(F(G_θ(z), t))) ]
```

Generator maximises classifier failure; `D` and `G` play the standard non-saturating game. The **discriminator is attached to the bottleneck of the fake denoiser** `µ_fake` (a stack of 4×4 stride-2 convs + GroupNorm + SiLU pooling to 4×4 then to a scalar), so most of `D`'s weights are *already* the fake critic — minimal extra parameters. Inspired by LADD / SDXL-Lightning / DiffusionGAN (`papers/2408.08019.md`).

**Why this matters conceptually:** reverse-KL is mode-seeking. With only the score-distillation gradient, the student converges to a high-density mode of the teacher but happily drops minority modes; combined with imperfect teacher scores it tends to over-saturate / over-smooth (Fig. 7 third column: "no GAN" produces flat, plastic-looking images). The GAN against *real* data injects mode-covering pressure and corrects teacher-score bias the student couldn't escape otherwise. Adding GAN: FID 2.61 → 1.51 on ImageNet; on SDXL the no-GAN ablation gets patch-FID 27.66 vs DMD2's 20.86.

### 4. Multi-step generator with backward simulation

DMD was 1-step only. For SDXL the 1-step generator can't cover the full prompt distribution (capacity bottleneck), so DMD2 introduces a 4-step sampler. Fixed schedule (`t = 999, 749, 499, 249`); each step alternates **denoise** `x̂_{t_i} = G_θ(x_{t_i}, t_i)` and **forward-renoise** `x_{t_{i+1}} = α_{t_{i+1}} x̂_{t_i} + σ_{t_{i+1}} ε` — same as a consistency-model sampler.

Train-test mismatch problem: standard multi-step distillation feeds the generator **noisy real images** at training time but **noisy student samples** at inference (post step 1). DMD2 closes this with **backward simulation**: at training time, simulate the inference sampling chain *with the current student* up to step `i`, then supervise step `i+1` on those simulated inputs. Tractable because the student is only 4 steps total.

A concurrent paper (Imagine Flash) had the same idea but kept a regression loss; that regression term itself suffers from train-test mismatch (teacher was never trained on synthetic inputs). DMD2's distribution-matching loss is parameterisation-agnostic to `G_θ`'s inputs, so backward simulation actually closes the gap. Without backward simulation: patch-FID 24.21 → DMD2 20.86.

## Architecture, training, inference cost

- **Generator `G_θ`**: same UNet as the teacher (SDXL UNet @ 1024², SD1.5 UNet @ 512², EDM UNet @ 64²). Initialised from teacher weights.
- **Real score `µ_real`**: frozen teacher diffusion model.
- **Fake critic `µ_fake`**: a copy of the teacher, trained on-line on student samples via DSM. Same architecture and parameter count as the teacher.
- **Discriminator**: prediction head on `µ_fake`'s middle block — 4×4 stride-2 convs + GroupNorm + SiLU, pool to 4×4, linear projection. Adds negligible parameters.

Three networks live in memory at training time. Optimiser: AdamW lr 2e-6 (gen), weight decay 0.01. ImageNet training: 7× A100, batch 280, 200K iters (2 days) for the base; +150K iters @ 5e-7 with GAN enabled for the extended run (~5 days). SDXL training is heavier (CPU offload).

**Inference cost** is **purely a function of `# forward passes through the UNet`** — same as teacher. 1-step = 1× UNet forward; 4-step = 4× UNet forward, no other overhead. The fake critic and discriminator are training-time only; at inference DMD2 = the teacher's UNet, called 1–4 times. That's the whole point: it inherits the teacher's parallelism and per-pass cost.

## How this translates to raw audio

The DMD2 recipe ports to audio with minor surgery:

1. **Teacher = pretrained audio FM or diffusion model.** Anything with a denoiser / velocity head and known α_t, σ_t works. Rectified-flow audio teachers convert into a `µ_real(x_t, t)` predictor by reading `µ = (x_t - σ_t · v) / α_t`. Same trick as SiD-DiT (`papers/2509.25127.md`).
2. **Fake critic `µ_fake`** = copy of teacher, trained on-line on student samples with standard FM/DSM loss. **Memory matters here**: three audio UNets at 24 GB-VRAM (4090) is tight. Either the fake critic shares most weights with the teacher (LoRA-style adapter on the teacher) or we shrink it. Paper finds the fake critic can be smaller than the teacher; worth testing on audio.
3. **Discriminator**: attach a small conv head to the fake critic's bottleneck — for a 1-D UNet, equivalent to a stack of 1-D strided convs pooling to a scalar logit per (audio, t) pair. The diffused-input trick (real *and* fake are noised to the same `t` before classification) is essentially DiffusionGAN — it stabilises the discriminator and reduces shortcut features. We should re-use it.
4. **GAN against real audio**: we need a real-audio reference set the discriminator can see. For an unconditional drum-loop generator: a curated drum sample bank. For text-to-audio: paired (text, audio) data — limiting, since DMD2's whole point was distillation needs no paired data, but the GAN re-introduces a data dependency. The teacher itself was trained on paired data so this isn't an extra constraint, just a clarification.
5. **TTUR**: 5 fake-critic updates per generator update. On audio at 24 kHz × 4 s = 96k samples, each fake-critic step is one full UNet forward+backward; this 5× factor is the dominant training cost. Probably not negotiable — without TTUR the recipe falls back to the unstable baseline.
6. **Backward simulation for multi-step**: same idea verbatim. For audio, where one-step quality is more elusive than for image (phase coherence, transients), expect to need 2–4 step student. Multi-step student samplers in audio are essentially free (the same one UNet, called 2–4 times instead of 1) so this is a worthwhile knob.

**The diversity lesson is the most important one for audio:** raw-audio generation is *more* sensitive to mode collapse than images because perceptual diversity in audio is dominated by texture/timbre, which sits in exactly the long-tail high-frequency content that reverse-KL distillation strips out first. A pure score-distillation audio student is likely to converge to a generic "average" timbre. **The real-data GAN anchor is what saves diversity** — drop it at your peril. This is the failure mode ADD/LADD-derived audio fast-generators tend to exhibit too, just from the opposite direction.

CPU inference cost: identical to teacher per UNet-forward × NFE. If we have a teacher running at, say, 0.5× real time on CPU per pass for a 4-s clip, DMD2 1-step gets us to ~2× real-time; 4-step matches the teacher in latency budget but with much better per-step quality. The interesting move is: **smaller student than teacher** + DMD2 distillation, trading network capacity for NFE.

## Strengths

- Cleanest published score-distillation + GAN recipe; ablations are unusually rigorous.
- Removes the dataset-construction bottleneck that made DMD impractical at T2I scale.
- Student exceeds teacher on FID at 100× fewer NFE — first distillation method to do this cleanly.
- Backward-simulation idea is general; we can lift it for any multi-step distillation we try in audio.
- Codebase released.

## Limitations / open questions

- **No CPU benchmark.** All numbers are A100. SDXL-scale inference is far from CPU-realistic regardless.
- **Diversity gap remains** (DMD2 0.61 vs teacher 0.64) — not closed, just narrowed. For audio textures this gap may be perceptually large.
- **Fixed CFG scale at training time.** No flexible test-time guidance control. For audio, if we want a "creativity dial" at inference (variable CFG), DMD2 as-is doesn't support it.
- **Three-network training is heavy.** Teacher + fake critic + (small) discriminator = ~3× teacher VRAM. The audio implementation needs care.
- **No 1-step SDXL result.** DMD2 explicitly retreats to 4-step for SDXL because 1-step capacity is a problem. Suggests model-capacity scaling matters; audio analogues should test 1, 2, 4 steps.
- **GAN re-introduces a real-data dependency** that pure SiD/SiD-DiT avoids. For audio where curated real-audio sets are scarce, the no-data variant (drop GAN, accept lower diversity) may be a forced choice.

## Contrast with neighbours

- **vs. original DMD** (`papers/2509.25127.md` mentions in passing): same DMD reverse-KL skeleton, no regression term, TTUR, plus GAN.
- **vs. SiD / SiD-DiT** (`papers/2509.25127.md`): SiD uses a *Fisher-divergence* score-distillation objective (more stable than DMD's KL flavour, hence no TTUR needed) and an *adversarial* variant `SiD^a_α` for extra gains. DMD2 uses KL + TTUR + GAN. Both end up at "score distillation + adversarial term", but DMD2 leans more heavily on the GAN; SiD-DiT keeps the GAN as an optional add-on. For a pure data-free audio teacher distillation, SiD-DiT's data-free base recipe is the cleaner start; for "I have a real-audio set and want to maximise quality", DMD2 is the recipe.
- **vs. ADD / LADD / SDXL-Lightning / SDXL-Turbo** (related papers in repo): these are *pure* adversarial distillation. DMD2 includes the GAN they have *and* the score-distillation gradient they lack. The 4-step ablations show why this matters: pure GAN gets low whole-image FID but bad patch-FID and bad text alignment — the diffusion gradient anchors local detail and prompt coherence.
- **vs. Consistency Models / CTM / LCM**: trajectory distillation, follows the teacher ODE path; DMD2 is score distillation, allowed to deviate. DMD2 beats CTM on ImageNet-64 1-step (1.28 vs 1.92).

## Things to test in this repo

1. Smallest viable audio teacher + DMD2 → measure CPU latency at 1 / 2 / 4 NFE on 4-s clips.
2. Ablate the GAN term on audio specifically — is the diversity loss as severe as suspected? Measure with FAD or a perceptual diversity score on a multi-modal dataset (drums, sfx, voice).
3. TTUR ratio sweep on audio (1, 3, 5, 10) — image found 5 optimal; audio may differ given different noise schedules and longer training trajectories.
4. Backward simulation for a 2-step audio student vs naive forward-noise-real training — measure the train-test gap directly.
5. Test the "smaller student than teacher" trade-off: distill a 0.5× teacher-size student with DMD2, compare CPU latency / quality vs same-size 1-step student.
