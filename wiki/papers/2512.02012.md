# TL;DR — why this matters for raw-audio CPU gen

iMF is a **distillation-free, from-scratch 1-NFE generator** that hits 1.72 FID on ImageNet 256x256 — closing roughly half the remaining gap to multi-step diffusion without any teacher, GAN, or pretraining. For this project the value isn't the FID number; it's the **training recipe for one-step generators that doesn't depend on a separately trained multi-step model**. The two specific contributions are tactical and portable: (i) reformulate MeanFlow's training loss so the regression target is **network-independent** (a stop-gradient'd compound function `V_θ` predicts the true marginal velocity `v = e − x`, rather than MF's original target `(e − x) − (t − r)·JVP`, which depended on the network's own `u_θ` prediction); (ii) make CFG scale a **conditioning input** at training time instead of a frozen hyperparameter, restoring inference-time guidance flexibility while staying 1-NFE. Both translate directly to any 1-D flow model on raw audio. The headline 1.72 FID also runs on a 610 M-param transformer at ImageNet latent resolution (32×32×4 tokens), which is enormous for our CPU budget — so for us iMF is a **recipe** to apply on a small 1-D backbone, not a checkpoint to use.

# What problem does it solve

The setting is **fastforward generative models** — models that learn to leap across large intervals of the ODE/SDE trajectory in a single (or very few) function evaluations. MeanFlow (Geng et al. 2025, NeurIPS) parameterises an *average* velocity field `u(z_t, r, t) = (1/(t−r)) ∫_r^t v(z_τ) dτ` and uses the **MeanFlow identity**

```
v(z_t) = u(z_t) + (t − r) · d/dt u(z_t)
```

as a training target. The total derivative `d/dt u` is computed via a Jacobian-vector product (JVP) inside the network. Sampling is one Euler step on `u`: `z_0 = z_1 − u_θ(z_1)` with `(r, t) = (0, 1)`.

iMF identifies and fixes two practical problems with that recipe:

1. **The training target depends on the network.** Original MF substitutes the unknown true `u` inside the JVP with the network's own prediction `u_θ`, giving a target `u_tgt = (e − x) − (t − r) · JVP(u_θ; e − x)`. Training optimises `‖u_θ − sg(u_tgt)‖²`. Because `u_tgt` is itself a function of `u_θ`, this is not a standard regression problem; the paper reports the original MF loss has **higher variance and is non-decreasing** during training even when sampling quality improves (Fig. 3).
2. **CFG scale is frozen at training time.** Original MF builds a fixed CFG scale `ω` into the training-time target field `v_cfg = ω·v(z_t | c) + (1 − ω)·v(z_t)`. The paper shows the optimal `ω` shifts with model capacity, training length, *and* NFE budget (Fig. 4), so a frozen `ω` is always suboptimal in at least one of those axes.

# Method

## (1) Reformulating MF as a v-loss with a stop-gradient'd compound function

The MeanFlow identity rearranges to

```
v(z_t) = u(z_t) + (t − r) · d/dt u(z_t)
```

Define a **compound function**

```
V_θ(z_t) ≜ u_θ(z_t) + (t − r) · JVP_sg(u_θ; v_θ)
```

where `v_θ` is the model's own estimate of the instantaneous velocity *inside the JVP only*, and the loss is the standard Flow-Matching regression

```
L_iMF = 𝔼_{t,r,x,e} ‖ V_θ(z_t) − (e − x) ‖²
```

Two design choices distinguish this from the original MF target Eq. (6):

- The regression target is `(e − x)` (the conditional velocity, no `(t − r)·JVP` term). The target is **fully network-independent** — a standard FM target.
- The JVP's tangent vector `v` is *not* approximated by the conditional `(e − x)`. Instead it is approximated by another network prediction `v_θ` of the marginal velocity `v(z_t) = 𝔼[e − x | z_t]`. The marginal `v_θ` has much lower variance than the conditional `(e − x)`, which is the actual fix that stabilises training: Fig. 3 shows the iMF loss decreases monotonically while the original MF loss climbs.

Two ways to obtain `v_θ` without a separate network:

- **Boundary condition.** By definition `v(z_t, t, t) ≡ u(z_t, t, t)`, so just *call the same network with `r = t`*. Zero extra parameters. Empirically sufficient.
- **Auxiliary v-head.** Add a small head (L = 8 layers in their config) sharing most of the trunk; train it with an additional FM loss `‖v_θ − (e − x)‖²`. Used only inside the JVP, not at inference. Slight further quality gain at the cost of training-only parameters.

The stop-gradient on the JVP outcome is kept inside `V_θ` (same as original MF) — the paper notes that, while the formulation is technically valid without it, removing it introduces higher-order gradients w.r.t. θ that make optimisation harder.

## (2) Flexible CFG as conditioning

Instead of baking `ω` into the training target, iMF conditions the network on `ω` (and optionally on a CFG interval `[t_min, t_max]` à la Kynkäänniemi et al. 2024). The training-time target is the standard CFG-warped average velocity

```
v_tgt = (e − x) + (1 − 1/ω) · (u_θ^cfg(z_t | t, c) − u_θ^cfg(z_t | t, ∅))
```

with `ω` sampled per-batch from a power-law distribution biased toward small values (`ω ∝ ω^{−β}` over `[1.0, ω_max=8.0]`, β=1 or 2). `ω = 1` is the unconditional case. The set `Ω = {ω, t_min, t_max}` is fed to the network as additional condition tokens. At inference, `ω` is chosen freely. This recovers near-optimal `ω` for any given model size / training length / NFE without retraining.

A nice byproduct (Tab. 1b): setting `ω = 1.0` at inference is *not* the same as a model trained at fixed `ω = 1.0` — the model trained over the full `ω` range *generalises better* even when asked to do unconditional sampling (FID 20.95 vs 30.76 at `ω = 1`). Training across a range of guidance values acts as a useful regulariser.

## (3) Architecture: in-context conditioning replaces adaLN-zero

Original MF inherits DiT's adaLN-zero for conditioning. With multiple heterogeneous conditions (`r, t, c, ω, t_min, t_max`), summing all condition embeddings inside adaLN-zero degrades performance and adaLN-zero itself is parameter-heavy.

iMF instead uses **in-context conditioning**: each condition is processed by a 2-layer MLP, replicated into multiple tokens (8 for class, 4 for each other condition, per Tab. 4), each with a learnable type embedding, then **concatenated along the sequence axis** with the image-latent tokens. Standard Transformer blocks. No adaLN-zero anywhere.

Effect:
- **~1/3 parameter reduction** at fixed depth and width (133M → 89M for B-size; 610M for XL/2 is ~10% smaller than the MF-XL/2 it replaces).
- FID further drops by 0.48 over `ω`-conditioning alone (Tab. 1c).
- Standard Transformer block plus SwiGLU / RMSNorm / RoPE on top of this gets the final 0.7 FID gain.

| iMF-XL/2| 48    | 1024  | 610M   | 174.6  | **1.72**  | 282.0 |

Backbone is a DiT-style Transformer operating on the latent grid of a frozen pretrained VAE: 256×256 RGB → 32×32×4 latents → patch size 2×2 → 16×16 = 256 image tokens + ~32 condition tokens.

## Training recipe (Tab. 4)

- Adam (β1=0.9, β2=0.95), constant lr 1e-4, 10-epoch linear warmup.
- Batch 256 (ablations) or 1024 (system-level), 240–800 epochs.
- EMA decay 0.9999.
- 50% of samples have `r ≠ t` (the rest have `r = t` so the JVP term vanishes and the loss reduces to plain FM at the boundary).
- `t, r` sampled logit-normal `(−0.4, 1.0)`.
- Class drop 0.1, ω sampled from `Beta(1 or 2)` over `[1, 8]`.
- "Adaptive weighting" from original MF carried over for both the MF loss and the auxiliary v-head loss.

Implementation is in JAX/TPU, on top of the public MF codebase.

# Inference cost — the headline for this project

This is what makes the paper relevant to us at all.

- **1 NFE** for the generator. Sampling is `z_0 = z_1 − u_θ(z_1)`. Two NFEs (one more refinement step) closes a further 0.18 FID (1.72 → 1.54).
- At inference, the auxiliary v-head is dropped; only `u_θ` is needed.
- **VAE decoder is a separate cost** the paper flags explicitly — for a true 1-NFE pipeline at our scale, the tokenizer/decoder dominates. Their stated direction for future work is "efficient tokenizers or pixel-space generation."
- On image latents the XL/2 generator is 174 Gflops per sample. At our CPU target, even the B/2 (25 Gflops) is well above real-time for 1 s of audio (~22 k samples) — direct port not viable, but the *recipe* is.

Ablation contributions are roughly additive (Tab. 1, all on the B/2 backbone, 240 epochs):
1. MF baseline: 6.17 → iMF v-loss: 5.68 (− 0.49)
2. + flexible CFG conditioning: 4.57 (− 1.11)
3. + in-context conditioning (replace adaLN-zero): 4.09 (− 0.48), and **−1/3 params**

5. + longer training (640 ep): 3.39

The v-loss alone (1) does most of the optimisation-stability work; CFG conditioning (2) is the biggest single quality jump; in-context conditioning (3) is mostly a parameter-efficiency / scaling win.

# How this translates to raw audio

The whole point of MeanFlow / iMF is to learn a **one-shot displacement** `z_0 = z_1 − u_θ(z_1)` on the flow-matching trajectory. Translation to raw 1-D audio is almost mechanical:

- **The objective is dimension-agnostic.** Replace image patches with 1-D conv / 1-D transformer tokens over raw samples. The losses, JVP machinery, and CFG conditioning are unchanged. JVP through a 1-D Conv/Transformer is standard in PyTorch (`torch.func.jvp`).
- **Backbone choice matters more than for images.** iMF runs a DiT over 256 latent tokens. For audio at 22.05 kHz × 1 s = 22 050 samples, even a heavy downsampling chain (factor-300 as in CRASH) leaves ~70 tokens — Transformer-friendly. At higher SR or longer clips, sequence length quickly becomes the bottleneck. The natural test is iMF-style training on the **CRASH 1-D U-Net** with σ replaced by `(t, r, ω, …)` and `u_θ` predicting the displacement.
- **No VAE for raw sample-space.** iMF runs on VAE latents because their decoder is dirt cheap relative to the generator. For raw audio we *don't have* an analogous neural latent without buying back a decoder (EnCodec / DAC). The paper explicitly anticipates this — its closing remark argues for "efficient tokenizers or pixel-space generation". We are in the "pixel-space" camp for audio. The interesting open question: does the in-context conditioning + flexible CFG recipe still pay off when the generator is a 1-D conv working directly on samples, with no latent compression?
- **CFG analogue for audio.** Class conditioning maps to instrument / drum-type / pitch labels (cf. CRASH's classifier-mixing trick). Flexible CFG conditioning means we'd no longer have to choose `ω` before training a drum generator — we sweep it at inference for free. Useful in a research-iteration sense.
- **Stability claim is the load-bearing thing.** The original MF training-loss non-monotonicity (Fig. 3) is exactly the kind of pathology that would be hard to debug on a small audio replication if we hadn't read this paper first. Use the iMF v-loss form from the start.
- **Distillation-free is a real advantage on our 4090 budget.** We do not have spare compute to train a multi-step teacher *and* a distilled student. iMF needs only one training run.
- **Phase / waveform fidelity.** MF/iMF makes no claims about phase. The loss is plain `‖V_θ − (e − x)‖²` on raw samples (in audio terms). It is identical to what FM/diffusion does, just at one NFE. Any phase issues we'd see at 50 NFE will also be there at 1 NFE — distillation/one-step doesn't introduce *new* phase artefacts, it just doesn't fix the ones already in the FM target.

# Strengths

- One-NFE, from-scratch, **no distillation, no GAN, no teacher**. Single training run.
- The v-loss reformulation is a small, mechanical edit on the original MF training loop (the auxiliary v-head adds at most an extra small head; the boundary-condition version is parameterless).
- Flexible CFG via conditioning is plug-and-play on any FM/MF/diffusion model that uses CFG.
- In-context conditioning removes adaLN-zero and saves ~1/3 of the parameter count at fixed depth/width — at our CPU budget this is meaningful.
- The from-scratch 1-NFE FID (1.72) is competitive with *distilled* 1-NFE methods of similar size, and within striking distance of multi-step methods.

# Limitations (project-relevant)

- 610 M params at the SOTA point; even iMF-B/2 (89 M) is at the upper edge of "small". A meaningful audio replication would need to retest the recipe at <10 M params on a 1-D conv backbone.
- Backbone is a DiT, not a 1-D conv. Whether the same v-loss stability story holds when the network is a small 1-D U-Net with FiLM conditioning is an open question.
- All results are on ImageNet latents from a pretrained VAE — the paper's own conclusion is that this is borrowed compute that one-NFE generation makes increasingly visible.
- Training uses 240–800 epochs of ImageNet on TPUs. For us the relevant question is "how quickly does iMF train from scratch on a tiny raw-audio corpus" — paper offers no signal on small-data regimes.
- The CFG-conditioning sweep is uniform-ish over `ω ∈ [1, 8]`. For audio with weaker class signal, the right range may be different.
- No latency numbers anywhere in the paper, just Gflops. Standard image-paper omission.

# Connection to other ingested papers

- **FD-loss (2604.28190).** The FD-loss paper post-trains an iMF-style 1-NFE generator (it explicitly cites "iMF" as one of its bases) using a distributional Fréchet objective — these two methods are **stackable**: train an iMF-style 1-NFE generator from scratch, then post-train it with FD-loss for further quality. Both papers come from overlapping author groups (Geng appears on both). For the raw-audio path, the natural combined recipe is: 1-D iMF training → FD-loss post-train with VGGish/CLAP/AudioMAE multi-rep.
- **CRASH (2106.07431).** CRASH provides the architecture template (1-D U-Net, FiLM(σ), factor-300 downsample at 44.1 kHz) we'd want to put iMF training on top of. CRASH's 50-NFE DDIM trajectory could also be a teacher, but iMF doesn't *need* one — that's the whole point.

# Open questions for the repo

1. Does the v-loss + JVP machinery train stably on a small 1-D conv U-Net (say a shrunk CRASH backbone with ~5 M params) on a single 4090, on a small drum dataset (~30 k clips)?
2. What's the smallest backbone where the iMF recipe still beats a plain FM baseline at 1 NFE? Useful to know before scaling.
3. Does the in-context conditioning architecture make sense for a 1-D conv (where there are no "tokens" to concat) or do we fall back to FiLM? Cleanest test: condition tokens prepended to a Transformer-bottleneck variant of the U-Net.
4. How does CFG over class labels translate when class conditioning is weak (e.g. one-shot drum kicks with no fine sub-class signal)? Does flexible CFG still help, or does it collapse to a no-op?
5. JVP cost in autograd at 1-D length 21 000 — measurable overhead per training step relative to plain FM? (Should be ~2x forward, not a blocker.)
6. **CPU forward-pass cost at iMF-B/2 scale on 1-D inputs.** This is the unanswered question across every method in the wiki — needs a real benchmark before any of these recipes are evaluated.
