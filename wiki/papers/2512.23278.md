# Flow2GAN (ICLR 2026)

## TL;DR (project lens)

A **conditional** (mel / Encodec-token) audio generator that lands **1-, 2-, 4-step waveform synthesis with a real, published CPU benchmark** at 24 kHz. Two stages: (1) flow-matching pre-train with two audio-specific edits — endpoint x₁-prediction instead of velocity prediction, plus a spectral-energy-inverse loss weighting on STFT-domain prediction error; (2) GAN fine-tune (MPD + MRD discriminators, HingeGAN + FM + multi-scale mel L1) of the FM checkpoint to recover fine detail at low step counts. Backbone is a **multi-branch ConvNeXt operating on three time-frequency resolutions of the STFT** (no waveform-domain convs anywhere) — three parallel STFT→ConvNeXt→iSTFT branches whose outputs are summed in the time domain. **78.9 M params, 1-step xRT ≈ 4.85 on an Intel Xeon CPU at 24 kHz, batch 16, 1 s clips**. This is the first paper we have ingested with a measured CPU-real-time number for raw waveform output — and it is firmly above real-time. It is *conditional* (vocoder regime), not unconditional generative audio, but the architecture, loss design, and CPU number are direct evidence for our budget.

## Problem

Audio vocoders / waveform generators split into:
- **GAN** family (MelGAN, HiFi-GAN, BigVGAN, Vocos): one-step at inference, but adversarial training is unstable and slow to converge.
- **Diffusion / Flow-Matching** family (DiffWave, WaveGrad, PeriodWave, RFWave, WaveFM): stable training and strong quality but multi-step inference at deploy time.

The paper's framing: get diffusion-quality outputs at GAN-grade inference cost (1–4 NFE) on **CPU**, for the conditional waveform-synthesis setting (mel or codec tokens → waveform). They specifically argue that vanilla flow matching has two audio-specific failure modes (Section 4.1).

## Method

### Stage 1 — Improved Flow Matching for audio

Standard FM: linear path `x_t = (1−t)x₀ + t x₁`, fit network `f_θ(x_t, t | c) ≈ v_t = x₁ − x₀` via MSE. Issues, framed audio-specifically:

1. **Velocity estimation difficulty.** Audio is mostly empty (silent segments / frequency bands with ~0 energy). In those regions the velocity target is `x₁ − x₀ ≈ −x₀`, i.e. the network has to learn to exactly cancel noise everywhere it's quiet. They argue this is harder than learning x₁ directly.

2. **Loss-perception mismatch.** MSE weights loud and quiet regions equally; perception is much more sensitive to artifacts in quieter regions.

**Fix 1 — predict x₁, not v.** Reparameterise the network to output `ĝ_θ(x_t, t|c) = x̂₁`. Algebra: `v_t = (x₁ − x_t)/(1−t)`, so

  L_FM = E[ ‖ (g_θ(x_t,t|c) − x_t)/(1−t) − (x₁−x_t)/(1−t) ‖² ] = E[ ‖g_θ − x₁‖² / (1−t)² ]

They drop the `1/(1−t)²` factor (Eq. 4), arguing it emphasises small t which matches the few-step-sampling regime. Sampling becomes `x_{t_{i+1}} = x_{t_i} + (t_{i+1}−t_i) · (g_θ − x_t)/(1−t_i)`.

**Fix 2 — spectral-energy-inverse loss weighting.** Map the prediction error into a smoothed power-spectrogram domain `S(x) = LinFB(|STFT(x)|²)` and weight each (frame, freq-bin) element by `1/√(S(x₁)+ε)`, clamped to `[0.01, 100]` for stability:

  L_FM″ = E[ Σ_{i,j} ( S(g_θ − x_t) / √(S(x₁) + ε) )_{i,j} ]

This is more comprehensive than PriorGrad / RFWave's per-frame energy scaling (which weights along time only) — they scale along both time and frequency dimensions.

### Stage 2 — GAN fine-tuning for few-step inference

Take the FM-trained network `g_θ` and unroll it for N = 1, 2, or 4 steps to construct `G_θ^N(x₀|c)`. For N > 1, backprop through all forward passes (end-to-end). Note: **separate models are trained for each N** — they don't share weights across step counts (unlike Shortcut Models which condition on N).

Discriminators: **MPD** (multi-period, from HiFi-GAN) + **MRD** (multi-resolution, from UnivNet). Loss = HingeGAN adversarial + L1 feature matching + multi-scale mel reconstruction (Kumar 2023 RVQ recipe). Training cost is small — 11k–110k GAN fine-tuning iterations is enough; total compute is much cheaper than training a pure GAN from scratch (which takes 110–660k iters to underperform, Table 4).

### Architecture — multi-resolution ConvNeXt over STFT (no waveform-space convs)

Inspired by Vocos. Three parallel branches:
- Each branch: STFT (different window/hop per branch) → concat real/imag along channel dim → ConvNeXt stack → iSTFT → waveform.
- For mel-conditioning: branch resolutions `(N-FFT, hop) ∈ {(512,256), (256,128), (128,64)}`, embedding dims `{768, 512, 384}`, 8 ConvNeXt layers each, kernel 7, FFN expansion 3.
- For Encodec-token conditioning: `{(640,320), (320,160), (160,80)}`.
- Outputs of three branches are summed in waveform domain.
- A separate **ConvNeXt condition encoder** (embedding dim 512, 4 layers) extracts deeper features from the mel / codec input; its output is fed as condition to all three branches, *forward-passed once* and reused across FM sampling steps.
- BiasNorm normalisation + PReLU activations. ScaledAdam optimizer.

This is fundamentally a **spectral-coefficient model**, not a raw-waveform model: every conv runs on STFT coefficients. The "raw audio" emerges only at the iSTFT outputs of each branch. Important for our project because it sidesteps the long-1D-sequence problem entirely — sequence length is `T_audio / hop` per branch, an order of magnitude shorter than samples.

## Inference cost (the headline)

Table 7, **Intel Xeon Platinum 8457C CPU, batch 16, 1 s clips, 24 kHz**, xRT = generated audio seconds / wall-clock seconds (higher = faster than real-time):

| Model | Params (M) | CPU xRT | GPU xRT (H100) |
|---|---|---|---|
| BigVGAN | 112.4 | 0.214 | 69.8 |
| Vocos | 13.5 | 387.6 | 6440.8 |
| RFWave (10-step) | 18.1 | 0.37 | 158.8 |
| PeriodWave-Turbo (4-step) | 70.24 | 0.12 | 43.7 |
| WaveFM (1-step) | 19.5 | 0.64 | 226.3 |
| **Flow2GAN 1-step** | 78.9 | **4.85** | 851.7 |
| **Flow2GAN 2-step** | 78.9 | **2.46** | 449.3 |
| **Flow2GAN 4-step** | 78.9 | **1.35** | 228.5 |

Read-out:
- BigVGAN, RFWave, PeriodWave-Turbo, WaveFM are **all sub-real-time on this CPU** despite being widely cited.
- Vocos at 387× real-time is by far the fastest — but Vocos is 1-step single-resolution and gives noticeably worse quality (Table 1: PESQ 3.62 vs Flow2GAN 4-step 4.48, MOS 4.10 vs 4.58).
- Flow2GAN 1-step at **4.85× real-time** sits above real-time *and* hits SOTA-class quality (PESQ 4.19, ViSQOL 4.96, MOS 4.39 — comparable to BigVGAN's 4.33 in MOS).
- The 1- / 2- / 4-step xRT roughly halves with each doubling of N — i.e. **per-step cost ≈ 0.2 s of audio per CPU-second per step** at 24 kHz mono batch-16, scaling near-linearly in N.

This is a tight existence proof that **the STFT-branch ConvNeXt design is the right shape for CPU few-step audio**. Note however that batch=16 inflates throughput significantly versus the unbatched-streaming use case we care about for short one-shots; single-clip CPU latency would need to be re-measured.

## Quality results (selected)

**LibriTTS (mel-conditioned, 24 kHz, Table 1).**
- Flow2GAN 4-step: PESQ 4.484, ViSQOL 4.986, FSD 0.016, MOS 4.58.
- Beats Vocos, RFWave, PeriodWave-Turbo, WaveFM across most metrics; tied / very close to BigVGAN-v2 (which uses a much larger training set).
- Flow2GAN 1-step still beats Vocos and RFWave by large margins (PESQ 4.19 vs 3.62 / 4.22).

**Encodec audio-token conditioning, universal audio (Table 2).** At 6 kbps: Flow2GAN 4-step achieves FSD 0.678 vs PeriodWave-Turbo 0.712, MBD 6.177, EnCodec 1.239 — better generalisation across speech+music+SFX.

**Ablations.**
- Table 3: predicting x₁ alone (no spectral weighting) lifts 2-step PESQ from 2.35 → 2.81; adding spectral weighting → 3.47. Per-frame-only weighting (PriorGrad/RFWave-style) actively hurts in this setup.
- Table 5 (vs Shortcut Models, same backbone): Flow2GAN > Shortcut > standard FM at 1, 2, 4 steps. So the dedicated-per-N-models strategy beats step-conditioning on this benchmark.
- Table 6: multi-resolution backbone (78.9 M, 3 branches) ≈ ties a single-resolution doubled-depth baseline (79.9 M), but the multi-res variant is slightly better. Condition encoder is necessary.
- Table 9 (FM-only at N steps before GAN fine-tune): 1→2 steps gives PESQ 2.28 → 3.47, 16 steps → 4.17. GAN fine-tune at 1 step (4.30) **beats FM-only at 16 steps (4.17)**. The headline argument for the hybrid.

## How this translates to our raw-sample-space audio project

**What ports directly:**

1. **STFT-domain conv backbone with iSTFT readout is a serious CPU contender.** Even if our setting is unconditional generation rather than mel-conditioned vocoding, the per-step cost story is dominated by sequence-length-on-conv-stack. Running ConvNeXt at hop-rate (e.g. 320 samples/token at 24 kHz = 75 tokens/s) is ~hundreds of times cheaper than running 1D conv at 24 kHz, and the iSTFT recovers waveform. This is the Vocos design pattern, validated again here.
2. **Endpoint (x₁) prediction over velocity prediction.** Their motivation — empty-region velocity is unstable — applies even more strongly to **unconditional** audio where the silent regions are large at training time and the noise prior is N(0,I). Worth A/B-ing in our experiments.
3. **Spectral-energy-inverse loss weighting** on both time and frequency dimensions. This is a small, clean, drop-in modification to any FM/diffusion loss in audio and the ablation in Table 3 shows it's load-bearing (≈0.6 PESQ).
4. **FM pretrain → GAN finetune as a few-step recipe.** Two-stage is much cheaper than pure GAN (Table 4: GAN fine-tune at 11k iters > pure GAN at 660k iters). Compatible with anything we train via score / FM.
5. **CPU benchmark methodology.** Intel Xeon Platinum 8457C, batch 16, 1 s clips, xRT = seconds-of-audio / wall-clock. This should be **our reporting convention** for CPU-inference numbers, with the addition of batch=1 for fair short-one-shot latency.

**What doesn't port:**
- The whole setup is **conditional** (mel-spectrogram or Encodec tokens drive the generation). It is a vocoder, not an unconditional generator. The flow-matching field starts from `x₀ ~ N(0,I)` but the network is conditioned on `c`, which carries essentially all the semantic information. Removing `c` is a separate (and much harder) problem.
- Three separate models for N=1, 2, 4 — wasteful if compute matters. Shortcut Models / iMF / MeanFlow style step-conditioning is cheaper at train time, even if slightly weaker per Table 5.

**Open questions for replication:**
- Does the STFT-ConvNeXt-iSTFT backbone work as well in the **unconditional** regime? Without a conditioning signal, will three branches collapse to redundant predictions or maintain useful diversity?
- Single-clip (batch=1) CPU latency rather than batch=16 throughput. The CRASH/iMF comparison is meaningful only at batch=1 for the "play a sound now" UX.
- Can we stack iMF (from-scratch 1-NFE flow-matching with the v-loss reparameterisation) with this STFT-branch backbone, and skip GAN fine-tuning entirely?
