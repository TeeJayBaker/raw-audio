# MIND — Monge Inception Distance

## TL;DR (project-relevant)

A drop-in FID replacement that swaps the closed-form 2-Wasserstein-between-Gaussians for an averaged sliced-1D-Wasserstein on the same embeddings. It is a **proper distance**, **~10× more sample-efficient** (5k samples ≈ FID@50k), **~100× faster to compute**, **~10× lower peak memory**, and **much harder to game by moment-matching**. For raw-audio on a 4090 + CPU-inference workflow, this matters concretely: we can run evaluation during/after training with batches of 1–5k generations instead of 50k, which is the difference between an evaluator we actually run every epoch vs. one we never run. The metric is **embedding-agnostic** — the paper uses Inception-v3 for back-compat but explicitly notes any feature extractor works, including audio embeddings (CLAP, audio-DINO, etc.). For us that means: pick an audio embedding once, then MIND drops in with no further engineering.

## What FID is doing wrong

FID fits a Gaussian to the d=2048 Inception features of generated and real, then takes the closed-form 2-Wasserstein. Two problems the paper hammers on:

1. **Statistical cost.** The empirical covariance $\hat\Sigma_n$ is rank-deficient when $n \le d$, so $n$ has to be of order $d$ before the second term in $W_2^2(\mathcal N, \mathcal N)$ stops being noise-dominated. For Inception-v3, that's why everyone uses 10–50k samples. At audio rates, generating 50k samples even of 1-second clips on a 4090 is a non-trivial chunk of wall time; on CPU it's painful.
2. **Not a proper distance.** $\text{FID}=0$ only requires matching first two moments. The paper exhibits an explicit construction (Prop 4.1) of a degenerate distribution $\hat q$ supported on $2r$ points $\mu \pm \alpha u_i$ (eigendirections of $\Sigma$, with $\alpha=\sqrt{\text{tr}\Sigma}$) that has the same mean and covariance as the target and hence FID = 0 — and they show you can push real image embeddings toward this set without changing the images visually (Fig 11). FID can be **hacked**.

## The method, concretely

Replace the Gaussian-2-Wasserstein with the **squared sliced Wasserstein** of the same embeddings:

$$\text{MIND}(p_\theta, p_\text{data}) = \alpha\, \mathbb E_{u\sim\mathcal U(S^{d-1})}\bigl[W_2^2(u^\top p_\theta,\, u^\top p_\text{data})\bigr]$$

With $M$ random unit projections and $n$ samples per side, the estimator is

$$\widehat{\text{MIND}} = \frac{\alpha}{nM}\sum_{i=1}^M \sum_{j=1}^n \bigl|\text{sort}(u_i^\top X)_j - \text{sort}(u_i^\top Y)_j\bigr|^2$$

The 1D Wasserstein is exact via sorting — that's the whole computational trick. Scaling $\alpha = 3d \approx 6000$ for $d=2048$ is chosen to put MIND values in roughly the same range as FID (purely for adoption friction).

Sliced Wasserstein **is a proper distance** (Bonnotte 2013), so $\text{MIND}=0 \Leftrightarrow p=q$. That kills the moment-matching attack. Sample complexity of sliced-W does not blow up with $d$ (Nadjahi 2020) — that's why 5k is enough.

Hyperparameters the paper settled on: $M \in [100, 1000]$ projections is plenty (Fig 10a — variance plateaus past $M\approx 1000$, $M=100$ already near-optimal); $n \approx 5000$ samples.

## Reference implementation (paper Fig 4)

The whole thing is one screenful of PyTorch:

```python
def monge_inception_distance_torch(x, y, rng_seed, n_projections=100):
    num_samples, d = x.shape
    ALPHA = 3 * d
    g = torch.Generator(device=x.device).manual_seed(rng_seed)
    u = torch.randn((n_projections, d), generator=g, device=x.device)
    u = u / torch.linalg.norm(u, dim=-1, keepdim=True)
    x_p = x @ u.T
    y_p = y @ u.T
    dists = (torch.topk(x_p, num_samples, dim=-1).values
             - torch.topk(y_p, num_samples, dim=-1).values) ** 2
    return ALPHA * torch.mean(dists)
```

Sort = `topk(n)`, applied along the projection axis. $O(MnD + Mn\log n)$ total. Trivially parallel. No matrix inversions, no Cholesky, no $d\times d$ anything.

## Headline numbers

| Test | FID | MIND |
|---|---|---|
| Sample size for reliable training-checkpoint discrimination on ImageNet-64 | ~50k (~10k just barely) | 5k (works down to 2k) |
| Compute time (TPUv4, $n=5$k) | baseline | **~100× faster** |
| Peak memory (TPUv4, $n=5$k) | baseline | **~10× lower** |
| Moment-matching attack — ratio metric reduces to with no visible image change | 11.2% (FID), 12.2% (MMD), 2.6% ($\mu$FID), 4.2% ($\sigma$FID) | **31.1%** (much more resilient) |
| Number of projections $M$ needed | n/a | 100–1000 sufficient |

Correlation between $\text{MIND}_{5k}$ and $\text{FID}_{50k}$ across training of an ImageNet-64 diffusion model is ≥0.94 in the linked checkpoints table; trajectories are linearly related on log-log up to the affine scaling $\alpha \propto d$ (Fig 5).

The Sinkhorn divergence (entropic-regularized OT) is also benchmarked — comparable robustness, much heavier to compute, plus a hyperparameter $\varepsilon$ to tune.

## Why this matters for raw-audio CPU work

The paper is image-only experimentally, but the math is fully feature-agnostic — they're explicit about CLIP/DINO/audio applying directly. The translation to our setting is **clean and immediate**:

1. **Pick an audio embedding.** Candidates: CLAP, audio-DINO-style SSL models, PANNs/VGGish features, or whatever the audio-FAD-style literature settles on. The choice of $\psi_w$ is what makes the metric "perceptual"; MIND inherits whatever the embedding sees.
2. **Drop MIND on top.** Code is unchanged from the snippet above — embeddings are just vectors. $d=512$ (CLAP) or $d=2048$ keeps the $\alpha = 3d$ heuristic working.
3. **Win on sample count.** For us this is the big deal. A 1-step CPU-target model generating 1 s clips at 44.1 kHz: 5k samples vs. 50k samples is hours vs. day on a 4090, and it lets eval happen during training, not just at the end.
4. **Win on hacking-robustness.** As we go to one-step / few-step / distillation-heavy methods, the failure modes (mode collapse, moment-matching at the embedding level) are exactly the ones FID is blind to. A proper distance pushes back on that.

The standard FAD (Fréchet Audio Distance) inherits FID's exact flaws — same Gaussian closed form, same moments-only sensitivity, same $n \sim d$ requirement (with VGGish $d=128$, less catastrophic than Inception's $d=2048$, but still). **Monge Audio Distance** as a direct analog of MIND on whatever audio embedding we settle on is the natural project-side metric. The 1D sort makes it cheap enough to run on CPU as part of the same eval pipeline as the inference benchmark.

Caveats specific to audio:

- **Perceptual gap of the embedding.** Inception was trained on natural images; whatever audio backbone we use was probably trained on speech-or-everything (PANNs/VGGish) or audio-text pairs (CLAP), not on short musical samples. The metric is only as discriminative as the embedding, and for "musical loop / hit" content the embedding choice may matter more than the metric on top. MIND doesn't fix this — but at least it isn't *additionally* lossy by Gaussianizing.
- **Phase / micro-structure.** Embeddings tend to be mel-band-ish and discard phase. Two waveforms that differ in inaudible phase or in audible-but-not-mel-captured micro-structure will look identical. Again, this is an embedding limitation; MIND inherits it. We may still need a sample-space metric (e.g. multi-scale STFT, sample-domain spectral distance) alongside.
- **Sample length.** Embedding networks fix an input length. MIND just compares whatever $d$-vectors come out — we choose how to chunk/pool.

## Limitations of the paper itself

- Single-modality experiments (ImageNet-64 + a CelebA mixture). The "embedding-agnostic" claim is plausible but not demonstrated cross-modally in this paper.
- Choice of $M$, $\alpha$, and the squared (vs. unsquared) Wasserstein are pragmatic, not principled — they say so.
- They don't address whether MIND inherits the *embedding's* biases differently than FID does. The proof is moment-attack resilience at the embedding level, not perceptual fidelity.
- No discussion of stratified projections or variance-reduction beyond raising $M$. Quasi-MC for $u_i$ might help; left open.
