# Meta-evaluation: every fidelity/diversity metric fails at least one sanity check

## TL;DR (project lens)

A **meta-evaluation** position paper (ICML 2025) that does not propose a new generator, embedding, or loss. It proposes (a) six **desiderata** for any single-number fidelity-or-diversity metric, and (b) a library of **sanity-check stress tests** with precise, programmatically checked pass/fail criteria. It then runs every popular pair (I-Prec/I-Rec, Density/Coverage, α-precision/β-recall = IAP/IBR, Precision-Cover/Recall-Cover, symPrec/symRec, P-Prec/P-Rec) through the suite and finds **every metric fails a large fraction of the checks** — including basic ones like "metric value should converge as you grow the real dataset" or "metric should reflect a difference confined to one dimension when many other dimensions are identical". Headline tables (3 and 4 in the paper) are red-heavy.

For us, this paper is the framing for the audio-side metric stack. Three operational consequences:

1. **No single audio metric is safe.** Adopt a **bundle** — pick metrics whose failure modes are *independent* on the eight checklist axes the paper distils. Whenever the bundle agrees on a verdict we trust it; when it disagrees we have a diagnostic.
2. **Re-run the paper's sanity-check constructions on audio metrics** (FAD-VGGish, FAD-CLAP, FAD-MERT, MIND, FDr^k-audio, Vendi-CLAP, Coverage-MERT, etc.). The constructions are domain-agnostic (Gaussians, Gaussian mixtures, hypercubes, hypersphere surfaces, sphere-vs-torus, mode-drop sequences, Pareto-outlier injection, discrete-vs-continuous, scaled-one-dim) — apply them in *embedding space* of each audio embedding network. Code is open-sourced at `github.com/vanderschaarlab/position-fidelity-diversity-metrics-flawed`.
3. **No metric is suitable for absolute evaluations.** This is the paper's strongest claim, and it falls out of D4 (bounds) failing for nearly all metrics in practice. Any "this 1-NFE audio model is good" claim has to be *relative to a baseline* on multiple metrics; absolute thresholds are not meaningful.

## What this paper is (and is not)

It is **not** a survey of metrics. It is **not** a proposal for a new metric. It is a position paper with three contributions:

1. A six-point **desiderata** list: D1 (purpose), D2 (hyperparameters), D3 (data), D4 (bounds), D5 (invariance), D6 (computation).
2. A **sanity-check suite** — 17 named constructions, mostly drawn from prior work (Naeem 2020, Alaa 2022, Cheema & Urner 2023, Khayatkhoei & Abdalmageed 2023, Park & Kim 2023, Kynkäänniemi 2019), plus four new tabular-focused ones (Pareto outliers, discrete-vs-continuous, scaling one dim, one-disjoint-dim-many-identical).
3. **Pass/fail tables** for six fidelity metrics × 17 checks (Table 3) and six diversity metrics × 17 checks (Table 4) with precisely defined programmatic criteria.

The 17 checks group into eight families (their Section 5.2 checklist axes), reproduced below in the form most useful for us.

## The six desiderata

Lifted verbatim from §2:

| Code | Name | What it requires |
|---|---|---|
| **D1a** | purpose: target | metric measures a quantity of *direct* practical interest |
| **D1b** | purpose: interpretable | metric gives interpretable info on `real vs generated` difference |
| **D1c** | purpose: proxy | metric correlates with an impractical target (e.g. human eval) |
| **D2** | hyperparameters | minimal HPs; effect of HPs as clear as possible. HPs with a standard value / method-of-choosing don't count |
| **D3** | data | requires less data than the problem of interest has; converges past some sample size (paper picks 1000) |
| **D4** | bounds | clear lower **and** upper bounds. Identical distributions should hit one bound; "as bad as it gets" should hit the other |
| **D5** | invariance | invariant to domain-irrelevant transformations of real **and** synthetic data (e.g. scaling, permuting categories, permuting variables) |
| **D6** | computation | tractable in reasonable time |

D1 is the only one that admits multiple sub-flavours (a / b / c) — a metric is allowed to satisfy any one. The rest are unconditional. Note the asymmetric framing of D3: the paper specifically *does not* want full invariance to dataset size — it wants the metric to converge past some practical threshold (1000 samples is their bar; 55% of OpenML datasets have ≥ 1000 points).

## The sanity-check suite

The 17 checks in their groupings (paper Table 2, with my one-line gloss of each):

**Gaussian checks** — pairs of `d`-dim Gaussians that differ in exactly one way.
- *Gaussian Mean Difference* — shifted mean. Should produce non-trivial fidelity/diversity gap.
- *Gaussian Mean Diff + Outlier* — same as above plus one outlier added to one side; tests outlier robustness.
- *Gaussian Std Difference* — same mean, different std. Diversity metric should distinguish "high" (oversharp/peaked) from "low" (over-spread) appropriately.
- *One Disjoint Dim + Many Identical Dim* — one dim has shifted mean, many other dims are statistically identical. Tests whether metric can find a difference confined to a low-dim subspace.
- *Scaling One Dimension* — one dim is rescaled but distribution still matches; tests D5 (invariance to scale).

**Gaussian Mixture / mode-drop checks**
- *Mode Collapse* — synthetic has one wide component covering both modes of a 2-mode real. Tests whether collapse is detected.
- *Mode Dropping + Invention* — drop modes from synthetic and add fresh ones not in real. Tests D1b (interpretability) and D4 (bounds).
- *Sequential Mode Dropping* — drop modes one at a time from a 10-mode mixture.
- *Simultaneous Mode Dropping* — reduce weight of 9 of 10 modes simultaneously.

**Geometry checks**
- *Uniform Hypercube, Varying Sample Size* — symmetrically scale `|Φ_r| = |Φ_g|`. Tests convergence (D3) and absolute-bound behaviour.
- *Uniform Hypercube, Varying Synthetic Size* — hold `|Φ_r|` fixed, vary `|Φ_g|`. Tests D2 (`|Φ_g|` is technically a metric hyperparameter).
- *Hypersphere Surface* — uniform on a `(d-1)`-sphere surface, both sides differ in radius only. Khayatkhoei & Abdalmageed 2023 found that most metrics misbehave asymmetrically (caring about r_g < r_r but not r_g > r_r).
- *Sphere vs Torus* — one distribution is uniform on a sphere; the other is uniform on a torus that surrounds the sphere but is disjoint. Tests whether the metric works on non-trivial geometry.

**Tabular-data-focused (also useful on continuous embeddings of audio)**
- *Discrete Num vs Continuous Num* — Gaussian rounded to integers vs the original Gaussian; varies the rounding granularity.
- *Gaussian Mean Diff + Pareto* — adds a heavy-tailed (Pareto) random variable identically distributed in both real and synthetic. Tests whether heavy-tailed noise drowns the signal.

## How metrics fared (project-relevant rows)

Paper's Tables 3 and 4 are dense. The headline patterns:

- **Every metric fails the basic D3 check** (Hypercube, Varying Sample Size — i.e. metric should converge as both real and synthetic grow). Every fidelity and every diversity metric except a few corner cases shows it.
- **Every metric fails the absolute-bounds check (D4) on the One-Disjoint-Dim + Many-Identical-Dim setting**, except IAP and IBR which partially pass. The paper attributes this to the **neural-network embedding** used by α-precision/β-recall: it learns a representation where the single odd dimension stays visible. The Euclidean-distance-based metrics drown the signal in the many identical dimensions. **This matters for audio**: VGGish/CLAP/MERT embeddings are neural; we should not assume "embedding swamps low-rank differences" without testing.
- **All metrics fail Discrete-vs-Continuous Numerical** at fine discretisations. Mostly a tabular concern, but relevant if we ever use an integer-quantised embedding (e.g. EnCodec-token frequency).
- **Density and Coverage** are the most-broken pair: Density's value can blow up to ~200 in high dimensions in the hypercube settings even for identical distributions (Figure 9, 13). It violates D4 spectacularly.
- **I-Prec / I-Rec** are the most-outlier-fragile pair (a single outlier can dominate Improved Precision because the kNN radius includes it). The paper recommends avoiding them when outliers are present.
- **C-Prec / C-Rec** (Precision-Cover / Recall-Cover) and **symPrec/symRec** pass the Hypersphere-Surface check that motivated them — but fail most of the basic D1b checks. They're specialist tools.
- **P-Prec / P-Rec** (Probabilistic Precision/Recall, Park & Kim 2023) pass a comparable share of checks to IAP/IBR but lose to them on the geometry checks (hypersphere).

The strongest qualitative ranking the paper supports — fewest red cells per row — is roughly:

- For **fidelity**: IAP and P-Prec lead; I-Prec and symPrec are unreliable in the presence of outliers.
- For **diversity**: IBR and P-Rec lead; Coverage has bad bounds; I-Rec is outlier-fragile.

But the explicit position is *no single metric is reliable*, so the practical takeaway is the checklist (next section), not a winner.

## The Practical Advice Checklist (§5.2, lightly paraphrased)

The paper's practitioner checklist, framed as 8 questions:

1. **Absolute or relative evaluation?** If absolute ("is the model good?") — *all current metrics are bad*; no recommendation.
2. **Does eval-dataset size depend on data-prep choices** (missing-data handling, subset choice)? If yes — *investigate the size sensitivity of any metric used*, since all metrics are dataset-size-sensitive past D3 thresholds.
3. **Is detecting mode collapse / dropping / invention important?** Prefer **I-Prec, P-Prec, P-Rec**.
4. **Is outlier robustness important?** Avoid **I-Prec, symPrec, I-Rec**.
5. **Is detecting a difference in one dimension among many important?** *All current metrics are bad.*
6. **Is distinguishing continuous from discrete numerical important?** *All current metrics are bad.*
7. **Distinguishing distributions on close surfaces (hypersphere surface check)?** Prefer **symPrec, IAP, symRec**.
8. **Distinguishing distributions on complex shapes (sphere-vs-torus)?** Avoid **C-Prec** among fidelity; prefer **IBR** among diversity.

When more than one metric is recommended after answering the questions, **compute all of them and see whether they agree**. Disagreement is itself diagnostic.

The paper's "Takeaways for Practitioners" box compresses to:

- No metric is suitable for absolute evaluations.
- Dataset-size effects need to be investigated in every evaluation.
- Real-data evaluations require care — real datasets have *multiple* failure modes simultaneously, and a metric failing one can pollute its read on the others.

## Reasons all metrics fail the hardest checks (§5.3)

The paper conjectures the root cause for the worst failures (One-Disjoint-Dim, Hypersphere, Sphere-vs-Torus, Discrete-vs-Continuous):

- **All evaluated metrics use Euclidean distance** in some space (either raw data or after a learned embedding).
- Euclidean distance **drowns low-rank or geometric signals** in high dimensions.
- The neural-network embedding under IAP/IBR helps a little (it can stretch the odd dimension) but is itself a black box; the other metrics get no such help.
- **Future direction: go beyond Euclidean geometry.** They cite topological-feature approaches (Barannikov 2021, Southern 2023, Kim 2023 = TopP&R) as candidates, but note Kim 2023's TopP&R is too slow for them to include (>30 min on 1000 samples of a 2D Gaussian, vs <0.1 s for I-Prec/I-Rec/Density/Coverage).

This is the most actionable theoretical pointer for us: **non-Euclidean embedding-space metrics** (topological, kernel-based with non-trivial kernels, Wasserstein-on-something-other-than-L2) are the research direction the paper endorses. The MIND / FD-as-loss line (which we already track) lives in that space.

## Translates to raw audio

**The sanity-check methodology is domain-agnostic.** Every construction in §4.1 lives in `R^d` and runs in any embedding space. The audio adaptation is simply:

1. Pick the audio embedding (VGGish, CLAP, MERT, DINO-audio, etc.) — call it `φ`.
2. Generate the synthetic real/fake datasets for each sanity check **directly in the embedding space `R^d` of `φ`**, *not* in waveform space. Most checks (Gaussians, mixtures, hypercubes, Pareto) don't have any audio analogue and there is no need to invent one — the entire point is to test the metric's behaviour on known constructions.
3. Score the audio fidelity/diversity metric (FAD, MIND, Kernel-Audio-Distance, Vendi, Coverage-MERT, FDr^k-audio, etc.) on those embedding-space constructions and tick the pass/fail criteria.

This is much lower-friction than waveform-space synthesis. The result is a per-embedding sanity-check table: "FAD-VGGish passes X of 17 checks, FAD-CLAP passes Y, MIND-MERT passes Z". That table is the **per-bundle calibration sheet** we need before quoting numbers in any APT-1NFE results writeup.

**Bundle composition (working proposal for this repo).** The paper's checklist suggests the bundle should detect each adversarial-training failure mode at least once:

| Failure mode of 1-NFE audio APT | Which metric in bundle catches it | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Oversharpening** (perceptual sharpness inflates FAD-VGGish without other improvements) | FAD-CLAP + Vendi-CLAP **disagree** with FAD-VGGish (CLAP is text-grounded, less reactive to perceptual sharpness alone) | Cross-embedding disagreement |
| **Mode dropping** | **Coverage-MERT** drops (Coverage is the cheapest mode-drop detector when it works) plus **I-Prec** (mode-aware) | Paper Q3 in checklist |
| **Tail loss** (rare timbres / atypical samples vanish) | **Vendi-CLAP** drops; **β-recall (IBR)** drops | Paper recommends IBR for tail/coverage on complex shapes (Q8) |
| **Memorisation** (low FAD because we replayed train set) | **FAD on test set ≫ FAD on train set**, plus train-test similarity (kNN distance to train); metric pair must include a held-out FAD | Outside the paper but consistent with its D4-bounds argument |

This bundle deliberately spans the eight axes in the paper's checklist:

- **Outlier robustness** — avoid I-Prec / symPrec / I-Rec as primary. Use IAP / IBR / P-Prec / P-Rec in their place. (FAD itself is mean-and-cov-based; outliers move it but predictably.)
- **Mode-collapse detection** — I-Prec or P-Prec, plus Coverage.
- **Geometry on non-trivial supports** — symPrec / symRec / IAP for "close surfaces" if we ever evaluate on a structured subset (e.g. "drum hits with constrained pitch range" is roughly a low-dim manifold).
- **Absolute-bounds awareness** — never quote a single FAD without a reference, and never claim "FAD = X means quality is good".
- **Sample-size sensitivity** — record `|Φ_r|` and `|Φ_g|` for every reported number; rerun at ×2 and ×0.5 to verify stability.

**What this paper does NOT remove from the bundle.** It does not invalidate using FAD or MIND or any other audio metric in *relative* comparisons between two of our own models, holding `(embedding, dataset, |Φ_r|, |Φ_g|)` fixed. The paper's failure modes mostly bite the *absolute* and *cross-study* interpretation of a number, not the within-study A-vs-B comparison.

## Strengths

- **The pass/fail criteria are programmatic.** Code at `github.com/vanderschaarlab/position-fidelity-diversity-metrics-flawed` — drop-in for our own audio metrics with a swap of the data-generation function (replace 2D Gaussian sampler with a CLAP-embedding-distribution sampler).
- **Six-axis desiderata + 17 checks** is the most thorough single-paper synthesis of the metric-failure literature so far. Subsumes Naeem 2020, Alaa 2022, Cheema & Urner 2023, Park & Kim 2023, Khayatkhoei & Abdalmageed 2023 into one comparable matrix.
- **The bundle-of-metrics recommendation has theoretical teeth.** It's not "report many metrics for completeness" — it's "compose the bundle so the failure modes are independent on these specific axes".
- **Identifies the Euclidean-geometry root cause** of the hardest failures and points at topology / non-Euclidean approaches (TopP&R, Manifold Topology Divergence) as the research direction. Aligns with our existing tracking of MIND / FD-as-loss as siblings outside the Euclidean-FID lineage.

## Limitations / open questions

- **Excludes curve-valued metrics** (Sajjadi 2018 P/R curves, Djolonga 2020, Simon 2019, Siry 2023, Sykes 2024) and **topological-feature metrics** (Kim 2023 = TopP&R, too slow). The paper explicitly defers these. For us, **MIND** (entropy of nearest-neighbour distances, no Euclidean assumption beyond the embedding) and **kernel-based audio distances** are in scope and should be added to the same matrix.
- **No audio metrics evaluated.** FAD, FDr^k, MIND, Vendi-CLAP are not in their tables. We need to either run the published code on those metrics in their embedding spaces or write a thin audio-side wrapper. Both are tractable.
- **Position-paper format.** No new metric. So this is a *framing* contribution, not a *tool* contribution. The tool — the open-source sanity-check suite — is the actual reusable artefact.
- **D6 (computation) gets short shrift.** TopP&R and similar non-Euclidean candidates are noted as candidates and immediately discarded for runtime. For our CPU-realtime project, the same constraint applies to *evaluation*: a metric that needs 30 min on 1000 samples is not viable at the rate we'd like to evaluate model variants. Practical bundle composition has to balance failure-mode coverage against runtime.

## What to actually do in this repo

1. **Vendor the sanity-check suite.** Pull the GitHub repo into `tools/metric-sanity-checks/` (or similar) and run it as-is on a baseline Gaussian → confirm reproduction of the paper's tables.
2. **Wrap audio embeddings.** Replace the data-generation function in each check with one that draws samples in the embedding space of (VGGish / CLAP / MERT / DINO-audio). The checks themselves don't change.
3. **Run the suite on every audio metric we adopt** for the APT-1NFE line and any future raw-sample-space evaluations. Produce a per-embedding pass/fail matrix in `reports/audio-metric-sanity-checks.html`.
4. **Adopt the bundle composition above** for any APT-1NFE results writeup. No FAD-only headline numbers.
5. **Whenever the bundle disagrees** in a results comparison, treat the disagreement itself as the primary finding and dig into which failure-mode axis is firing. That diagnostic is more valuable than a single number agreeing.
