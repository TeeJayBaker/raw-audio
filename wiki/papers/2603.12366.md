# TL;DR — why this matters for raw-audio CPU gen

A theory-leaning follow-up to **Drifting Models ([2602.04770](2602.04770.md))** that (i) re-derives the cross-minus-self drift field `V_{q,p} = V_p^+ − V_q^-` as a *one-iteration approximation* of the Wasserstein gradient flow of the **Sinkhorn divergence** `S_τ(p, q)`, and (ii) replaces Drifting's one-sided softmax kernel normalisation with a **full two-sided Sinkhorn scaling** of the coupling matrices. The replacement closes Drifting's identifiability gap — Sinkhorn divergence is provably definite (`S_τ(p, q) = 0 ⇔ p = q`), so `V ≡ 0 ⇒ q = p` rigorously, while in plain Drifting this implication only held under a hand-wavy linear-independence assumption. **Inference is identical to plain Drifting**: one forward pass of `f_θ`. Training is slightly more expensive — each step runs ~30 Sinkhorn iterations on `[N × N_pos]` and `[N × N_neg]` kernel matrices — but in return the method (a) becomes much less sensitive to kernel temperature τ, (b) preserves mode coverage at low τ where Drifting collapses, and (c) removes Drifting's ad-hoc self-distance masking heuristic. For us: the SD machinery is **drop-in for any drifting-based 1-NFE audio recipe** without touching inference latency, and the low-τ stability gain is exactly what you want when the feature-space kernel is sharply peaked (which it will be in any 1D-waveform φ-space).

# What problem does it solve

Plain Drifting ([2602.04770](2602.04770.md)) has three loose ends, all stemming from its one-sided softmax normalisation:

1. **Identifiability gap.** `V = 0 ⇔ q = p` was only argued in one direction (`q = p ⇒ V = 0`); the converse needed a non-degeneracy assumption that can fail in practice.
2. **Temperature fragility.** At small τ the Gibbs kernel `exp(−‖x − y‖² / τ)` becomes sharply peaked. The repulsion term `V_q^-` is dominated by self-similarities `k(x_i, x_i) = 1`; without surgery it would collapse to a self-mean-shift vector of zero, killing the repulsion. Drifting masks self-distances with a `+1e6` surrogate — a heuristic that "completely changes the drift dynamics" (their words, here).
3. **Two-sided normalisation by hand.** Drifting in practice uses a geometric mean of row- and column-softmax (without justification). This paper shows that move is *secretly* a one-step approximation of full Sinkhorn scaling — they were already drifting toward the right answer.

Sinkhorn-Drifting fixes all three by replacing the softmax normalisations with proper Sinkhorn-scaled couplings.

# Method, the part that distinguishes it from neighbours

## The bridge: Drifting as Sinkhorn-gradient-flow approximation

Both Drifting's `V_{q,p}` and the Wasserstein gradient flow of `S_τ(p, q)` share the **same algebraic skeleton**: a cross-minus-self barycentric projection

```
V(x) = P_cross · Y − P_self · X
```

where `Y` are target (positive) samples, `X` are model (negative) samples, and `P_*` are row-stochastic coupling matrices. The two methods differ *only in how P is built*:

| | Drifting | Sinkhorn-Drifting |
|---|---|---|
| `P_XY[i, j]` | `k(x_i, y_j) / Σ_ℓ k(x_i, y_ℓ)` (one-sided row-softmax over y) | `RowNormalize(Sinkhorn(K_pos, r, c_pos, T))` (doubly-stochastic, T~30 iters) |
| `P_XX[i, j]` | same one-sided softmax | full Sinkhorn-scaled, both marginals enforced |
| Identifiability | `V = 0` does not provably imply `q = p` (Remark 3.12) | `S_τ(p, q) = 0 ⇔ p = q` from Feydy et al. 2019, so `V_{q,p}^∞ = 0 ⇒ q = p` (Prop 3.5–3.7) |

The relevant fact from Feydy et al. 2019 is that the **debiased Sinkhorn divergence**

```
S_τ(α, β) := OT_τ(α, β) − ½ OT_τ(α, α) − ½ OT_τ(β, β)        (10)
```

is positive-definite, strictly convex in q, and vanishes iff α = β — exactly the property Drifting was missing.

## Algorithm (their Algorithm 1)

```
def sinkhorn_drift(X, Y_pos, Y_neg, τ, T):
    D_pos = cdist²(X, Y_pos)      # [N, N_pos]
    D_neg = cdist²(X, Y_neg)      # [N, N_neg]
    L_pos = exp(-D_pos / τ)
    L_neg = exp(-D_neg / τ)
    r = 1_N / N                    # uniform marginal on X
    c_pos = 1_{N_+} / N_pos
    c_neg = 1_{N_-} / N_neg
    Π_pos = Sinkhorn(L_pos, r, c_pos, T)   # T iters, e.g. T=30
    Π_neg = Sinkhorn(L_neg, r, c_neg, T)
    P_pos = RowNormalize(Π_pos)
    P_neg = RowNormalize(Π_neg)
    V = P_pos @ Y_pos − P_neg @ Y_neg      # cross-minus-self drift
    return V
```

`T = 1` recovers Drifting's one-sided kernel. The "self-distance masking" trick from Drifting (`dist_neg += eye(N) * 1e6` to suppress the repulsion-killing diagonal) **is not needed** with Sinkhorn — the marginal constraint already prevents weight collapse onto the diagonal. `Y_neg` can be the same batch `X` (matching Drifting's practice) without masking.

## Training loss

Identical shape to Drifting:

```
L_Sinkhorn = E_ε [ ‖ f_θ(ε) − stopgrad( f_θ(ε) + V_{q_θ, p_data}^∞(f_θ(ε)) ) ‖² ]    (18)
```

The only change vs. Drifting (Eq. 7) is *how V is computed*. Stop-gradient on the target, no backprop through V — same as Drifting. Inference is unchanged: `x = f_θ(ε)`.

## Theoretical guarantees

- **Prop 3.5 / 3.7:** if `S_τ(p̂, q̂) = 0`, the level-`l` Sinkhorn drift vanishes for every `l`. In the smooth-density limit (Prop 3.7), `V_{q,p}^∞ ≡ 0 ⇒ q = p` directly.
- **Prop 3.8:** finite-sample estimator concentrates as `n^{-1/2}` with a τ-dependent constant `τ^{1−d/2} log(n)`. High d × low τ is the regime where you need bigger batches.
- For `T = 1`, **Sinkhorn-drift exactly reduces to plain Drifting** (Prop 3.3), so the method strictly generalises 2602.04770.

# Inference cost

**Identical to Drifting: 1 NFE.** The Sinkhorn scaling lives **only at training time**. Inference is still `x = f_θ(ε)`, a single forward pass of whatever backbone you put in. No solver, no second model. This is the property that matters for our CPU target.

# Training cost

Per training step adds, on top of Drifting:

- One Sinkhorn algorithm run on `[N × N_pos]` and one on `[N × N_neg]`, each ~30 iterations of alternating row/column scaling.
- Cost: `O(T · N · (N_pos + N_neg))` per step, dominated by elementwise ops on the kernel matrices — small compared to the generator forward+backward.
- Paper reports "slightly slower training" but no concrete factor; their experiments are smaller scale than Drifting's (no ImageNet-1k).

# Headline results

All experiments are **small-scale** — the paper is theory-first and only validates on toys + MNIST + FFHQ-in-ALAE-latent-space. There is no ImageNet 256² head-to-head with full Drifting. The reported gains are about **stability and mode coverage at low temperature**, not absolute SOTA.

| Setting | Metric | Baseline (Drifting) | Sinkhorn-Drifting | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2D 8-Gaussians, τ = 0.1 | W²₂ | ~7–8 (one-sided collapses to 1 mode) | covers all 8 modes | Two-sided softmax: also unstable. Sinkhorn: stable. |
| 2D Checkerboard, τ ∈ {0.01, 0.05, 0.1} | W²₂ | similar to Sinkhorn | similar | Multimodal-isolated targets benefit most. |
| MNIST latent (6D), τ = 0.005 | EMD | 73.21 (collapsed, 9.97 % accuracy = chance) | **8.57**, **100 %** | Drifting only recovers at τ = 0.1; Sinkhorn works across τ ∈ [0.005, 0.1]. |
| FFHQ-ALAE latent (512D), τ = 0.1 | FID | 187.7 | **37.1** | ~5.1× FID gain; ~3.1× EMD gain. |
| FFHQ-ALAE, τ = 1.0 | FID | 146.3 | **33.7** | ~4.3× FID gain. |
| FFHQ-ALAE, τ = 10.0 | FID | 45.3 | **33.9** | Gap narrows at large τ — Drifting is fine there, Sinkhorn-Drifting is still better. |

**Pattern: the lower the temperature, the bigger the win.** This is exactly the regime where Drifting's one-sided softmax concentrates mass on the nearest neighbour and the repulsion collapses.

# How this translates to raw audio

The Sinkhorn substitution is **method-agnostic with respect to the data domain**. It works on whatever space the kernel lives in. So everything in the audio-translation section of [Drifting (2602.04770)](2602.04770.md) carries over verbatim, plus three new considerations:

## 1. Low-τ regime is the audio regime

For raw audio in φ-space, the choice of τ controls "how close two clips have to sound to attract each other". Kernels that are too broad blur perceptually distinct clips; kernels that are too sharp collapse onto nearest-neighbour pulls and lose repulsion. The whole point of audio similarity is that **the perceptual distance function is steep** — small phase / pitch shifts that are inaudible should be close, larger shifts should be far. So we *want* small τ relative to typical inter-clip distances. Drifting's collapse-at-small-τ failure mode is precisely what we'd hit. **Sinkhorn-Drifting buys us the small-τ regime back without surgery.**

## 2. Self-distance masking goes away

Drifting's `dist_neg += eye(N) * 1e6` hack is exactly the kind of brittle trick we'd want to avoid in an audio replication where N (batch size) is constrained by VAE/encoder memory. Sinkhorn handles the diagonal via marginal constraints, so we can keep negatives = positives = batch without manual exclusion.

## 3. Multi-temperature averaging may become unnecessary

Drifting averages drifts across `τ ∈ {0.02, 0.05, 0.2}` for robustness. Sinkhorn-Drifting's headline claim is that **a single τ now works stably** — the temperature sweep on MNIST (Table 1) shows nearly flat EMD across two decades of τ. If that holds in audio φ-space, we save 3× the kernel-matrix compute per training step.

## 4. Training-time cost

The Sinkhorn loop adds `T · N · (N_pos + N_neg)` elementwise ops per training step. For N = N_pos = N_neg = 128 and T = 30, that's ~1M ops per drift call — totally dominated by the generator forward/backward on any reasonable audio backbone. Not a bottleneck.

## What's still missing (same as Drifting)

- We still need an audio φ. The Sinkhorn paper does **not** revisit the φ question — all their experiments are either toy 2D or in a pre-trained ALAE latent. So the AudioMAE / EnCodec / waveform-MAE question from 2602.04770 is unchanged.
- No CPU latency benchmarks anywhere in the paper.
- They explicitly couldn't run ImageNet-1k due to compute, so there is no large-scale head-to-head against Drifting's FID 1.54.

# Strengths

- **Closes Drifting's identifiability gap** with a real theorem, not just an assumption. This is the cleanest "drift = 0 ⇒ q = p" story currently available for 1-NFE generation.
- **Drop-in replacement: same inference cost, same loss shape, same backbone.** If you've implemented Drifting, you only need to swap the kernel-normalisation step for Sinkhorn-scaling.
- **Removes the self-distance masking hack.** One fewer hyperparameter / numerical-stability trap.
- **Robust across τ.** Most of the Drifting "tricks" (multi-temperature averaging, kernel x-axis softmax) can be dropped.
- Strict generalisation: `T = 1` recovers Drifting exactly, so this can't be worse than the baseline (in expectation).

# Limitations / things to test

- **No large-scale validation.** No ImageNet 256² number, so we don't know if the τ-stability gains carry to FID 1.54-level training runs.
- **Theory is for the `l = ∞` (fully-converged Sinkhorn) limit.** In practice they truncate at T = 30, and there's no quantitative bound on how `V^l` approximates `V^∞` for finite l. Empirically T = 30 works on their scale; we should sanity-check at audio scale.
- **Statistical rate worsens at low τ in high d.** `τ^{1−d/2}` factor in Prop 3.8. For audio in a high-d feature space, **at small τ we may need bigger batches** to keep the Monte-Carlo drift estimate well-behaved. This is the audio failure mode to watch for empirically.
- All experiments use Gaussian kernel `exp(−‖x − y‖² / τ)`; Drifting used Laplacian (ℓ₂, not squared) and made some hay out of it. Cross-comparison isn't apples-to-apples; an audio replication should sweep both kernels.
- They make no claim on whether `S_τ = 0` is *attainable* by the SGD optimiser. The identifiability theorem says the global optimum identifies p, q; it does not say the optimiser reaches it. Same caveat as Drifting.

# Open questions for this project

1. **Does Sinkhorn-Drifting close the gap to iMF / FD-loss in φ-space audio?** Drifting+Sinkhorn vs iMF vs FD-loss post-training on the same 1D waveform corpus.
2. **Is T=30 needed at audio scale, or does T=5–10 work?** Sinkhorn converges polylog in problem size, but the constant matters. A smaller T means more training throughput.
3. **At low τ in waveform-feature space, what batch size do we need to keep `‖V^∞ − V̂^∞‖` small?** Direct application of Prop 3.8: at `d` ~ feature dim and `τ` ~ typical inter-clip kernel temperature, what's `n`?
4. **Does the Sinkhorn modification also stabilise [MeanFlow-style] objectives?** Their bridge is specific to mean-shift drift but the cross-minus-self barycentric projection structure recurs in any kernel-based contrastive objective — including some flow-matching variants.
5. **Self-mask vs Sinkhorn ablation in raw audio.** Strong prior: Sinkhorn wins, but the audio kernel landscape is different from face-image latents.
