# EPG — End-to-end Pixel-space Generative model

## TL;DR (project-relevant)

EPG is an existence proof that you can train a strong **diffusion or one-step consistency model directly in raw sample space**, with no VAE and no off-the-shelf SSL model, and beat latent-diffusion (DiT/SiT) on ImageNet. The recipe is a two-stage curriculum: (1) pre-train the **encoder half** of a ViT with a contrastive + temporal-consistency objective (representations of clean image and its noisy versions along the same PF-ODE trajectory must align), then (2) attach a fresh decoder and fine-tune end-to-end as either a diffusion or a consistency model. For raw audio on CPU, the interesting bits are: the framing of "encoder = semantic learner, decoder = low-level reconstructor" lets you spend backbone capacity where it matters; the consistency variant gets one-step FID 8.82 on ImageNet-256 with 540M params; and patch size is scaled with resolution to keep token count (= compute) fixed, which is the right knob for long 1D audio.

## Problem

Pixel-space diffusion is "notoriously challenging" — high compute per step and slow convergence. Latent diffusion (LDM, DiT, SiT) sidesteps this with a pre-trained VAE, but inherits the VAE's reconstruction ceiling and depends on its training distribution. The authors want to *match* latent-diffusion quality while training only on pixels and without any pre-trained external model (no VAE, no MoCo/DINO, no teacher diffusion).

## Method

### Two-stage decomposition (the core idea)

Hypothesis: in a diffusion model, the encoder half of a ViT is doing semantic abstraction over noisy inputs (the "SSL-like" job), and the decoder half is conditioning on those semantics to spit out pixels. So decompose training the same way you would for classification SSL → fine-tune.

**Stage 1 — pre-train the encoder ("RCM", Representation Consistency Model).** ViT encoder + 3-layer MLP projector. Two loss terms (both NT-Xent on the [CLS] token):

- *Contrastive loss* (classic SimCLR/MoCo-style): two augmentations `y1, y2` of the same image are positives; other images in the batch are negatives. Operates on clean images.
- *Representation-consistency loss*: positive pair is two adjacent points `(x_{t_n}, x_{t_{n-1}})` on the **same PF-ODE trajectory** (obtained without a teacher by `x_{t_{n-1}} = x_{t_n} + t_n * ε` — same noise, lower noise level). Negative pairs are adjacent points from *different* trajectories. The pairs are processed by the online encoder vs. a stop-grad encoder.

Combined objective (Eq. 8):

```
E[ d_NTXent(L_θ(E_θ(y1, t0)), L_{θ⁻}(E_{θ⁻}(y2, t0)))    ← contrastive
 + d_NTXent(E_θ(x_{t_n}, t_n), E_{sg(θ)}(x_{t_{n-1}}, t_{n-1})) ]  ← temporal consistency
```

Key engineering bit: rather than annealing the EMA `θ⁻` on a hand-tuned schedule (the rRCM precursor did this; brittle), they **anneal the contrastive temperature** `τ` linearly from `τ_1 = 0.1` to `τ_2 = 0.2`. Small τ early → tight separation between trajectories; loosened τ later → tolerant alignment at high noise. Independently scheduled from other hyperparameters.

### Stage 2 — fine-tune end-to-end

Throw away the projector, glue a randomly-initialized ViT decoder onto the pre-trained encoder, train as:

- **Diffusion**: standard EDM denoising objective (`x`-prediction, LogNormal noise sampling). Could also be flow matching.
- **Consistency**: iCT-style consistency training (Eq. 5), plus an **auxiliary contrastive loss** between `f_θ(x_{t_n}, t_n)` and the clean image `x_0`, both passed through a *frozen copy* of the pre-trained encoder (Eq. 9). This is the trick that makes CT actually converge here — supplementary supervision without using an external model.

```
L_aux = d_NTXent( W_φ(f_θ(x_{t_n}, t_n), t_n) , W_φ(x_0, t_0) )    W_φ = frozen RCM encoder
```

### Architecture

Plain ViT, no U-Net. Encoder + decoder are two stacks of ViT blocks with **residual connections between encoder and decoder** (like SimpleDiffusion / DiffiT) and **adaLN-Zero** on the decoder for time/class conditioning.

Sizes (encoder, decoder blocks each):

| Model | Blocks (enc, dec) | Dim (enc, dec) | Params |
|---|---|---|---|
| B/16 | 12, 12 | 768, 768 | 229 M |
| L/16 | 16, 16 | 1024, 1024 | 540 M |
| XL/16 | 12, 12 | 768, 1584 | 583 M |
| XXL/16 | 12, 12 | 768, 1920 | 789 M |
| G/16 | 12, 12 | 768, 2688 | 1391 M |

Note the asymmetric XL/XXL/G — they scale the *decoder* width while keeping a 12-block 768-wide encoder. The encoder is "semantic" so doesn't need width; the decoder does the pixel reconstruction.

**Key for long sequences:** they keep the token count constant across resolutions by adjusting patch size — `16×16` on ImageNet-256 (256 tokens) and `32×32` on ImageNet-512 (also 256 tokens). Compute is set by token count, so scaling resolution costs only the input/output projection. This is precisely the trick that makes 1D long-sequence applications (audio) feasible: pick patch length so tokens stay manageable.

## Inference cost

- **Diffusion**: 75 NFE with Heun ODE sampler + classifier-free guidance (so 75×2 forward passes if you count CFG; they report `NFE 75` in tables).
- **Consistency**: **1 NFE**. EPG-L/16 (540 M) gets FID 8.82 one-step on ImageNet-256.
- GFLOPs per image (incl. CFG, ImageNet-256): EPG-G/16 = 321, EPG-XXL/16 = 176, EPG-XL/16 = 128 — vs. DiT-XL/2 = 312 (gen) + 119 (VAE) = 431. So pixel-space EPG is actually **cheaper** than DiT at comparable FID once you account for the VAE.

CPU expectation (raw audio relevance): a 540M ViT one-step on 256 tokens is still expensive on CPU — order of seconds per image. For audio we'd shrink: fewer tokens, fewer blocks, smaller width. But the *one-step* property is the win we'd port.

## Headline results

- ImageNet-256: **EPG-G/16 diffusion: FID 1.58** at 75 NFE (beats DiT-XL/2 at 2.27 with ~30% of its training compute, and SiT-XL/2 at 2.06).
- ImageNet-512: **EPG-L/32: FID 2.35** at 75 NFE, 540 M params, 113 GFLOPs.
- One-step consistency: **EPG-L/16: FID 8.82** in 1 NFE. First successful pixel-space consistency model trained from scratch (no VAE, no teacher diffusion). Beats latent iCT-XL/2 (FID 34.24) by a huge margin and is competitive with Shortcut-XL/2 (FID 10.60 at 1 NFE).
- Pre-training cost on ImageNet-256 (8×H200): RCM-B/16 = 57 h; RCM-L/16 = 100 h. Significantly cheaper than training the SD-VAE (160 h for sd-vae-ft-mse).

## Translation to raw audio

The pixel-space framing is exactly what we want — no VAE bottleneck, no learned latent space to maintain. What ports:

1. **The decomposition.** "Encoder learns semantics on noisy inputs; decoder reconstructs samples." For audio: encoder picks up timbre/pitch/event structure; decoder fills in waveform detail. Worth testing.
2. **Constant-token-count via patch scaling.** Image-256 → 16×16 patches → 256 tokens. Image-512 → 32×32 → still 256 tokens. For audio at 16 kHz: 1 s of mono = 16 000 samples; pick patch length so a 0.1–10 s clip stays at e.g. 256–1024 tokens. Patch length 64 gives 250 tokens for 1 s. This is the same lever and it's exactly the right one for CPU latency.
3. **One-step consistency in pixel space without a teacher.** EPG-L/16 demonstrates you can train CT *directly* from scratch in pixel space if you provide auxiliary semantic supervision (their Eq. 9 trick — frozen pre-trained encoder as the "semantic anchor"). For audio this is potentially huge: one-step waveform generation without ever training a 50-step diffusion teacher.

What does **not** trivially port:

- **Augmentation strategy.** Their pre-training relies on MoCo v3 augmentations (color jitter, crop, etc.) — these are image-specific. For audio we'd need an equivalent: time-shift, gain perturbation, pitch shift? An open question — what augmentations preserve "semantic identity" of an audio sample? For short musical samples, time-stretch and gain might be safe; pitch shift changes identity.
- **Phase / sign sensitivity.** Audio waveforms are signed and phase-sensitive. The contrastive loss operates on a CLS token, so it's semantic — phase shouldn't break it. But the decoder *must* reconstruct phase coherently; pixel L2-ish loss may give phasy/buzzy outputs unless we add a phase-aware term.
- **Stereo.** Treat as 2-channel patches (patch_len × 2 → flatten), same as RGB.
- **Sample rate.** Token count is the cost driver, not sample rate per se. Higher SR + proportionally larger patch length → same model cost.

## Strengths

- Clean ablations: pre-training loss terms (Tab. 13c) show contrastive loss is essential; removing it is **worse than training from scratch** because the encoder collapses to a useless representation.
- Demonstrates training a consistency model from scratch on high-res images **without a diffusion teacher** — closes a long-standing gap (iCT, ECT, IMM all leaned on teachers or weight-init tricks).
- Decoder asymmetry result is interesting: at fixed compute, growing the decoder beats growing the encoder. Implies "the semantic abstraction problem is small; the pixel reconstruction problem is what scales".

## Limitations / open questions

- Still under-performs the very best latent methods (REPA, RAE) at matched training budget — the paper admits this is a budget gap, not a fundamental one.
- One-step FID 8.82 is good for pixel space without a teacher, but Shortcut and IMM in latent space are better (with caveats — IMM uses 11× compute).
- The contrastive pre-training adds a whole training phase. For us, the experiment is: **can we skip pre-training entirely for short audio samples** (low-dimensional, simpler manifold) and still get one-step generation? Or is the semantic anchor (Eq. 9) doing the heavy lifting regardless?
- No latency benchmark for any backbone on commodity hardware. We need our own CPU numbers.

## Things to try in this repo

- **Port the architecture as-is to 1D.** Encoder+decoder ViT, patch_len ~64 on 16 kHz mono, 256 tokens for 1 s. Start with a B-scale model (~100 M). Train as a diffusion model first; then attempt consistency fine-tune with the auxiliary loss (Eq. 9).
- **Audio-appropriate augmentations.** Define a "MoCo for audio" augmentation stack: random gain, small time-shift, small EQ, additive noise. Pitch-shift only on instrument-class data where pitch isn't identity.
- **Ablate pre-training necessity.** Train EPG-style consistency *from scratch* (skip Stage 1). If short audio is easy enough, we may not need it.
- **Pinpoint the CPU latency wall.** Benchmark one forward pass of EPG-B/16 (229 M, 256 tokens) on CPU. Set this as a calibration baseline.
