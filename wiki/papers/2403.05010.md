# TL;DR (project-relevant)

A **conditional vocoder** (mel-spectrogram or EnCodec tokens → waveform), *not* an unconditional generator — but it bundles several tricks that port cleanly to our raw-audio short-sample setting: (1) **rectified flow with 10 Euler steps**, (2) **operate in STFT-frame space and predict the complex spectrogram** (ISTFT once at the end, instead of generating samples directly), (3) **multi-band parallelism** — the complex spectrogram is sliced along the frequency axis into 8 subbands processed concurrently by one shared backbone, (4) an **equal-straightness time-step selection** for the Euler integrator, (5) ConvNeXtV2 1D backbone with AdaLN(σ-index, t). Reported speed is 160× real-time on a 4090 for 22 kHz speech at 18M params and NFE=10. **Zero CPU benchmark**, like every other audio paper in this repo. The "predict complex spectrogram then single ISTFT" lever is the headline takeaway for our project: it converts a sample-rate-length problem into a frame-rate-length problem (~256× shorter sequence) before any neural compute happens.

## What this paper actually is

A vocoder for speech and music: given mel-spectrogram or discrete EnCodec tokens, reconstruct the time-domain waveform. **Not unconditional generation.** Sits in the same niche as Vocos, BigVGAN, PriorGrad, FreGrad, MBD. RFWave's pitch over GAN-based vocoders (BigVGAN, Vocos): diffusion-grade quality and OOD generalization, none of the GAN training pathologies. Pitch over diffusion vocoders (PriorGrad, FreGrad, MBD): they all need 6–50 sampling steps *at sample rate*, RFWave needs 10 steps *at frame rate*.

## Method in detail

### Frame-level rectified flow on complex spectrograms

Standard rectified flow: linear interpolation `X_t = (1-t)X_0 + tX_1` with `X_0 ∼ N(0,I)` and `X_1` the target. Velocity field `v(X_t, t|C) ≈ X_1 − X_0` learned by MSE; sampling is forward Euler with `n` equal-or-chosen steps. Nothing novel about the RF objective itself.

The **structural choice** is what matters here: `X_1` is the *complex STFT spectrogram* of the waveform (real / imaginary parts stacked), not the waveform itself. Sequence length is `F` frames (hundreds), not `T` samples (tens of thousands). All neural compute happens at frame rate; a single deterministic ISTFT recovers samples at the end. The paper notes the real/imag distribution is more spatially homogeneous than magnitude/phase, which helps the regression.

There's also a "time-domain" variant where the network operates on raw waveform but uses STFT/ISTFT to project in and out of frame space each step. It's slightly higher PESQ (better high-freq capture) but doubles per-step cost (extra STFT+ISTFT per step). Authors default to the time-domain variant in tables, frame-domain is faster.

### Multi-band parallelism

The full complex spectrogram (dimension `d` = 2 × n_fft//2 across real/imag) is sliced along the **frequency axis** into 8 equal-width subbands with an 8-dim overlap on each side. Subbands are stacked into the **batch dimension** and processed concurrently by one shared backbone, conditioned on a subband-index embedding via AdaLN. At inference all 8 subbands are predicted **independently and in parallel** (no autoregressive low→high cascade, unlike MBD), which avoids error accumulation up the frequency ladder. An **overlap loss** (MSE on the overlapping margins between adjacent predicted subbands) trains the model to be consistent at the seams without needing cross-subband conditioning at inference.

This is genuinely different from prior multi-band vocoders: Multi-band MelGAN uses PQMF on the *waveform*; MBD models each band with a *separate* diffusion process. RFWave: one model, frequency-axis batched, parallel decode.

### Architecture

- 8 stacked **ConvNeXtV2** blocks, channel width 512, FFN inner width 1536, depthwise kernel 7.
- Inputs concatenated: noisy sample `X_t`, sinusoidal/Fourier-feature embedding of `t`, conditioning `C` (mel-spec or EnCodec tokens), optional EnCodec bandwidth index, subband index.
- Conditioning injection: sinusoidal `t` embedding added element-wise to every block input; **subband index via AdaLN** (adaptive LayerNorm with learnable per-subband γ/β).
- **18.1 M params for 22 kHz**, 20.5 M for 44.1 kHz. By contrast BigVGAN is 107–116 M.
- Trained at 22.05 / 24 kHz on 32 512-sample crops (≈128 frames), batch 64, AdamW LR 2e-4 with cosine to 2e-6.

### Three auxiliary losses

The RF velocity MSE alone leaves audible artifacts; three add-ons fix them.

1. **Energy-balanced loss.** Per-frame, per-subband energy varies enormously (music ≠ silence). Plain MSE under-weights low-energy frames → audible noise in silent regions. Compute per-subband per-feature std `σ = √Var(X_1 − X_0)` along the frame axis, divide both prediction and target by it before the MSE.
   `min_v E ‖(X_1 − X_0)/σ − v(X_t, t|C)/σ‖²`
   Conceptually similar to PriorGrad's data-dependent prior, but it lives in the loss not in the prior — no manual schedule design needed.
2. **Overlap loss.** MSE on the 8-dim margins between adjacent subbands. Weight 0.01 relative to the RF loss.
3. **STFT loss.** From the linear-interpolation form, `v ≈ X_1 − X_0`, so a one-step estimate of the clean spectrogram is `X̃_1 = X_0 + v`. Apply spectral-convergence + log-magnitude STFT losses on `X̃_1` against the true `X_1`. Weight 0.01. Note: this loss is on STFT-of-the-waveform-derived-from-the-predicted-spectrogram, so for the frequency-domain model it requires an extra ISTFT during training.

### Equal-straightness Euler time-step selection

Standard rectified-flow inference uses uniform `t ∈ {0, 1/n, …, (n−1)/n}`. Authors instead define a **straightness curve** `S(v) = ∫₀ᵗ E‖(X_1 − X_0) − v(X_t, τ|C)‖² dτ` and pick the `n` time points such that the **cumulative straightness increases by the same amount per interval**. Figure A.2 shows the deviation `E‖(X_1−X_0)−v(X_t,t|C)‖²` is U-shaped: high near `t=0` and `t=1`, low in the middle. Equal-straightness clusters Euler steps near the endpoints where the velocity field is changing fast and spreads them out in the easy middle. Computed once per trained model from a single batch of 96, then frozen. Gives the largest single-line ablation gain (PESQ 4.211 → 4.275, see Table 5).

This is a clean, model-agnostic trick: **any rectified-flow / flow-matching model** can compute its own straightness curve post-hoc and reallocate Euler steps. It costs nothing at inference.

## Inference cost

| Setting | NFE | Params | xRT (4090, batch 1, PyTorch, no kernels) | GPU mem |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| RFWave, 22 kHz | 10 | 18.1 M | **162.6×** | 780 MB |
| RFWave, 44.1 kHz | 10 | 20.5 M | **152.6×** | 902 MB |
| BigVGAN (GAN) | 1 | 107.7 M | 72.7× | 1436 MB |
| BigVGAN, 44.1 kHz | 1 | 116.5 M | 39.0× | 1740 MB |
| Vocos (iSTFT GAN) | 1 | 13.5 M | **2078×** | 590 MB |
| PriorGrad (diffusion) | 6 | 2.6 M | 16.7× | 4976 MB |
| FreGrad (diffusion) | 50 | 1.7 M | 7.5× | 2720 MB |
| MBD (diffusion) | 20 | 411 M | 4.8× | 5480 MB |

All numbers from paper Table 7, RTX 4090, batch size 1, LJSpeech test set. **No CPU number is reported anywhere in the paper.**

Key observation: RFWave is **2.2× faster than BigVGAN on GPU** despite using 10 NFE vs BigVGAN's 1, because BigVGAN works at the waveform sample rate and RFWave works at the STFT frame rate. The frame-rate advantage swamps the NFE disadvantage. Vocos (also frame-rate, 1 NFE, GAN) is 13× faster again — that's the headline number for "what is achievable at frame-rate" but Vocos has GAN-training-quality problems on OOD audio.

## Results

- **Speech (LibriTTS in-domain) MOS:** RFWave 3.82 ≥ BigVGAN 3.78 ≥ Vocos 3.74 (ground truth 3.91). Statistically tied with BigVGAN.
- **Music (MUSDB18 out-of-domain) MOS:** RFWave 3.67 > BigVGAN 3.51 > Vocos 3.10 (ground truth 3.80). **Notable OOD gap** — diffusion-type method generalizes better than GANs even with Snake activation (BigVGAN).
- **vs diffusion vocoders (PriorGrad, FreGrad):** RFWave wins on MOS (3.95 vs 3.75 / 2.99) and all objective metrics (PESQ 4.20, ViSQOL 4.46, V/UV F1 0.98).
- **EnCodec tokens → waveform:** beats EnCodec's own GAN decoder and MBD on MOS across 1.5 / 3.0 / 6.0 / 12.0 kbps. ViSQOL slightly behind EnCodec's GAN (paper attributes this to a GAN bias in ViSQOL).

### Ablations worth noting

- **Rectified flow vs DDPM** (same backbone, time-domain, 3 losses): RF at 10 steps PESQ 4.211, ViSQOL 4.578, 162× RT. DDPM at 50 steps PESQ 2.790, ViSQOL 4.499, 34× RT. RF is the critical ingredient — the *same architecture* trained with DDPM is much worse even at 5× the NFE.
- **ConvNeXtV2 vs ResNet** (same param count): ConvNeXtV2 PESQ 4.211 vs ResNet 3.865. ConvNeXt is the right backbone.
- **Backbone scaling:** half-size 8.6M params → 228× RT, PESQ 4.128. Double-size 28.5M params → 112× RT, PESQ 4.285. Sublinear quality returns to size; the small variant is essentially as good for many uses.
- **Loss ablation** (each added incrementally on top of plain RF, time-domain):
  - base time-domain RF: PESQ 4.127
  - + energy-balanced loss: PESQ 4.181 (helps)
  - + overlap loss: PESQ 4.158 (slight regression on PESQ but removes subband seam artifacts)
  - + STFT loss: PESQ 4.211 (helps, but slightly hurts phase metrics — magnitude bias)
  - + equal straightness: PESQ 4.275 (free)

## What translates to our project (raw sample-space, short samples, CPU)

This is a vocoder, but several mechanisms are reusable:

1. **Predict the complex spectrogram, ISTFT at the end.** This is the single biggest lever in the paper. For a 1 s 22 kHz clip, sample-rate length is 22 050; frame-rate length at hop 256 is 86. **A ~256× reduction in sequence length before any compute happens.** For our unconditional / short-sample generation target this is directly applicable: a complex-spectrogram diffusion at ~86–256 frames is much more tractable on CPU than a raw-sample diffusion at 22 050 samples. The trade-off: you commit to STFT as an inductive bias and the model must learn a coherent phase. RFWave shows this works at vocoder quality; whether it works for *unconditional* generation (no conditioning to lock phase) is open.
2. **Frequency-axis multi-band batching.** Independent of (1). Take your complex spectrogram, slice frequency into K subbands, batch them, single shared backbone, add overlap loss. Equivalent to a `K×` width reduction at the cost of `K×` batch. Especially useful when the spectrogram is wide (high sample rate) and the time axis is short — exactly our short-sample regime.
3. **Equal-straightness Euler.** Free quality bump for any rectified-flow / flow-matching model we train, including ones unrelated to this paper. Compute the straightness curve once after training; rebin Euler steps. Tiny code change.
4. **Energy-balanced loss.** General-purpose fix for "MSE under-weights low-energy regions"; relevant for any audio diffusion where silence-region artefacts are a problem (typical for one-shots with attack-decay envelopes — drum tails, plucked-string releases).
5. **What does *not* translate.** The conditioning structure (mel/EnCodec → waveform) is irrelevant for our unconditional regime. ViSQOL/PESQ/V-UV-F1 are speech metrics; for music one-shots we'd evaluate with FAD or FDr^k regardless of what this paper does. The 4090 GPU numbers don't tell us anything about CPU latency.

### CPU back-of-envelope

18 M params × 10 NFE × ~86 frames per second is **far** cheaper per generated second than 18 M params × 10 NFE × 22 050 samples, even allowing for the wider channel dim at frame rate. We have no CPU number for RFWave but the ratio vs sample-rate operation is the same multiplicative factor the GPU benchmark shows. The 18 M / 10-NFE budget is plausibly in real-time territory on a modern laptop CPU — worth a quick benchmark.

## Strengths

- Cleanest published demonstration that **frame-level + few-NFE rectified flow** beats both diffusion vocoders (at >5× speedup) and GAN vocoders (on quality and OOD generalization).
- Several **modular tricks** (energy-balanced loss, equal-straightness Euler, frequency-axis multi-band) that compose into other systems.
- Honest ablations — each component's contribution measured separately.
- Works on speech *and* music *and* environmental sound; same model, retrained per domain.

## Limitations / open questions for this project

- **Vocoder only.** All conclusions are conditioned on a strong mel/EnCodec input. Whether the complex-spectrogram-as-target trick survives the absence of conditioning is unanswered.
- **No CPU benchmark.** Repeat the refrain.
- **No FAD or audio FD-metric.** Quality measured by PESQ / ViSQOL / MOS — speech-biased. For music short-sample work we'd need to re-evaluate.
- **Complex-spectrogram phase generation is hard unconditionally.** Vocoders get phase from the conditioning context; unconditional models would have to learn coherent phase from noise. Possible failure mode if we port the recipe directly to unconditional drum/music one-shots.
- **Multi-band assumes a wide spectrogram.** For very short clips (0.1 s) the frame axis is already short; freq-axis multi-band still applies but the gain shrinks.
- **8 ConvNeXtV2 blocks × 512 ch × kernel 7** is a non-trivial baseline. Pruning to a CPU-real-time-on-laptop config is its own project.
