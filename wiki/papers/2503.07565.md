## TL;DR (project lens)

A **single-stage, from-scratch few-step generator** that drops the two stable-training crutches one-step methods normally need: no teacher, no GAN, no LPIPS, no ad-hoc tricks. The training objective is **MMD between distributions at two time-steps along a stochastic interpolant**, learned by *inductive bootstrapping* — the model at time `s` is forced to match itself jumped from a slightly earlier `r` and the original `t`. Headline: **1.99 FID on ImageNet-256 at 8 NFE** (DiT-XL/2, 675 M params, no distillation). The most important result for this project is structural: **Lemma 1 proves Consistency Models are a single-particle (M=1), first-moment (energy-kernel) special case of IMM**, and the paper attributes CM training instability directly to the repulsion-force and higher-moment terms that the M=1 collapse drops. Particle number `M` is the explicit knob: `M=1` collapses on ImageNet-256 (this is CM), `M=2–4` is stable. For raw-audio few-step training from scratch on CPU, IMM is the cleanest unifying framework we have for the consistency-model family — and tells us *why* CT-from-scratch is unstable and what minimum change fixes it.

## Why this matters for raw-audio CPU generation

- **From-scratch and single-stage** — no precomputed teacher, no GAN player to balance, no LPIPS (which is image-only and doesn't translate to audio). All of these blockers are gone.
- **Few-step, not one-step, but flexibly so.** Trained once, evaluated at any NFE ∈ {1, 2, 4, 8}. Quality improves monotonically with NFE *and* with model size (Fig. 7). For audio on CPU, this lets us pick the cheapest NFE that still meets perceptual quality without retraining.
- **CM instability is now a known structural issue, not a tuning issue.** Anyone trying CT-from-scratch on raw waveforms inherits this. IMM's M ≥ 2 / MMD kernel formulation is the documented fix.
- **Architecture-agnostic.** Reported on DDPM++ (CIFAR) and DiT (ImageNet) without special components (no JVP-through-flash-attention as in CTM, no LPIPS, no weight-decay schedule). A 1-D conv or 1-D transformer with the same loss should "just work" — no audio φ required.

## The method

### Setup: stochastic interpolants

Same setup as flow matching / DDIM: an interpolant `x_t = α_t x + σ_t ε` between data `x ∼ q` (at `t=0`) and prior `ε ∼ p` (at `t=1`). Marginal `q_t(x_t) = ∫∫ q_t(x_t | x, ε) q(x) p(ε) dx dε`.

### What the model learns

A **one-step sampler** parameterised as `f^θ_{s,t}(x_t)` that maps `x_t ∼ q_t` to `x_s ∼ q_s` for any `s < t`. At inference, push `ε ∼ N(0, σ_d² I)` straight to `s=0` (one step) or chain through intermediates (few-step).

### Training objective — the inductive bootstrap

This is the core idea. You can't directly minimise `D(q_s, p^θ_{s|t})` for arbitrary `(s, t)` because `q_t` is far from the model's current iterate at large `t` (naïve objective, Eq. 7). IMM instead enforces a **self-consistency** between two jumps: at any `(s, r, t)` with `s ≤ r ≤ t`,

```
p^{θ_n}_{s|t}(x_s)  ≈  p^{θ_{n−1}}_{s|r}(x_s)
```

The right side is the *target* — the model at the previous iterate, jumped from a closer-in time `r` (where it's already known to be good by induction). The left side is the *current* model jumping farther, from `t`. Loss is **MMD between the two**:

```
L_IMM(θ) = E_{s,t} [ w(s, t) · MMD²( p^{θ_n}_{s|t}(x_s), p^{θ_{n−1}}_{s|r(s,t)}(x_s) ) ]
```

By induction on the time-step grid (`n` is the grid index, not the optimiser step), the model converges to `q_s` for all `s ∈ [0, 1]`. Theorem 1: in the limit (infinite data + capacity), `MMD²(q_s, p^θ_{s|t}) → 0`.

The MMD is estimated with `M` particles per `(s, t)` group inside the batch (`M = 2, 4` in practice). Particles in the same group share `(s, r, t)` and contribute pairwise kernel evaluations. Variance trick: **reuse `x_t` for `x_r`** by setting `x_r = DDIM(x_t, x, r, t)` (Appendix C.2) — preserves the marginal `q_r` while reducing sample noise.

### MMD details

Kernel choice matters. They use a **Laplace kernel** with time-dependent bandwidth, `k_{s,t}(x, y) = exp(−w̃(s, t) max(‖x − y‖, ε)/D)`. Laplace gave better gradients than RBF in their ablations.

Critically — and this is the point for our wiki — **MMD with a characteristic kernel matches *all* moments**, not just first moments (Eq. 1). Choosing `k(x, y) = −‖x − y‖²` (the energy kernel, conditionally positive definite) collapses MMD to a first-moment-only objective. The improved-CM pseudo-Huber loss `k_c(x, y) = c − √(‖x − y‖² + c²)` is a CPD kernel that matches all moments with weights set by `c` (Lemma 2 in the paper).

### Network parameterisation

```
f^θ_{s,t}(x_t) = c_skip(s, t) x_t + c_out(s, t) G_θ(c_in(t) x_t, c_noise(s), c_noise(t))
```

Three valid choices (ablated in Table 3):

- **Identity** — `c_skip = 0, c_out = 1`. Always works.
- **Simple-EDM** — `c_skip = α_t / (α_t² + σ_t²)`, `c_out = −σ_t σ_d / √(α_t² + σ_t²)`. Best on CIFAR. With cosine schedule this reduces exactly to v-prediction.
- **Euler-FM** — `c_skip = 1, c_out = −t σ_d`. Best on ImageNet-256, paired with OT-FM schedule.

`c_noise(t) = ct` with `c ≥ 1` (they use ~1000); larger `c` helps the network distinguish nearby `r` and `t`.

### Algorithm 1 (skeleton)

```
Sample batch B, split into B/M groups (each shares (s, r, t)).
For each group:
  x_t = DDIM(ε, x, t, 1)               # noise data to t
  x_r = DDIM(x_t, x, r, t)             # reuse — variance reduction
Class-label drop for CFG with prob p.
Minimise L̂_IMM via Eq. 67 (MMD V-statistic over M×M kernel grid).
θ^− := EMA / stop-grad copy of θ.
```

**Cost per step:** 2 forward passes regardless of `M` — one with `θ` (for x_t side), one with `θ^−` (for x_r side, stop-grad). MMD matrix cost is `O(BM)` and negligible vs forward-pass `O(BK)`.

## The CM-as-special-case lemma (the structural payoff)

**Lemma 1.** When `x_t = x_t'` (so `M = 1`, a single particle per group), `x_r = x_r'`, and the kernel is the energy kernel `k(x, y) = −‖x − y‖²` with `s > 0` small, IMM's loss (Eq. 12) reduces to

```
E_{x_t, x, t} [ w(s, t) · ‖ G_θ(x_t, t) − G_{θ^−}(x_r, r) ‖² ]
```

— **exactly the CM loss** (point-wise L2 between current and EMA networks at adjacent times along the PF-ODE).

Two terms are dropped in this collapse:

1. **The repulsion force.** MMD has cross-particle terms `−k(x_{s,t}, x'_{s,r}) − k(x'_{s,t}, x_{s,r})`. With `M = 1` you have no cross particles; the repulsion vanishes.
2. **Higher moments.** The energy kernel matches only the **first** moment. CPD kernels with non-trivial structure (Laplace, RBF, pseudo-Huber) match all higher moments too.

The paper's read: **these two omissions are why CT-from-scratch is unstable.** The single-particle, first-moment IMM objective is what you literally get if you take iCT and remove its training crutches (LPIPS, schedules, weight decay). Their Fig. 5 directly confirms: on ImageNet-256, `M = 1` (= CM) collapses; `M = 2, 4` is stable.

This is the cleanest unification of the consistency-model and moment-matching families I've seen written down.

## Other notable choices

- **Pushforward vs Restart sampling.** Pushforward = repeated one-step jumps with intermediate `t_1` (η_{t_1} = 1.4 they found empirically). Restart re-noises between steps. Pushforward+uniform won on ImageNet-256 (Fig. 6).
- **Flow schedule.** OT-FM + Euler parameterisation wins on ImageNet-256; cosine + Simple-EDM wins on CIFAR-10 (Table 3).
- **Weighting.** VDM-style `w(s, t) = ½ σ(b − λ_t)(−dλ_t/dt) · α_t / (α_t² + σ_t²)` with the `1/(α_t² + σ_t²)` factor up-weighting middle time-steps (a known FM trick).
- **CFG.** Standard label-drop training; `w = 1.25–1.5` at sample time. No special CFG-conditioning (unlike iMF).
- **`r(s, t)` mapping.** Constant decrement in `η_t = σ_t / α_t`. Gap chosen as `(η_max − η_min)/2^k` for `k ∈ {10, …, 15}`. They warn `k = 14` becomes unstable; sweet spot is `k = 11, 12, 13`.

## Translation to raw audio

- **Direct drop-in.** No teacher, no LPIPS (image-only), no GAN, no VAE, no JVP through flash attention (unlike CTM). The full ingredient list is: a 1-D backbone (`G_θ`), a DDIM-style interpolant, an MMD with a CPD kernel, an EMA copy. All audio-agnostic.
- **Particle number `M` is the project knob.** The fact that `M=1` (= CT) collapses on ImageNet but `M=2, 4` is stable directly predicts that any *naïve consistency-from-scratch on raw waveforms* will be unstable, and that the minimum fix is to switch to MMD with `M ≥ 2`. This is a strong, testable prior for our first one-step audio experiments.
- **Kernel choice for waveforms.** The Laplace kernel is dimension-agnostic but the **bandwidth** matters: paper uses `w̃(s, t) = 1/|c_out(s, t)|` so the kernel auto-scales with target dynamic range. For raw waveform at sample rate `r`, samples are bounded in roughly `[−1, 1]` so the bandwidth choice transfers; for log-mel or learned-latent representations we'd need to re-tune.
- **Compute.** 2 forward passes per training step. For batch `B`, MMD matrix is `O(BM)` — negligible. Training cost is comparable to standard FM. **At inference, 1–8 forward passes** — the same budget as iMF, Drifting, EPG-consistency. For a 1-s 16-kHz raw waveform on a CPU-friendly 1-D backbone, an 8-NFE IMM might still be far slower than RT, but 1–2 NFE may be tractable.
- **Comparison to other from-scratch one-step paths.** IMM is the *most general* framework but the *least audio-validated* — it's a clean theory but hasn't been tried on 1-D anything. iMF is more surgical (a smaller patch on FM with explicit guidance conditioning); EPG ships with its own SSL anchor; Drifting needs an audio φ. IMM is the cleanest "no extras" recipe.
- **Stereo / phase.** Nothing in the loss touches phase explicitly — MMD compares raw samples in waveform space. The same caveats as any raw-waveform method apply: kernel bandwidth in sample space may need tuning per dataset (drums vs speech vs music).
- **One project-relevant warning.** IMM ablations show that `M=1` *trains stably on CIFAR-10* but *collapses on ImageNet-256*. The collapse appears as you scale to higher-dim, harder distributions. For audio, 1-D waveforms at 16k–48k samples are very high-dim — we should plan for `M ≥ 2` from the start, not retreat to `M = 1` as a "simpler" baseline.

## Strengths

- Single-stage from-scratch + few-step + no special tricks. Almost unique in the literature.
- Cleanly subsumes Consistency Models and explains their instability.
- Strong empirical results without LPIPS or distillation.
- Particle number `M` is a simple, well-ablated knob.
- Schedule / parameterisation choices are well-ablated (Tables 3, 4; Figs 10, 11).
- 8-NFE IMM matches and exceeds 250-NFE diffusion *with the same architecture and parameter count*.

## Limitations / open questions

- **Not yet 1-NFE-competitive at scale.** IMM at 1 NFE on ImageNet-256 is FID 8.05 (XL/2, `w=1.5`) — substantially worse than iMF (1.72) or Drifting (1.54–1.61). IMM shines from 2 NFE onwards. For our 1-NFE CPU-target, this is a real cost-vs-quality trade-off compared to iMF.
- No audio results in the paper; no 1-D backbone tried.
- Kernel bandwidth `w̃(s, t)` is tuned heuristically; theory doesn't fix it.
- Particle number `M` interacts with batch size `B` (you get `B/M` groups). Small `B` on the audio side (we'd typically have `B ≤ 64` at long sequence lengths) means `M = 2` already halves the effective per-group batch — needs measurement.
- The proof of stability is "induction over the time-step grid with infinite data and capacity" — finite-data behavior at long `r(s, t)` gaps is empirical.
