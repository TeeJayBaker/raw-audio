# TL;DR — why this matters for raw-audio CPU gen

SoFlow is a third **from-scratch, distillation-free, GAN-free 1-NFE recipe** in the MeanFlow / fastforward family. Its single load-bearing claim relative to MeanFlow/iMF is **no Jacobian-vector product anywhere in training** — the consistency loss is constructed from a Taylor-expansion identity that only ever needs ordinary forward/backward through the network. For us this matters mechanically: JVP through `torch.func.jvp` works but is incompatible with PyTorch's memory-efficient attention (SoFlow Tab. 5 shows MeanFlow falls back to "math attention", taking ~50 GB and 2.39 it/s vs SoFlow at 35 GB and 2.94 it/s — **−31% memory, +23% throughput** with the same DiT). On a 24-GB 4090 with 1-D backbones at long sequence lengths, that headroom is the difference between fitting and not fitting. SoFlow beats MeanFlow on every DiT size at matched epochs (XL/2: **2.96 vs 3.43 FID @ 1-NFE**, 2.66 vs 2.93 @ 2-NFE), but does *not* beat iMF (1.72 FID @ 1-NFE) — the SoFlow paper does not cite iMF and is concurrent. For raw-audio replication SoFlow is the obvious choice when JVP is the bottleneck; iMF remains the obvious choice when JVP isn't.

# What problem does it solve

The MeanFlow family parametrises an *average* velocity `u_θ(z_t, r, t)` and trains via the MeanFlow identity, which requires the time-derivative `d/dt u` — implemented as a Jacobian-vector product (JVP) through the network. Two practical pains follow:

1. **PyTorch's `torch.func.jvp` is incompatible with `F.scaled_dot_product_attention`'s flash/memem-efficient kernels**, forcing MeanFlow to use the slower "math" attention backend. SoFlow Appendix D quantifies the cost on identical DiT codebases.
2. The training target is network-dependent — iMF (concurrent, not cited) attacks this with a v-loss reparameterisation. SoFlow attacks it differently: replace the JVP-based identity with a **finite-difference consistency identity** over the time axis, which uses only forward/backward.

SoFlow's framing: rather than learning the *velocity* field `v(x_t, t)` and substituting it into the MeanFlow identity, **directly learn the solution function** `f(x_t, t, s)` of the velocity ODE — the map that takes the state at time `t` to its evolved state at time `s` along the ODE. One-NFE sampling is then literally `x_0 = f_θ(x_1, t=1, s=0)`.

# Method

## (1) Bi-time parameterisation

The network takes **two times**, `t` and `s`, and predicts the solution `f_θ(x_t, t, s)` — the value of the ODE trajectory at time `s` started from `x_t` at time `t`. Two times are fed by adding positional embeddings of their difference `s − t` (Sec. 5.1).

The parameterisation enforces the boundary condition `f_θ(x_t, t, t) = x_t` by writing

```
f_θ(x_t, t, s) = a(t, s) · x_t + b(t, s) · F_θ(x_t, t, s)
```

with `a(t, t) = 1` and `b(t, t) = 0`. Two specific instantiations:

- **Linear-Euler**: `f_θ = x_t + (s − t) · F_θ` (this is the configuration that wins in Tab. 1d).
- **Trigonometric**: `f_θ = cos((π/2)(s−t)) · x_t + sin((π/2)(s−t)) · F_θ`.

The linear noising schedule `x_t = (1−t) x_0 + t x_1` is paired with the Euler parameterisation; trigonometric is paired with the cosine schedule. Linear-Euler is best (11.59 vs 13–19 FID for the other three combos in ablation).

## (2) Two losses: Flow Matching + Solution Consistency

### Flow Matching loss

By the ODE definition, at `t = s` the third-argument derivative gives the velocity:
```
∂_3 f_θ(x_t, t, t) = v(x_t, t).
```
With the linear-Euler parameterisation, `∂_3 f_θ(x_t, t, t) = b'(t, t) · F_θ(x_t, t, t) + 0 = F_θ(x_t, t, t)`. So `F_θ` at `s = t` directly outputs the marginal velocity. The Flow Matching loss is the standard conditional FM regression on `F_θ`:

```
L_FM = E[ w_FM / n · ‖ ∂_2 a(t,t) · x_t + ∂_2 b(t,t) · F_θ(x_t, t, t) − (α'_t x_0 + β'_t x_1) ‖² ]
```

(Their Eq. 17; the target `α'_t x_0 + β'_t x_1` is the conditional velocity that approximates the intractable marginal `v(x_t, t)`.)

### Solution Consistency loss — the JVP-free trick

The key identity. For any three time points `s < l < t` with `l` close to `t`, the solution function satisfies (Taylor expansion in `l` around `t`, plus the ODE definition `∂_3 f = v ∘ f`):

```
f_θ(x_t, t, s) ≈ f_θ−( x_t + v(x_t, t) · (l − t),  l,  s )       (Eq. 19)
```

i.e. **"jumping from `(t, s)` should equal: take one explicit Euler step of size `(l − t)` in input space using the true velocity `v`, then jump from `(l, s)`"**. The RHS is evaluated with stop-gradient (the EMA / detached copy). The training loss is plain MSE between the two:

```
L_SCM = E[ w_SCM / n · ‖ f_θ(x_t, t, s)  −  f_θ−( x_t + (α'_t x_0 + β'_t x_1)(l − t), l, s ) ‖² ]
```

where the intractable `v(x_t, t)` inside the Euler nudge is replaced by the conditional velocity `(α'_t x_0 + β'_t x_1)` (the same FM-style approximation).

**No JVP.** The only network calls are `f_θ(x_t, t, s)` (gradient path) and `f_θ−(x_t', l, s)` (stop-grad). Both are ordinary forward passes — compatible with memory-efficient attention. The price: the loss requires the auxiliary intermediate time `l ∈ (s, t)`, sampled per-batch via `l = t + (s − t) · r(k, K)` where `r(k, K)` is a monotonically-decreasing schedule from `r_init` toward 0 across training. As training progresses `l → t`, tightening the Taylor approximation.

### Total loss
```
L = λ · L_FM + (1 − λ) · L_SCM        with λ = 0.75 best.
```
The FM share is non-trivially large — 75% of samples train on FM, only 25% on the consistency loss. The FM loss is not redundant: it (a) gives the network a clean marginal-velocity prediction at the boundary, and (b) directly supports CFG during training (the velocity for CFG is the same `F_θ` evaluated at `s = t`). Without it, FID is much worse (Tab. 1b: λ=0 → 66.36, λ=0.75 → 58.57).

### Adaptive weighting (carried over from MF/iMF)

```
w_FM(t, MSE) = 1 / ( |∂_2 b(t, t)| · (MSE + ε)^p )
w_SCM(t, l, MSE) = 1 / ( (t − l) · |b(l, s)| ) · 1 / ( (MSE / (t−l)²) + ε )^p
```

The `(t − l)²` rescaling reflects that the SCM residual is `O((t − l)²)` (their theoretical bound, Eq. 34). `p = 0.5–1.0` works; `p = 0` (plain MSE) is significantly worse (Tab. 1c: 68.25 vs 58.57).

## (3) CFG **during training**, not at inference

Because `F_θ` predicts a velocity at `s = t`, CFG can be applied **at training time** in the standard way:
```
v_g(x_t, t, c) = w · v(x_t, t | c) + (1 − w) · v(x_t, t).
```
The unconditional `v(x_t, t)` is provided by the same network with the condition replaced by an empty label `φ` (dropout 0.1 during training). The training-time CFG-mixed velocity is then plugged into both `L_FM^g` and `L_SCM^g`, where it appears inside the Euler nudge in the SCM loss target.

A bonus mixing knob — the **velocity mix ratio `m`** — interpolates the noisy conditional-velocity estimate `(α'_t x_0 + β'_t x_1)` with the model-predicted guided velocity `v_guided = F_θ−(x_t, t, t, c)`:
```
v_mix = m · (w · (α'_t x_0 + β'_t x_1) + (1 − w) · v_uncond) + (1 − m) · v_guided.
```
`m` small (0.25) is best (Tab. 1f). The model-predicted velocity has lower variance than the stochastic conditional, so weighting toward it stabilises training.

**Result:** the trained model has CFG baked in. At inference SoFlow runs exactly 1 NFE (or 2 NFE) — no inference-time CFG pass needed.

## (4) Architecture

Standard DiT (Peebles & Xie 2023), unchanged. The bi-time conditioning is a single extra positional embedding of `s − t`. Scales tested: B/4, B/2, M/2, L/2, XL/2 — same depth/width grid as MeanFlow for direct comparison.

| Config     | Params | Depth | Width | Heads | Patch | Epochs |
|------------|--------|-------|-------|-------|-------|--------|
| SoFlow-B/4 | 131 M  | 12    | 768   | 12    | 4×4   | 80     |
| SoFlow-B/2 | 131 M  | 12    | 768   | 12    | 2×2   | 240    |
| SoFlow-M/2 | 308 M  | 16    | 1024  | 16    | 2×2   | 240    |

## (5) Training recipe (ImageNet 256×256)

- AdamW, lr 1e-4 constant, β = (0.9, 0.99), no LR decay, no WD.
- EMA decay 0.9999.
- Time sampling: logit-normal. `t ~ σ(N(0.2, 0.8²))`, `s ~ σ(N(−1.0, 0.8²))`, `l = t + (s − t)·r(k, K)`. (Note: `μ_t > 0 > μ_s` — the network sees mostly forward jumps from noisy `t` toward clean `s`.)
- `l → t` schedule: exponential `r(k, K) = r_init · (r_end / r_init)^(k/K)` with `r_init = 1/10`, `r_end = 1/500`.
- λ = 0.75 (FM share), `p = 1.0` (adaptive weight exponent).
- CFG strength `w`: 2.5 / 2.25 / 2.0 / 2.0 for B-M-L-XL.
- Velocity mix `m = 0.25`.
- CFG decay in high-noise region: `w` smoothly decays toward 1.0 for `t > 0.8` via `1 − exp(−(1/40) · t'/(1 − t'))`, ensuring the guided velocity field is continuously differentiable.
- VAE is SD-VAE-ft-MSE (same as DiT / MeanFlow / iMF).

CIFAR-10 (32×32, pixel space, U-Net backbone): batch 1024, 800K iters, RAdam, otherwise comparable.

# Inference cost — the headline for this project

- **1 NFE.** One forward pass of `f_θ(x_1, t=1, s=0)`.
- **2 NFE = best.** Re-noise and re-apply gives a meaningful FID drop at every scale (XL/2: 2.96 → 2.66). This is consistent with consistency-model behaviour.
- No inference-time CFG pass — CFG is internal to the trained `F_θ`.
- VAE decoder is separate (same caveat as iMF / MeanFlow).
- **At our scale and target:** SoFlow-B/4 is 131 M params @ 5.6 Gflops/sample. On 1-D inputs this is still well above our CPU real-time budget for a 1 s clip; what we'd port is the *recipe* on a 1-D conv/transformer backbone with O(1) M params.

**Training cost** is the differentiator (Appendix D, fair-comparison codebase):

| Setting                       | GPU memory | Training speed |
|-------------------------------|------------|----------------|
| MeanFlow + math attention     | 51.45 GB   | 2.39 it/s      |
| SoFlow + math attention       | 38.95 GB   | 2.84 it/s      |
| SoFlow + memory-efficient attn| **35.44 GB** | **2.94 it/s** |

Same DiT-B/2 backbone, same batch, same codebase — only loss differs. SoFlow wins because (a) no JVP means no extra forward-mode-AD graph in memory, (b) memory-efficient attention is usable. For a 4090 with 24 GB this matters at typical batches.

# How this translates to raw audio

- **The whole training pipeline is dimension-agnostic.** Replace 2-D patch tokens with 1-D conv / 1-D transformer tokens on raw samples. Losses, schedules, weighting, and CFG mechanics are unchanged.
- **The JVP-free property is the actual reason to use SoFlow over iMF for an audio replication.** At 22.05 kHz × 1 s ≈ 22 k samples, even after a factor-300 downsample (≈ 70 tokens) the Transformer's attention dominates memory; on 4090 with 24 GB, the ability to use `F.scaled_dot_product_attention` with the flash backend matters. With longer clips (≥ 5 s) or higher SR (44.1 kHz stereo), this is the difference between fitting a workable batch and not.
- **The bi-time input is trivial on 1-D.** Two scalars (`t`, `s`), embed `s − t` with a sinusoidal positional encoding and add or concat to the time embedding the model already needs. Works for FiLM-conditioning (1-D U-Net) just as well as for token-prepended conditioning (1-D DiT).
- **Linear-Euler parameterisation pairs naturally with the linear (rectified-flow) noising used by everything else in this wiki.** No extra design choice needed.
- **CFG-during-training is structurally identical to iMF's flexible-CFG conditioning** in *what it buys* (no inference-time guidance pass), but **mechanically different**: iMF conditions on `ω` as an input so `ω` is sweepable at inference; SoFlow bakes a fixed `w` into the trained network. For audio research where we want to sweep CFG strength, iMF's design is more flexible; SoFlow's is simpler.
- **`l → t` schedule is the one extra hyperparameter we'd need to tune.** Exponential 1/10 → 1/500 across training works for ImageNet; analogous to consistency models' time-step schedule.
- **Phase / waveform fidelity.** Same caveat as iMF: the loss is plain MSE on raw samples. Phase issues at 50 NFE remain at 1 NFE — one-step doesn't introduce *new* phase artefacts, it just doesn't fix the ones the FM target already has.
- **Distillation-free + GAN-free + JVP-free is a useful combination.** Single training run, ordinary autograd, no compiled JVP issues, no flash-attention conflict. Lowest-friction one-step recipe to actually replicate in PyTorch.

# Strengths

- **No JVP** → memory-efficient attention works → ~31% less GPU memory and ~23% more throughput vs MeanFlow on identical DiT. On a 24 GB consumer GPU this is the deciding factor.
- From-scratch, no teacher, no GAN, no auxiliary network (no v-head as in iMF) — purely one network, two losses.
- Beats MeanFlow at every scale tested, at matched epochs.
- 2-NFE is meaningfully better than 1-NFE — graceful trade-off if quality matters more than latency.
- CFG is baked in at training time, so 1-NFE inference is truly one network call.

# Limitations (project-relevant)

- **Does not beat iMF.** SoFlow XL/2 = 2.96 FID @ 1-NFE, iMF XL/2 = 1.72 FID. The "no JVP" advantage is about *training cost*, not generation quality. If GPU memory isn't tight, iMF wins on FID.
- **The two methods are not directly comparable**: iMF uses a different architecture (in-context conditioning, ~610 M params, 89-M B/2), SoFlow uses vanilla DiT (676 M XL/2, 131 M B/2). Whether iMF's quality edge would survive a SoFlow-style "minimal-changes-to-DiT" reproduction is open.
- Inference-time CFG strength is **fixed at training time** (unlike iMF). To sweep CFG, you'd retrain.
- Three new hyperparameters: `λ` (FM share), `m` (velocity mix), `r_init`/`r_end` (l-schedule). Only `λ` is highly sensitive.
- Bounded only above for the consistency loss residual; the Taylor approximation requires `l → t` for the bound to tighten. Convergence guarantee is `O(√δ)` where `δ` includes the schedule slack — looser than iMF's clean v-loss form.
- All headline numbers are on ImageNet latents from SD-VAE. CIFAR-10 pixel-space result (2.86 FID) is encouraging but small-resolution.
- No latency / wall-time numbers (standard image-paper omission). Training-efficiency numbers in Tab. 5 are the only wall-time signal.

# Connection to other ingested papers

- **MeanFlow (Geng et al. 2025, NeurIPS).** SoFlow's stated baseline at every scale; SoFlow beats it. The JVP-free objective is the headline difference.
- **iMF (2512.02012).** Concurrent. Not cited by SoFlow. Same family (from-scratch fastforward), different angle:
  - iMF *keeps* the JVP but reformulates the regression target so it is network-independent, and conditions on `ω`.
  - SoFlow *removes* the JVP entirely by replacing the MeanFlow identity with a Taylor-finite-difference consistency identity, and bakes `w` into training.
  - **iMF wins on FID** (1.72 vs 2.96), **SoFlow wins on training compute** (no JVP → flash attention works).
  - On a 4090 with a small 1-D audio backbone where memory isn't the binding constraint, iMF is the better recipe for quality. When pushing batch size / sequence length to the GPU's limit, SoFlow wins.
- **Drifting (2602.04770).** Also from-scratch 1-NFE, but takes the kernel-mean-shift-in-φ-space approach — totally different mathematical route. Drifting 1-NFE FID on ImageNet pixel = 1.61, latent = 1.54. So in the from-scratch 1-NFE leaderboard: **Drifting ≈ iMF (~1.5–1.7) > SoFlow (2.96) > MeanFlow (3.43)**.
- **FD-loss (2604.28190).** Post-training trick, stackable on top of any 1-NFE base. SoFlow + FD-loss is a natural pipeline (same as iMF + FD-loss).
- **CRASH (2106.07431).** Provides the 1-D U-Net template we'd train SoFlow on for a first audio replication. SoFlow's CIFAR-10 result is on a U-Net, so the recipe works outside DiT.

# Open questions for the repo

1. **Does SoFlow's training-efficiency advantage survive on 1-D backbones?** The JVP+flash-attention conflict is a Transformer-specific story. On a 1-D U-Net (CRASH-style), `torch.func.jvp` works without conflicting with anything — so iMF's training-cost penalty there may be much smaller. The SoFlow vs iMF tradeoff might invert.
2. **Combining SoFlow's JVP-free SCM loss with iMF's flexible CFG conditioning** — is there a hybrid that gets iMF's quality without the JVP? Conceptually: take SoFlow's `f_θ(x_t, t, s)` plus condition on `ω` à la iMF.
3. **CIFAR-10 pixel result (2.86 FID) is the only non-latent number in the paper.** On 32×32 raw images, a U-Net trains SoFlow successfully at one NFE. This is the closest published evidence that the recipe works on small, raw, non-tokenised inputs. For raw 1-D audio of comparable dimensionality (say 1024-sample clips at 16 kHz), is a small SoFlow trainable on the 4090?
4. **`l → t` schedule sensitivity** at our scale — does the exponential schedule transfer, or does training-on-tiny-data need a different schedule?
5. **CPU forward-pass cost of SoFlow-B/4 (131 M params, 5.6 Gflops/sample) on 1-D inputs.** Same blanket question as for every method in the wiki.
6. **Does CFG-baked-in (SoFlow) vs CFG-conditioning (iMF) matter for audio research workflow?** We typically *want* to sweep guidance at inference for ablation reasons; iMF's design supports this for free, SoFlow doesn't.
