## TL;DR (project-relevant)

ComVo is a **fully complex-valued (CVNN) iSTFT GAN vocoder** — the entire generator and the multi-resolution discriminator process the complex spectrogram with native complex arithmetic instead of the standard "stack real/imag as separate real channels" trick used by Vocos / RFWave / Flow2GAN. Mel-conditioned, from-scratch GAN (no FM, no diffusion, no consistency), so structurally **1-NFE by construction**. Architecturally it is a **direct Vocos descendant** with the conv/norm/activation/discriminator stack swapped to complex-valued versions, plus two new tricks: **phase quantization** (a structured complex nonlinearity that discretises phase angles, STE-trained) as the load-bearing perceptual-quality lever, and a **block-matrix realisation** of CVNN ops that cuts training time by 25 % without changing the model. **GPU xRT 819 at batch 1** (between Vocos 4658 and HiFi-GAN 259), 13.28 M params; **no CPU benchmark anywhere in the paper** — leaves the headline ComVo-on-CPU number open. The complex-valued architecture is the audio architectural story; phase quantization is portable to any STFT-domain head; CVNN cost roughly doubles per-parameter memory (`G_RD_R` 51.6 MB vs `G_CD_R` 101.2 MB at same params).

## Why this matters here

Three slots in this project's design space:

1. **Architecture sub-family.** Adds a third sub-variant under STFT-domain ConvNeXt: **(a)** single-branch + freq-axis multi-band batching (RFWave), **(b)** multi-branch at different (N-FFT, hop) (Flow2GAN), **(c)** single-branch complex-valued (ComVo). All three operate at hop-rate, all three iSTFT-read-out. ComVo's pitch is that the standard "treat real/imag as separate channels" baked into (a) and (b) discards algebraic structure.
2. **Phase quantization is portable.** It's a complex-valued nonlinearity: `z_q = |z| · e^{i · (2π/N_q) · round(N_q θ / 2π)}` with N_q ≈ 128 quantization levels and an STE through the round. The paper validates it under their CVNN backbone but the operation itself is architecture-agnostic — it's a regulariser on the phase that any STFT-domain head can apply.
3. **From-scratch GAN benchmark with the same backbone family as Flow2GAN/Vocos.** ComVo is HiFi-GAN-style **MPD + complex MRD + feature-matching + mel-L1**, no FM pretrain, no consistency stage. Comparable to HiFi-GAN's place in the methods.md "from-scratch GAN" cell, not to Flow2GAN's "FM-pretrain + GAN finetune" cell. Both ComVo and Flow2GAN ship 1-NFE STFT-domain ConvNeXt vocoders; the recipe difference is "FM warm-start + GAN finetune" (Flow2GAN) vs "pure GAN from scratch" (ComVo).

## Method

### Generator (Fig. 2a)

Vocos architecture verbatim, but with every layer made complex:

```
Mel-spectrogram (initialise imag = 0, treat as complex)
  → Complex Conv1d
  → Phase Quantization layer
  → N × Complex ConvNeXt block:
       Complex Depthwise Conv
       Complex LayerNorm
       Complex Pointwise Conv (expand)
       Complex GELU (split-GELU; the "split" branch)
       Complex Pointwise Conv (contract)
       residual add
  → Complex Head
  → iSTFT  → audio
```

CVNN building blocks (Appx. A — they reuse the standard Trabelsi 2018 / Voigtlaender 2023 toolkit):

- **Complex Conv:** real filter `h = a + ib`, `z' = (x*a − y*b) + i(x*b + y*a)` on input `z = x + iy`. Four real convs per complex one; the block-matrix scheme below fuses them.
- **Complex activation:** explicit choice is **split-GELU** (apply GELU separately to real and imag) for backbone compatibility; the appendix discusses the more principled magnitude-only `f(z) = f_Mag(|z|) · e^{iθ}` but it's not used inside the ConvNeXt block.
- **Complex LayerNorm:** centre + decorrelate by `Σ^{-1/2}` with the full 2×2 (real/imag) covariance, then learnable complex affine `γz + β`. Captures real-imag cross-covariance that split-channel norms miss.
- **CVNN per-parameter memory ≈ 2× RVNN** at matched width — every weight stores a (real, imag) pair. The paper's apples-to-apples comparison fixes either parameter count (then RVNN has half the memory) or memory (then RVNN has 2× the params); both ablations are reported.

### Phase quantization (§4.3, Eq. 1)

For a complex feature `z = r·e^{iθ}` with `θ ∈ (−π, π]`:

```
θ_q = (2π / N_q) · round(N_q · θ / (2π))
z_q = r · e^{i·θ_q}
```

Applied **once after the initial complex Conv1d** (not inside every ConvNeXt block). Discretises phase angles to `N_q` levels, leaving magnitude untouched. STE on the backward pass — `dθ_q/dθ = 1` — so gradients flow through. Acts as a regulariser on the phase representation: limits "unwarranted phase variability in intermediate representations" and pushes the network toward coherent phase patterns.

Ablation (Tab. 6):

| N_q | UTMOS | MR-STFT | PESQ | Periodicity | V/UV F1 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 (off) | 3.6646 | **0.8435** | 3.7756 | 0.0915 | **0.9625** |
| **128** | **3.6901** | 0.8439 | 3.8239 | **0.0903** | 0.9609 |
| 256 | 3.6423 | 0.8466 | 3.8127 | 0.0926 | 0.9597 |
| 512 | 3.6412 | 0.8489 | **3.8248** | 0.0896 | 0.9613 |

Best perceptual scores at moderate quantization (`N_q = 128`); slight MR-STFT reconstruction cost (0.8435 → 0.8439). Finer levels diminish returns. **The strongest single ablation in the paper for `N_q = 128` is `+0.026 UTMOS / +0.05 PESQ` over no phase quantization** — modest but consistent.

### Complex multi-resolution discriminator (cMRD, §4.2, Fig. 2c)

Vocos-style multi-resolution STFT discriminator with **complex Conv2d + complex LeakyReLU**, three resolutions (sub-discriminators i, ii, iii). Hinge loss applied **independently to the real and imaginary parts** (Eq. 15): each sub-discriminator emits a complex output, `[D]_R` and `[D]_I` are separately compared to 1 / 0 with hinge losses, sum is the cMRD loss. **MPD branch is kept real-valued and unchanged from HiFi-GAN** — MPD operates on time-domain waveform segments where there's no meaningful complex structure to preserve.

The Grad-CAM analysis (Fig. 3) is the most informative qualitative result: **with real-valued MRD, attention maps are diffuse and poorly aligned with speech-relevant spectral structure; with cMRD, attention concentrates on harmonics and onsets across all three sub-discriminators.** Replacing only the discriminator (`G_R D_R` → `G_R D_C`) already moves MR-STFT 0.8856 → 0.8679 and PESQ 3.6266 → 3.6399 (Tab. 5); replacing only the generator (`G_R D_R` → `G_C D_R`) gets the bigger win. Both ⇒ `G_C D_C` is the strongest.

### Training objective (Appx. C)

Standard HiFi-GAN-style assembly with hinges and feature matching:

- **MPD loss (Eq. 14):** hinge on real outputs, summed over K period-discriminators.
- **cMRD loss (Eq. 15):** hinge applied independently to real and imag outputs of each complex sub-discriminator. Generator hinge (Eq. 18) symmetrically `½ · (hinge_R + hinge_I)`.
- **Mel L1 loss (Eq. 16)** on log-Mel spectrograms — same as HiFi-GAN.
- **Feature matching** L1 on intermediate discriminator features for both MPD and cMRD branches (Eq. 19+).

No phase loss (unlike WaveFM), no spectral-energy-inverse weighting (unlike Flow2GAN), no consistency, no FM. Purely from-scratch adversarial.

### Block-matrix computation scheme (§4.4)

Implementation trick orthogonal to the model. Standard CVNN layers track real/imag as separate tensors and do four real multiplies per complex one:

```
[Re(z')]   [W_r  -W_i] [x]
[Im(z')] = [W_i   W_r] [y]
```

PyTorch's default `complex64` autograd builds four nodes per layer; the block-matrix formulation channel-concatenates real/imag and does **one** matmul against the structured 2×2 block matrix. Custom autograd reuses the same block structure on the backward (Eq. 4). Result (Tab. 7): MR-STFT identical (0.8465 → 0.8435), training time **183 h → 138 h (−25 %)**, generator backward-graph nodes 5686 → 2547 (−55 %), cMRD nodes 4248 → 1404 (−67 %). Slight GPU xRT *regression* at inference (702.26 → 696.91) because PyTorch's `complex64` kernel is itself well-optimised; the win is purely training-time graph complexity.

## Results

### Setup

- **Data:** LibriTTS train-{clean-100, clean-360, other-500}, eval on test-{clean, other}. OOD: MUSDB18-HQ.
- **Audio:** 24 kHz, STFT FFT=1024 / hop=256 / Hann-1024, **100 mel bins**, max freq 12 kHz.
- **Baselines:** HiFi-GAN v1, iSTFTNet, BigVGAN-base, Vocos.
- **TTS pipeline:** Matcha-TTS acoustic model → each vocoder, evaluated independently.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| HiFi-GAN | 3.3453 | 1.0455 | 2.9360 | 0.1554 | 0.9174 | 4.00 | −0.09 |

**ComVo wins every objective metric**, MOS ties at 4.07 (statistical equivalence to GT 4.08 within error). MUSDB18-HQ OOD (Tab. 3+4): also wins objectives, ties top on average SMOS.

### Cost (Tab. 9)

|---|---|---|---|
| HiFi-GAN | 14.00 | 53.40 | 259.08 |

**ComVo is ~5.7× slower than Vocos on GPU at matched parameter count**, but ~2× memory because every weight is a real-imag pair. **CPU benchmark is not reported.** The 13.28 M params vs Vocos 13.54 M is a like-for-like comparison; the inference-time cost is dominated by the CVNN forward replacing the real-channel-concat forward.

### Complex-vs-real at matched memory (Tab. 10)

| Model | Params (M) | Memory (MB) | UTMOS↑ | MR-STFT↓ | PESQ↑ |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| G_R D_R | 13.54 | 51.62 | 3.6025 | 0.8856 | 3.6266 |
| G_R D_R 2× | 27.05 | 103.19 | 3.6164 | 0.8622 | 3.6336 |
| **G_C D_R** | 13.28 | 101.24 | **3.6452** | **0.8597** | **3.7375** |

The doubled-RVNN ablation pins down that **the win is from complex-domain modelling, not from the doubled per-parameter memory**: at matched memory budget, `G_C D_R` beats `G_R D_R 2×` (UTMOS 3.6452 vs 3.6164, PESQ 3.7375 vs 3.6336) at half the parameter count.

### TTS pipeline (Tab. 8)

Matcha-TTS → vocoder: ComVo UTMOS 3.4403, MOS 3.92 — highest UTMOS, tied-top MOS. Matters because the *deployed* setting (acoustic-model mel → vocoder) is not the same as the *ground-truth-mel → vocoder* setting; ComVo's quality margin survives.

## Project-relevant takeaways

- **Third sub-variant in the STFT-domain ConvNeXt family.** RFWave (freq-axis subband batching), Flow2GAN (multi-resolution branches), ComVo (complex-valued layers) are three orthogonal axes for "what do you do at the same hop-rate sequence length?" — they should compose. None of the three has been tested in combination.
- **Phase quantization is a portable trick.** Architecturally a one-liner (`z_q = |z| · e^{i · (2π/N_q) · round(N_q θ / 2π)}` with STE), it can be applied at the input or output of any STFT-domain head — including Flow2GAN's multi-branch one and RFWave's subband-batched one — without touching the rest of the network. Same-paper ablation says `N_q ≈ 128` is the perceptual sweet spot. **The mechanism (regularise phase, prevent drift) should be tested on any STFT-domain method this project ports.**
- **CVNN-everywhere has a CPU cost question the paper does not answer.** GPU xRT 819 vs Vocos 4658 is ~5.7× slower at matched params; on CPU the gap is likely worse because complex-valued kernels are less optimised in CPU BLAS than `float32`. **The headline ComVo-on-CPU number is the empty cell of this paper.** Until it's measured, ComVo's project-relevance is "an architectural lesson + a portable trick", not "a CPU-fast deployment recipe".
- **From-scratch GAN cleanly beats Vocos at matched params + matched recipe**, no FM pretrain, no consistency stage. This is the Vocos competitor in the from-scratch-GAN cell of methods.md (alongside HiFi-GAN and R3GAN). The recipe is `MPD + cMRD + feature-matching + mel-L1` with default hinge; no exotic post-training. **For project purposes: the gap between ComVo and Flow2GAN is largely the "from-scratch GAN" vs "FM-pretrain + GAN-finetune" gap at matched-ish CPU-cost-class architectures, plus the complex-vs-real-channel architecture difference, plus the model-size difference (13.28 M vs 78.9 M).**
- **The architecture-axis vs recipe-axis decomposition is now tighter.** ComVo answers "do you need FM pretrain to beat Vocos with GAN-only at matched size?" — no, you don't; complex-valued layers + phase quantization get you there. Flow2GAN answers "if you scale up params 6× and add FM pretrain + GAN finetune, do you beat ComVo?" — likely yes, but at substantial cost.
- **CVNN backbone audio is now mature enough to revisit at the unconditional-generation question.** ComVo, like every published STFT-domain method, is a conditional vocoder. The CVNN-vs-RVNN advantage (Tab. 10) is largest exactly when the data is *inherently complex-valued* — i.e., when the network has to learn coherent phase. Unconditional generation has no mel to lock phase in from, so the **complex-vs-real gap may be even larger in the unconditional regime**. Untested.

## Limitations / open questions

- **No CPU benchmark.** Headline gap. Until measured, the architectural argument is incomplete for this project.
- **Phase quantization sensitivity to backbone.** Validated only inside ComVo's CVNN ConvNeXt. Whether it ports unchanged to Flow2GAN's multi-branch RVNN (treating phase from `atan2(imag, real)` on a real-channel-stacked complex spectrogram) is untested.
- **Loss design is conservatively split.** Authors flag in §6 that hinges are applied component-wise (real and imag separately) on the cMRD outputs and that split-GELU is used inside the ConvNeXt block — both are "keep RVNN compatibility" choices, not principled complex-domain losses / activations. Magnitude-only activation `f(z) = f_Mag(|z|) · e^{iθ}` from Appx. A is the obvious upgrade, untested. A genuinely complex-domain hinge / Wasserstein loss on the cMRD output is untested.
- **No diffusion / flow-matching version.** The paper conclusion explicitly flags "extend this framework beyond adversarial training to other generative paradigms (e.g., diffusion or flow-matching)" as future work. **The empty cell that ComVo creates: CVNN-everywhere + FM-pretrain + GAN-finetune (Flow2GAN's recipe with ComVo's backbone).**
- **Multi-GPU training under-optimised.** Authors report DDP under-performs on complex parameters in their current PyTorch stack, so they report single-GPU only. Practical scaling story is open.
- **Training instability with finer quantization.** Tab. 6: `N_q = 256` and `512` slightly degrade UTMOS / V/UV — diminishing returns past 128, consistent with their framing of phase quantization as a regulariser.
