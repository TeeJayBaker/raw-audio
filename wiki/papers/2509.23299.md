# MeanFlowSE — MeanFlow applied to speech enhancement in SSL-feature space

## TL;DR (project-relevant)

**The first published MeanFlow application to audio, alongside [SplitMeanFlow (2507.16884)](2507.16884.md). Operates on neither raw waveform nor a VAE latent of the waveform alone — it sits in a *third* representation space: a frozen SSL feature space (WavLM-Large) used as the conditioning signal, with a separately-frozen WaveVAE as the actual generative latent.** Concretely: WavLM (frozen, 24-layer weighted-sum fusion) extracts an acoustic-semantic representation `z_y` from the noisy waveform `y`; a small DiT (8 layers, 8 heads, hidden 512, FFN 2048, **40.7 M trainable params**) is trained with the original Geng MeanFlow objective (autograd JVP, log-normal `(r, t)` sampling, adaptive ℓ² loss, flow ratio 0.25) to predict the average-velocity field that refines Gaussian noise `ε` into the clean WaveVAE latent `z_x = Enc(x)`; the frozen WaveVAE decoder maps `z_0 = ε − û(ε, 0, 1)` back to a 16 kHz waveform `x̂ = Dec(z_0)`. **RTF 0.013 on a 4090 GPU at 1 NFE — the lowest in the table, including discriminative baselines** — beats SELM (G, 0.042), FlowSE (G, 0.121), AnyEnhance (G, 1.423), LLaSE-G1 (G, 0.057), even FRCRN (D, 0.024) and MP-SENet (D, 0.019). DNSMOS ties or marginally beats FlowSE/AnyEnhance/LLaSE-G1; WER 8.5 % is the lowest among generative baselines. **The recipe is conditional (SE), not unconditional gen**, and lives in a frozen-SSL + frozen-VAE pipeline that is the precise opposite of this project's "no codec, no intermediate representation" stance. But it's the closest existing audio MeanFlow application to study before any raw-waveform unconditional MeanFlow attempt — and it makes the **representation-space choice the headline architectural decision** of any audio MeanFlow port.

## Where this lands in the audio-MeanFlow landscape

Two MeanFlow audio applications now exist:

| Paper | Domain | Operating space | NFE | Training | Hardware report | Conditional? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **SplitMeanFlow (2507.16884)** | TTS (speech) | Latent (Seed-TTS acoustic-diffusion features) | 1–2 | FM teacher → SplitMeanFlow distill (JVP-free, algebraic identity) | Wall-clock vs FM teacher only; deployed in Doubao TTS | Yes (text → speech) |
| **MeanFlowSE (this)** | Speech enhancement | **SSL conditioning + VAE latent** (WavLM-Large + KALL-E WaveVAE, both frozen) | 1 | From scratch on DNS Challenge, original MeanFlow objective with JVP | RTF 0.013 on 4090; no CPU | Yes (noisy → clean speech) |

**Zero raw-waveform unconditional audio MeanFlow exists.** Both audio applications are conditional, both go through some kind of frozen intermediate representation, neither reports CPU. The "what's the project's contribution" question is now sharply defined: raw-waveform, unconditional, CPU-benchmarked is the empty cell.

## The representation-space choice is the headline design decision

The paper's main ablation is **SSL conditioning vs VAE conditioning** (Tab 1, Tab 2). Replacing the WavLM-noisy-feature condition `z_y` with a noisy-waveform VAE latent (same VAE used on the clean side) **drops OVRL DNSMOS from 3.368 → 2.727 (With Reverb)** and **WER from 8.5 % → 18.4 %** — a 2× WER degradation at the same RTF / same params. Their stated reason: "SSL latents capture long-range dependencies and phonetic content"; the VAE encoder's noisy-input embeddings are "irregular and noisy". This is a strong empirical claim that **SSL features and VAE latents are not interchangeable for MeanFlow conditioning** — they encode different things, and SSL beats VAE specifically for the speech-enhancement task where phonetic preservation matters.

For raw-waveform unconditional generation, this ablation has no direct mirror — but it makes the broader point that **the choice of operating space is not just a compute knob**, it's a quality knob. The MeanFlow loss does what the loss does in whatever space you put it; the space defines what's easy and what's lost. Pixel-space (image), VAE-latent (image, T2A), SSL-feature-conditioned + VAE-latent (this paper), raw waveform (the project's target) are four different commitments, with different implicit priors over what counts as a "natural" sample to generate.

## Method specifics — what's actually new vs vanilla MeanFlow

Almost nothing is new at the *MeanFlow* level. The paper uses:

- Geng MeanFlow's original loss form with autograd JVP, adaptive ℓ² weighting (`w = (δ² + c)^{-(1-γ)}`, `γ = 0.5`, `c = 10⁻³`).
- Log-normal `(r, t)` sampling (`µ = −0.4, σ = 1.0`).
- Flow ratio 0.25 (i.e. 25 % of samples with `r = t` boundary; **not** Geng's 75 % — note this is the opposite-of-Geng setting and matches AlphaFlow's preferred 25 %, though AlphaFlow isn't cited).
- Standard DiT backbone (8 layers, 8 heads, hidden 512, FFN 2048, 40.7 M params).
- AdaLN conditioning with `TE(r) + TE(t)` summed.

What's specific to the audio application:

1. **WavLM-Large as the conditioning extractor** (frozen, 24-layer trainable weighted-sum fusion with softmax normalisation). Features form `z_y` which is concatenated with the noisy DiT input.
2. **KALL-E WaveVAE as the generative latent** (frozen, 16 kHz waveform → 256-d latent at 25 Hz). The DiT's job is to refine Gaussian noise `ε` into the *clean-speech* VAE latent `z_x`, conditioned on the noisy-speech *SSL* features `z_y`.
3. **One-step inference identity**: `z_0 = ε − û(ε, 0, 1)`, where `ε` is sampled Gaussian noise (not the noisy speech latent). The noisy speech only enters via conditioning, not as the initial state of the flow.

The (3) is structurally important: this is **not** a flow-from-noisy-to-clean (which would be the natural FM-for-SE setup, as in FlowSE [14] cited here). It's a flow-from-Gaussian-noise-to-clean-VAE-latent **conditioned on** the noisy-SSL-feature. The conditional MeanFlow generates the clean latent from noise; the noisy input is only consulted, not transformed. **This decouples the source distribution from the conditioning distribution** — a relevant point if we ever build a conditional 1-step audio system, but irrelevant for unconditional generation where the source distribution is just Gaussian noise.

## Results

**Training:** Interspeech 2020 DNS Challenge dataset, 4 s segments, 16 kHz, SNR `U(−10, 20)` dB, 200 epochs, AdamW lr=1e-3 exp-decay 0.99/epoch, 8×4090.

**DNSMOS on DNS Challenge blind test (With Reverb / Without Reverb / Real Recording, OVRL ↑):**

- Best generative: MeanFlowSE 3.368 / 3.438 / 3.298 (tied or top in BAK, top OVRL on With Reverb and Real).
- FlowSE (G, 40-NFE FM in the same authors' prior work): 3.340 / 3.451 / 3.271 — MeanFlowSE matches at 1 NFE.
- MeanFlowSE_FM(100) (same model trained at flow-ratio = 0 → standard FM, 100 NFE inference): 3.420 / 3.475 / 3.337 — *better* than MeanFlow but 100× the latency.
- MeanFlowSE_FM(40): 3.346 / 3.439 / 3.273 — tied with MeanFlow at 40 NFE.

**Tab 2 — the headline:**

| System | Type | WER (%) ↓ | RTF ↓ | Params (M) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| MeanFlowSE | G | **8.5** | **0.013** | **40.7** |
| MeanFlowSE_VAE-input (ablation) | G | 18.4 | 0.013 | 40.7 |
| MeanFlowSE_FM(40) | G | 8.8 | 0.042 | 40.7 |
| MeanFlowSE_FM(100) | G | 7.7 | 0.086 | 40.7 |
| SELM (LM-based) | G | 22.2 | 0.042 | 219.0 |
| AnyEnhance (masked transformer) | G | 13.0 | 1.423 | 324.0 |
| FlowSE | G | 12.4 | 0.121 | 337.1 |
| LLaSE-G1 (LLaMA-based) | G | 14.6 | 0.057 | 1072.9 |
| FRCRN (discriminative) | D | 5.9 | 0.024 | 13.1 |
| MP-SENet (discriminative) | D | 9.3 | 0.019 | 2.1 |

Notes on this table:

- **RTF 0.013 is GPU-only.** On a 4090. The paper does not report CPU. There is no parameter-counting consistency on the "G" baselines: many of them are large LMs (LLaSE-G1 at 1.07 B, AnyEnhance at 324 M, FlowSE at 337 M) so the RTF comparison is partly a model-size comparison. The 40.7 M MeanFlowSE is **the smallest generative model** in the table by an order of magnitude; the RTF gap to FlowSE (40.7 M MeanFlow vs 337 M FM) is **8× model size and ~10× NFE collapsed**.
- **The 100-NFE FM trained on the same backbone has lower WER (7.7) and higher DNSMOS than 1-NFE MeanFlow.** The 1-step recipe trades ~10 % WER and ~2 % DNSMOS for 6.6× speedup (0.086 → 0.013 RTF). This is the standard 1-step-vs-multi-step trade-off and the same trade SplitMeanFlow makes on TTS.
- **FRCRN (discriminative, 13.1 M) at WER 5.9 % is still ahead of MeanFlowSE at 8.5 %** — the generative-models-win-on-quality, discriminative-models-win-on-WER pattern remains. The paper's argument is that DNSMOS (perceptual) matters more for end-user listening; for downstream ASR robustness, MP-SENet at WER 9.3 / OVRL 3.374 (Without Reverb) is competitive without any generation machinery.

## Strengths

- **Demonstrates MeanFlow training stability on audio at small scale** (40.7 M params, 200 epochs, 8×4090). This is the smallest published MeanFlow audio model. SplitMeanFlow doesn't report parameter count or scale.
- **The SSL-conditioning ablation is clean and decisive** — SSL beats VAE-noisy-input for conditioning a MeanFlow SE system by 2× WER. The structural argument (SSL features are denoise-invariant phonetic representations; noisy-VAE-latents inherit the noise) is plausible and matches WavLM's design intent.
- **Smallest generative SE model in the comparison.** 40.7 M params with WER 8.5 % is parameter-efficient relative to FlowSE (337 M, WER 12.4 %), LLaSE-G1 (1072.9 M, WER 14.6 %), AnyEnhance (324 M, WER 13.0 %). The parameter efficiency comes from the SSL conditioning doing the heavy lifting (WavLM-Large is ~316 M frozen params doing the phonetic abstraction; the DiT just refines).
- **First clear empirical evidence that the SSL-features-as-conditioning route works for a one-step generative audio task.** Adds an answer point for the "what audio representation does MeanFlow want?" question.

## Limitations / open questions

- **No CPU benchmark.** This is the headline gap. RTF 0.013 on a 4090 says nothing about whether the SSL-feature pipeline (WavLM-Large forward pass on every 4 s clip) is feasible on CPU. WavLM-Large is **316 M frozen params** dominated by 24-layer transformer over 16 kHz waveform; the SSL forward at CPU is likely the bottleneck, not the 40.7 M DiT. The paper's "compact 40.7 M model" framing is misleading — the total compute graph at inference is `WavLM-Large(316M) + DiT(40.7M) + WaveVAE-decoder(small)`. CPU RTF of just the WavLM forward is the missing number.
- **Frozen-SSL + frozen-VAE pipeline.** Three independent training stages exist before the DiT trains: WavLM pretrain (on speech), KALL-E WaveVAE pretrain (on 16 kHz speech), then the DiT MeanFlow stage. **The full pipeline has three codec/representation floors** — WavLM's representation quality, WaveVAE's reconstruction quality, and the noise level. The MeanFlow stage can never exceed any of these floors. This is the worst-case form of the "intermediate representation" critique that motivates raw-waveform — and a clean argument that any "MeanFlow on audio" needs a representation-space ablation, not just an architectural one.
- **Conditional only.** No unconditional generation. The result that "MeanFlow can be trained at 40.7 M on speech-SE" doesn't transfer directly to "MeanFlow can be trained at 40.7 M on unconditional 1-D waveform" — the conditioning signal does substantial lifting, and the conditional one-step task is empirically easier than the unconditional one (the noisy input bounds the search space).
- **No comparison against SplitMeanFlow.** Both are 2025 papers; both apply MeanFlow to speech audio; both report 1-NFE results. The natural head-to-head — SplitMeanFlow (JVP-free) vs MeanFlowSE (JVP) on the same speech task — is missing. MeanFlowSE uses Geng's autograd JVP; SplitMeanFlow eliminates the JVP via algebraic identity. The "does the JVP matter for audio training quality / throughput?" question is unanswered.
- **Flow ratio 0.25, not 0.75.** The paper sets `r = t` for 25 % of training samples (the "flow ratio of 0.25" in §3.2). This is the **opposite** of Geng's reported 75 % heuristic for MeanFlow training and matches AlphaFlow's preferred regime — but AlphaFlow (2510.20771) is not cited and there's no ablation on the flow-ratio choice. Either the speech-SE task has different optimal `r = t` fraction or the authors picked this without sweeping. Open question.
- **No 1-NFE / 2-NFE / 4-NFE comparison on the *MeanFlow* objective.** The Tab 2 multi-step variants train a separate model at flow-ratio = 0 (standard FM) and run it at 40 / 100 NFE; the paper doesn't run the trained MeanFlow model at 2 or 4 inference steps (which would require multi-step `(r, t)` decomposition). The strict 1-NFE-vs-few-NFE trade-off on the same model is uncharacterised.
- **Untested on unseen low-SNR or non-speech (music, ambient) audio.** The training distribution is DNS Challenge speech + simulated noise; the SSL-conditioning argument leans on phonetic content (WavLM is speech-pretrained). For instrument or non-speech enhancement, this pipeline is undefined.

## Translation to raw-waveform unconditional MeanFlow

The paper does not directly help any raw-waveform attempt — it's the prior art that *defines what raw-waveform MeanFlow must differentiate against*, not a transferable recipe. Three reads:

1. **The "frozen SSL + frozen VAE + small DiT" pipeline is a different paradigm from raw-waveform.** It commits to *both* a representation-encoder pretraining stage *and* a waveform-VAE pretraining stage before MeanFlow training begins. Raw-waveform MeanFlow has neither. The paper's RTF 0.013 advantage comes substantially from those frozen stages doing the abstraction work; an unconditional raw-waveform MeanFlow at 40.7 M params on 1 s @ 22 kHz would have to do that work inside the 40.7 M backbone — quality and CPU cost both expected to be substantially worse.
2. **MeanFlow trains at small audio-domain scale.** 40.7 M params, 200 epochs, 8×4090. This is the smallest published MeanFlow at any modality (image papers all use 600 M+). The fact that training doesn't collapse at this scale on the (frozen-SSL-conditioned) speech-SE task is a positive data point for "MeanFlow doesn't strictly need huge models" — but the conditioning may be carrying the stability.
3. **The SSL-vs-VAE conditioning ablation is the right *type* of experiment to run for raw-waveform.** Specifically: for any raw-waveform conditional generation task we end up exploring (e.g. mel→wave, music-stem→wave, drum-class→wave), the question "what's the conditioning signal — a frozen SSL feature, a mel, a learned audio-φ, or raw inputs?" is now an established empirical axis. Match the analogous ablation in our setup.

## Composition surface

- **AlphaFlow curriculum + MeanFlowSE pipeline** — the α-schedule from FM to MeanFlow could replace the fixed-flow-ratio = 0.25 training with a curriculum from FM (flow-ratio 1.0, i.e. always `r = t`) down to MeanFlow (flow-ratio = 0, never `r = t`). Untested. AlphaFlow's claim is that this gives 15 % relative 1-NFE FID improvement on vanilla DiT; whether it also helps SE WER is open.
- **SplitMeanFlow loss + MeanFlowSE pipeline** — swap the JVP-based original MeanFlow loss for SplitMeanFlow's algebraic identity. Should be loss-equivalent in the limit `s → t` and JVP-free, enabling FlashAttention / FSDP / custom-accelerator deployment (which the paper would actually benefit from given the 4090-GPU-only RTF reporting). The Doubao TTS pipeline is precedent for SplitMeanFlow on speech.
- **TiM DDE + MeanFlowSE pipeline** — replace the autograd JVP with TiM's central finite difference (forward-only, 2 extra forwards instead of JVP). ~2× training throughput at matched quality, FlashAttn-compatible. Drop-in.
