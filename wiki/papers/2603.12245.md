# ELIT — Elastic Latent Interface Transformer

## TL;DR (project-relevant)

ELIT bolts a **variable-length set of latent tokens** onto a vanilla DiT/U-ViT/HDiT/MM-DiT via two lightweight cross-attention layers (`Read` from spatial → latent, `Write` from latent → spatial). Most transformer blocks now operate on the **latent interface** of `K` tokens, and `K` is **set at inference time** independently of the input resolution. Trained with random tail-dropping of latents, the model produces an **importance-ordered** representation: early latents capture global structure, later ones refine details. **Inference budget becomes a runtime knob**: a single ELIT checkpoint serves a continuum of quality/FLOPs points (60 budgets at 512px) without retraining, and at 25 % of the tokens it still beats the FLOPs-matched DiT baseline (ImageNet-512, FID 12.5 vs DiT 18.8). For raw-audio CPU gen this is the **first explicit "single model, many CPU budgets" recipe** in the wiki — the analogue of "give the user a quality slider that controls how many ms of inference they pay per sample, with no retraining." It's orthogonal to NFE reduction, to patch-size scaling, and to STFT-frame backbones; the Read attention also learns to **spend compute on hard regions** (transients/onsets in audio, busy textures in images) and skip easy ones (silences, sky). Caveats: at max budget ELIT is **slightly more expensive** than the equivalent DiT (the Read+Write+head+tail add overhead), and the design is for **multi-step DiT-class generators** (40-step RF here) — it does not by itself bring you to 1-NFE.

## Problem

A DiT spends FLOPs uniformly across spatial tokens, with the token count locked to image resolution × patch size. Two consequences this paper targets:

1. **Compute is pinned to input resolution.** Going from 256² to 512² with the same patch size quadruples the token count and quadruples (or worse — attention is quadratic) the per-step compute. Patch-size scaling (used by pMF, EPG) addresses this but still fixes one token count per checkpoint.
2. **Compute is spent uniformly.** Easy regions (sky, silence) get the same per-step budget as hard regions (faces, transients). The motivating experiment (Fig. 2) trains DiT-B/2 on synthetic zero-padded ImageNet-1k and shows DiT cannot reallocate compute from padded regions to real content — extra tokens that fall on zeros raise cost without helping quality.

ELIT decouples compute from resolution and lets the model learn non-uniform allocation, all within the standard rectified-flow training recipe and without any auxiliary losses.

## Method

### Latent interface

Spatial tokens `s ∈ R^{N×d}` come from standard patchification. ELIT additionally instantiates a **latent interface** `l ∈ R^{K×d}` — a learnable, variable-length set of `K` tokens. The architecture is split into three segments:

1. **Short spatial head** (`B_in` blocks). Standard DiT blocks operate on `s`; produces a refined spatial representation. Purpose: avoid pulling raw, noisy patches straight into the latent domain.
2. **Latent core** (`B_core` blocks). The big stack. A `Read` cross-attention pulls information from `s` into the latent interface; then standard transformer blocks operate **only on `l`**; then a `Write` cross-attention broadcasts updated latents back to `s`.
3. **Short spatial tail** (`B_out` blocks). Standard DiT blocks on `s`; produces the velocity prediction.

For DiT-XL/2 they use `(B_in, B_core, B_out) = (4, 20, 4)` — ~67–71 % of blocks live in the latent core, where token count is **the controllable budget** rather than the fixed `N`.

### Read / Write layers

```
Read:   l_O = l + MLP(l + CA(Q=l, K=V=s))     # l_O ∈ R^{K×d}
Write:  s_O = s + MLP(s + CA(Q=s, K=V=l_O))   # s_O ∈ R^{N×d}
```

Both use pre-norm, adaLN-Zero modulation (timestep-aware), and QK normalisation. **No hidden-dim expansion in the MLPs** to keep the added cost minimal. Single Read and single Write layer — ablations show stacking more Read layers gives no measurable gain (Tab. 6); a second Write layer or wider FFN helps but at FLOPs cost.

### Grouped cross-attention

Naive Read/Write is `O(N·K)` attention. They partition spatial tokens into `G` groups (e.g. 16 groups of `√N/4 × √N/4` patches on a 2D grid; in video, factorise across (time, space)), and latents into matching groups of `J = K/G` each. Cross-attention is **within each group only** → cost `O(N·K/G)`. The same set of `J` learnable positional embeddings is **reused across all `G` groups**, so increasing spatial resolution increases `G` and `N` together but leaves `J` (the controllable knob) intact.

Ablation (Tab. 4a, ImageNet 256px, 16² spatial tokens): `G = 16` (8×8 groups of 4 latents each) wins; `G = 1` (full cross-attention) is worse despite higher compute — coarse spatial structure helps regularise the interface. `G = N` (one latent per spatial location) underperforms — the latent interface needs to span more than one patch.

### Importance-ordered tail dropping (the multi-budget training trick)

During training, the **same prefix-keeping scheme as Nested Dropout** is applied per group. At each step, sample `J̄ ∼ Uniform{J_min, …, J_max}` once, broadcast to all groups and all GPUs, and **keep only the first `J̄` latents of each group, drop the rest** for every Read / Write / latent-core block. The first `J̄` latents in each group are forced — across many training steps — to be sufficient on their own, so the network learns to store globally-useful information in early latents and progressively-finer-detail in later ones.

At inference, choose `J̄ ≤ J` per call: more tokens → more compute → more detail. **60 distinct budgets at 512px (16 spatial groups, J_max = 60), 16 at 256px.** Same checkpoint.

**Joint > independent** (Tab. 8): a single joint multi-budget model beats independently-trained per-budget checkpoints at every budget tested (100 % / 50 % / 25 % tokens). Multi-budget training acts as a regulariser. To keep training FLOPs matched to the baseline (which is `J_max`), they **increase the batch size from 256 to 384** since average per-step compute is lower under random `J̄` sampling.

### Training objective — completely unchanged

```
L_RF = E_{t,x_1,x_0} ‖G_θ(x_t, t) − (x_1 − x_0)‖²        (logit-normal t)
```

Standard rectified flow, no auxiliary losses on the latent interface, no consistency constraint. The latent interface is supervised only through its effect on the final velocity prediction.

### Cheap CFG (CCFG): asymmetric compute for guidance

Classical CFG: `v_CFG = (λ+1) v(x_t | c) − λ v(x_t | ∅)`. The two terms cost the same. AutoGuidance (Karras 2024) replaces the unconditional term with a **weak version of the same model**, but typically requires either a separately trained weak model or hand-crafted corruptions.

ELIT's natively-multi-budget design gives AutoGuidance for free: evaluate the main term at full budget `J̄`, evaluate the guidance term at a **smaller budget** `J̄_w ≤ J̄`:

```
v_AG  = (λ+1) G(x_t | c; J̄) − λ G(x_t | c;        J̄_w)         # autoguidance
v_CCFG = (λ+1) G(x_t | c; J̄) − λ G(x_t | ∅;        J̄_w)         # AG + CFG
```

CCFG drops the class condition from the guidance term (so it's still doing CFG-style negative-prompting) **and** runs that term at lower budget — cutting the unconditional path's cost to ~35 % of full and reducing total generation cost by ~33 % vs vanilla CFG, with a 19 % better FID on ImageNet-512.

## Architecture details

### Where Read/Write get placed in baseline backbones

| Backbone | Spatial blocks | Read after block | Write after block |
|---|---|---|---|
| DiT-XL/2 | 28 | 4 | 24 |
| U-ViT-XL | 28 | 4 | 24 |
| HDiT-XL | 28 | 4 (also apply groupwise downsampling in the latent space) | 24 |
| MM-DiT (Qwen-Image 60-layer 20B) | 60 | 8 | 52 |

The pattern: 4-block spatial head, ~85 % of blocks in the latent core, 4-block spatial tail. HDiT additionally factors the hierarchical structure into the latent space (group-wise downsampling), keeping its native multi-scale property.

### FLOP accounting

Per-block FLOPs (paper Fig. 10), with `d` = hidden dim, `N` = spatial tokens, `J` = latents per group, `G` = group count, `K = JG`:

| Op | Spatial block | Latent block | Read | Write |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Attn proj | 8Nd² | 8JGd² | d²(4N + 4JG) | d²(4N + 4JG) |
| Attn matmul | 2N²d | 2J²G²d | 2JNd | 2JNd |
| FFN | 16Nd² | 16JGd² | 4JGd² | 4Nd² |

The latent core scales **with `K = JG`** not with `N` — so once `J ≪ N/G`, latent-block cost dwindles while Read/Write overhead stays modest. The empirical FLOPs/wall-clock curve (Fig. 11) drops monotonically as `J̄` decreases from 60 to 0 (10 ms → 4 ms per step at 512px on the reported hardware).

## Inference cost

ELIT does **not** target NFE reduction — main experiments use a 40-step Euler sampler. The lever is **per-step cost via `J̄`**:

- ImageNet-512 DiT-XL: baseline 806 TFLOPs/iter, ELIT 25%-tokens 386 TFLOPs/iter (≈ 47 % of baseline) at **better FID** (12.5 vs 18.8) and better FDD (217 vs 339).
- ImageNet-512 HDiT-XL: 776 → 391 TFLOPs (50 %) at FID 12.9 vs 12.8 baseline — match quality at half compute.
- 512px is the favourable regime: pMF/EPG-style fixed patching at high res hits quadratic-attention pain on `N`, which ELIT shrinks to a quadratic on `JG` (≪ N²).
- ImageNet-256: gains exist but smaller — the baseline `N=256` isn't where attention pain lives.

**Compute-quality slope.** Defining `ρ = (metric_ratio) / (FLOPs_ratio)` for a low- vs high-compute pair (Tab. 5): DiT patch-doubled `ρ = 0.56` (FID), HDiT smaller-backbone `ρ = 0.85`, ELIT 25% tokens `ρ = 0.52` — degradation per FLOP cut is **2× smaller than HDiT's** and matches DiT's patch-size knob, but ELIT's `J̄` is a **runtime** knob and patch-size requires retraining.

**Importantly, at max budget ELIT is slightly more expensive than the equivalent DiT** (831 vs 806 TFLOPs/iter, 698 M vs 675 M params). The Read+Write+head+tail+core add overhead. The win is exclusively in the *dynamic* / *reduced* budget regime; you'd never run ELIT at 100 % tokens to get a single fixed quality if you could just train a DiT.

## Translation to raw audio

The two structural ideas — **latent-interface decouples cost from input length** and **tail-dropping yields a runtime budget knob** — are dimension-agnostic and should port cleanly to 1-D waveform.

1. **Variable CPU latency at inference time, single trained checkpoint.** This is the killer feature for our project. An interactive audio gen UI wants a "fast preview vs. final render" slider; ELIT's `J̄` *is* that slider. Train once on 22 kHz mono 1-s clips with `J_max = 64` latents per group and `G = 4` time-groups (256 max latents); at inference, choose `J̄ ∈ {8, 16, 32, 64}` for a 4-budget continuum. No retraining, no checkpoint juggling.
2. **Read attention learns importance ordering — exactly what audio needs.** Audio is highly non-uniform: silences and sustained tones carry little information, transients/onsets / consonants carry a lot. The Fig. 2 motivating experiment (DiT fails to reallocate compute from zero-padded regions; ELIT succeeds via importance-weighted Read) is the image analogue of "ELIT will route compute to the transients and skip the silences without supervision." Direct analogue to RFWave's per-frame energy weighting and Flow2GAN's spectral-energy-inverse loss, but learned implicitly and at the **architecture** level rather than the loss level.
3. **Group structure ports.** For 1-D audio: factor as `G` time-groups; one Read attention pulls patch tokens within a time-group into `J` latents. At long clip lengths (`N = 10 s × 22 kHz / patch = 3 440` tokens with 64-sample patches), grouped Read is essential — full cross-attention would be `O(N · K) = O(N²/4)`. 32 time-groups of 4 latents each gives `O(N·K/32) ≈ 0.5 · N` per Read, comfortably cheap on CPU.
4. **Stacks with everything else.** ELIT is a **drop-in head/tail/core split + Read/Write** on top of an existing DiT-like backbone. It composes with:
   - **patch-size scaling (pMF):** patch length sets `N` for a given clip length; ELIT then decouples *cost* from `N` via `J̄`. The combination is "fixed-token-count across clip lengths via patch-size scaling, *and* variable-cost-within-a-fixed-token-count via `J̄`."
   - **fastforward FM (iMF / SoFlow / pMF):** ELIT's RF objective is unchanged, so a 1-NFE fastforward training recipe slots in directly — orthogonal axes.
   - **STFT-frame ConvNeXt (Flow2GAN / RFWave):** less obvious. ELIT is a transformer-style construction; transplanting Read/Write attention onto a conv backbone is nontrivial. If we end up with the conv backbone as our CPU-preferred shape, ELIT's contribution is mostly architectural inspiration ("a small fixed-cost interface processes most of the work; head and tail are short conv stacks") rather than direct code reuse.
   - **TeaCache** (timestep-cache reuse across FM steps): paper reports comparable gains when applied on top of ELIT-DiT — i.e. step-skipping and per-step token-budgeting are orthogonal axes.
   - **CCFG**: free 33 % inference speedup whenever CFG is used. For audio class-conditional or text-conditional gen, this is a direct port — drop the condition from the guidance term *and* run it at lower `J̄`.

### What does not port cleanly

- **Distillation-onto-pretrained scenario.** The Qwen-Image experiment fine-tunes ELIT *on top of a pretrained 20B backbone* with an extra distillation loss. Useful template if we ever have a pretrained audio diffusion model worth retrofitting; for from-scratch audio training, the standard from-scratch ELIT recipe applies (no distillation needed).
- **Single-step regime.** ELIT was evaluated at 40-NFE rectified flow. The paper does not test 1-NFE. The mechanism (variable token budget) is in principle compatible with iMF / pMF / SoFlow, but **no published evidence that tail-dropping + 1-NFE training is stable**. Worth a small ablation: train ELIT-DiT-B with iMF v-loss + tail-dropping at 1-NFE on CIFAR-10 or short-audio.
- **CCFG with autoguidance** assumes a class-conditional setup. For unconditional audio gen there's no `∅` to drop, so CCFG reduces to "run guidance term at smaller budget" — still a free speedup, less dramatic.

## Strengths

- **Truly drop-in.** Works across DiT, U-ViT, HDiT, MM-DiT with no objective change and no auxiliary losses. The single ablation table (Tab. 4d) shows constant batch size during training works almost as well as variable batch size — so even the bookkeeping is minimal.
- **Importance ordering is qualitatively interpretable.** Fig. 12 shows Read attention maps: first two latents attend to broad semantic regions (foreground subject, main object), last two latents attend to fine textures. Maps onto the audio analogue cleanly — first latents should pick up the envelope/transients/pitch contour, last latents should refine timbre detail.
- **Beats training-free token-merging (ToMe / SDTM).** Fig. 13: ToMe/SDTM are upper-bounded by base DiT quality at any given compute; ELIT *surpasses* DiT at 25 % tokens. Training-time importance ordering wins over training-free clustering.
- **Compatible with distillation/grafting/pruning.** Compatibility with the Grafting recipe (Chandrasegaran 2025) is preserved — degradation on graft is 12.6 % FID / 8.9 % IS, matching the original grafting paper's published numbers on DiT.

## Limitations / open questions

- **No 1-NFE story.** Multi-step only; whether the variable-budget knob composes with fastforward FM at 1-NFE is unproven.
- **Constant overhead at max budget.** At full `J_max` tokens, ELIT is ~3 % more expensive than baseline DiT — modest, but it's compute you only buy back when you reduce `J̄`. If your deployment never runs at reduced budget, ELIT loses on absolute speed.
- **Grouping is hand-designed.** `G = 16` chosen by ablation on ImageNet. Audio time-groups, frequency-groups, mixed time-frequency groups all unexplored — could matter a lot.
- **CCFG saturates faster than CFG**, per the paper's own discussion section. Image quality plateaus or degrades at high `λ` more aggressively with CCFG than with CFG. For audio classifier-free guidance scales >5, CCFG may not be safe.
- **Budget scheduling across noise levels untested.** Appendix G shows using 50 % tokens for high-noise steps (t < 0.5) and 100 % for low-noise steps gives comparable FID at 154 vs 188 TFLOPs/iter — but the paper leaves a principled noise-aware budget scheduler as future work. For audio, the high-noise / low-noise split likely maps to different perceptual content (early steps = coarse spectral envelope, late steps = phase detail).

## Things to try in this repo

- **Port ELIT-DiT-B to 1-D waveform, 1 s @ 22 kHz, 64-sample patches → N = 344 patch tokens; G = 8 time-groups, J_max = 32 latents/group → K = 256 latents.** Block split `(2, 8, 2)` mirroring DiT-B's 12-block stack. Compare vs a vanilla DiT-B with the same block count.
- **Multi-budget training with `J̄ ∼ Uniform{4, …, 32}`** — gives a continuum of ~8 budgets at inference. Benchmark CPU latency at `J̄ ∈ {4, 8, 16, 32}` to map out the latency-vs-FAD frontier.
- **Read attention visualisation on transient-vs-sustain audio.** If the attention map concentrates on onsets/transients without supervision, the importance-ordering claim transfers cleanly to audio.
- **CCFG on unconditional audio** — drop the class drop, just run guidance term at half `J̄`. Free 33 % CPU speedup.
- **ELIT + pMF.** ELIT on a pMF backbone: fixed 256 patch-tokens across 1 s / 5 s / 10 s clips via patch-size scaling, *and* variable `J̄` at inference. Test whether the constant-cost-across-clip-length property holds under ELIT's latent-interface compute.
- **ELIT + iMF/pMF at 1-NFE.** Small ablation: does tail-dropping + 1-NFE v-loss training converge? If yes, this is a 1-NFE pixel-space audio gen recipe with a runtime budget knob — the most CPU-friendly combination in the wiki.
