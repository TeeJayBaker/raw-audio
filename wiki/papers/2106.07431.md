## TL;DR (project-relevant)

CRASH is the **canonical reference for score-based diffusion directly on 44.1 kHz raw drum waveforms**: a 1D FiLM-conditioned U-Net (downsampling factors 2-2-3-5-5, 300× total) trained with continuous-time denoising score matching on ~0.48 s mono one-shots. It is the first paper in the wiki that shows the recipe actually works in raw sample space at music sample rates — but it does so with **50–400 NFE on a 3090** and **no CPU benchmark**. For this repo it serves as (a) the architecture baseline we'd be trying to beat or distill, and (b) the source of several practical tricks (cosine σ schedule, RFF noise embedding, DDIM-as-ODE-reparam, post-hoc class conditioning via a separate noise-conditioned classifier) that carry over to any few-step method we end up running.

## What it actually is

A continuous-time variance-exploding-style score model on raw waveform. Forward SDE is the Song et al. (2021a) family `dx = f(t)x dt + g(t) dw`; training is the standard denoising score-matching mixture (Eq. 8 in the paper, λ(t) σ(t)⁻¹ ε-parameterization). The two non-trivial contributions are:

1. A reparameterization arguing **DDIM sampling is just one specific discretization of the same ODE**, derived by integrating `d(x/m) = d(σ/m) ε(x,σ)` between t_i and t_{i+1} (Eq. 15). This dissolves the apparent gap between DDPM-style schedules and DDIM. Practically: lets them re-use DDIM for any SDE they design.
2. A generalization of sub-VP that lets `m(t)` and `σ(t)` be specified independently as long as a smooth `σ(t)` with σ(0)=0, σ(1)=1-ε is chosen. They find a **cosine σ schedule** `σ(t) = ½[1 − cos((1−s)πt)]` with s=0.006 gives the best FAD — better than the linear-β VP schedule from Song et al., whose σ rises too fast near t=0 and forces the discretization to spend too few steps in the low-noise regime where the perceptual signal lives.

Inpainting, interpolation in two latent spaces (isotropic Gaussian at t=T, t-indexed code at intermediate t), and a "class-mixing" sampling scheme (weighted sum of per-class classifier gradients, Eq. 16) all fall out of the standard score-SDE toolkit; the class-mixing trick is a cute use of a separately trained noise-conditioned classifier rather than a class-conditional score net.

## Architecture (the part we care about for raw audio)

This is the load-bearing piece for the repo's interests. It's a **1D U-Net adapted from a source-separation architecture** (Meseguer-Brocal & Peeters 2019), with the noise level fed in via **FiLM** at every block.

```
input: x(t), shape (B, 1, 21000)         (~0.476 s at 44.1 kHz)
noise embed: σ → RFF(32 freqs, fixed N(0,16) sample) → MLP → 512-d
                ↓ broadcast as FiLM (γ, β) into every block

encoder DBlocks   (kernel 5 conv, then downsample)
  DBlock 128, /2     → 10500
  DBlock 128, /2     → 5250
  DBlock 256, /3     → 1750
  DBlock 512, /5     → 350
  DBlock 512, /5     → 70

decoder UBlocks (mirror, with skip cats from same-level DBlock)
  UBlock 128 ×2, ×3, ×5, ×5 …
output: 3×1 conv → ε̂(x,σ)
```

Inside each DBlock: downsample → 1×1 conv → FiLM → three 3×1 convs with dilations 1, 2, 4, 8 (residual block uses kernel 1). UBlocks are the same with `Upsampling` instead of `Downsampling` and an extra skip-concat from the matching DBlock. Channels grow 128 → 128 → 256 → 512 → 512.

Total downsampling factor 2·2·3·5·5 = **300**, so inputs must be a multiple of 300 — they pad/crop to **21,000 samples ≈ 0.476 s** at 44.1 kHz. This is the fundamental scale assumption of the network: short one-shots, not loops or phrases.

**Param count is not reported.** Order-of-magnitude estimate from layer counts and channel widths: roughly tens of millions of parameters (channels 128–512, ~5 levels, ~3 convs per block).

The σ-conditioning style (FiLM throughout, RFF embedding of σ rather than t) is presented explicitly as better than DiffWave's dilated-conv-no-downsample structure (Kong et al. 2021) and as better than WaveGrad's mel-conditioning architecture (Chen et al. 2020) — the claim is the σ-conditioned U-Net retains both advantages. No ablation against those alternatives in this paper.

## Training objective

Standard score-matching with the ε-parameterization. With `ε_θ(x(t), σ(t)) := −σ(t) s_θ(x(t), σ(t))`:

```
L(θ) = E_{t, x(0), ε} ‖ √λ(t)/σ(t) · [ ε_θ(m(t) x(0) + σ(t) ε, σ(t)) − ε ] ‖²
```

with `t ~ U[η, 1]` (η chosen so σ(η)=10⁻⁴, considered imperceptible), `x(0) ~ p_data`, `ε ~ N(0, I)`. Weighting follows Song et al. 2021a (`λ(t) ∝ σ²` for ancestral, or `λ(t) = g²(t)` for maximum likelihood — they note these coincide when log SNR is affine).

Adam, lr 2e-4, EMA 0.999, ~120 epochs, batch 180, GPU is a 3090. They observe (consistent with Song & Ermon 2020) that the EMA weights produce monotonically improving FAD while the raw weights are noisier.

## Inference cost — the headline gap for this repo

This is where the paper is honest but inconvenient.

| Sampler            | NFE | Reported wall time (RTX-3090, 27 000 drums @ 0.48 s) | "Faster than real time"? |
|---|---|---|---|
| SDE (Euler-Maruyama)| 400 | 12 h                                    | No (on GPU, batch 180)   |
| ODE (Euler)         | 400 | 12 h                                    | No                       |
| scipy RK45 (adaptive)| ~?  | 5 h                                     | Not clearly              |
| DDIM (reparam)      | 50  | **1.5 h → 3.5 h of generated audio**    | ~2.3× faster than RT on GPU |

The DDIM-50 timing is the one promising number: 1.5 h to generate 3.5 h of audio on a 3090 in batches of 180 amounts to ~2.3× real time, on GPU, with batching. **There is no CPU benchmark.** Extrapolating from a typical 30-50× CPU/GPU gap for this kind of conv U-Net at this width, naive single-sample CPU DDIM-50 inference is almost certainly **slower than real time** — likely by an order of magnitude. That's the gap this project exists to close.

FAD numbers (sub-VP cos schedule, their best):

| Schedule           | SDE 400 | ODE 400 | scipy | DDIM 50 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| VP exp (Song)      | 4.11    | 3.96    | 4.54  | 5.11    |
| VP cos             | 1.29    | 1.10    | 2.82  | 1.56    |
| sub-VP cos         | 1.34    | **0.98**| 3.08  | 3.36    |
| sub-VP 1-1 cos     | 1.41    | 1.23    | 2.92  | 2.93    |

Two observations matter for us:

- **Best NFE-400 FAD ≈ 1.0**, best DDIM-50 FAD ≈ 1.56 (VP cos). The drop from 400 to 50 NFE costs ~0.5 FAD on VP cos and is much worse on sub-VP cos — a reminder that **the SDE schedule that minimizes asymptotic FAD is not the one that minimizes few-step FAD**. Their best 400-NFE schedule (sub-VP cos) is the *worst* DDIM-50 schedule among cosine variants.
- The cosine σ schedule beats the linear-β VP schedule across the board, and the gap is largest at low NFE. This is the one design choice we should carry forward verbatim before doing anything fancier.

## Dataset

~300 k mono one-shots (kick, snare, cymbal, balanced), 44.1 kHz, padded to 21 000 samples (≈0.476 s). Same internal Sony dataset used in Nistal et al. 2020 (DrumGAN). **Not public.** Comparable open alternatives: Freesound drum subsets, the NeurIPS 2020 drum-synthesis dataset used by various follow-ups.

## Strengths

- Clean SDE/ODE/DDIM unification — the reparam in §3 is the cleanest derivation of "DDIM = one discretization of the ODE family" in the audio literature.
- Demonstrates that **44.1 kHz raw-waveform diffusion works** with a reasonable-size 1D conv U-Net and no spectrogram or codec intermediate.
- Cosine σ schedule and σ-RFF embedding are small, transferable wins.
- Class-mixing via a separate noise-conditioned classifier is genuinely flexible: one score net, retrainable conditioning.

## Limitations (project-relevant)

- **No CPU inference number, no params number, no FLOPs number.** The paper is silent on the metric this project most cares about.
- 50-NFE on GPU is "barely faster than RT with batching", and the schedule that's best at 50 NFE is different from the schedule that's best at 400 NFE — a clue that this network/schedule combo is *not* in the regime where consistency-style distillation will be free. Any 1-step / few-step adaptation will need real care.
- 0.476 s fixed length. Anything longer requires either re-training or some patching scheme not addressed here.
- The "FAD between test and noisy test set is 0.72" footnote means FAD ≈ 1.0 is **near the noise floor of the metric** — we shouldn't read too much into ranking differences below ~0.5 FAD.
- Diversity is reported as "a bit less diverse than the original dataset" — typical mode-collapse-lite that we should watch for when comparing.

## Translates to this repo as

- **A baseline to distill from.** If we want to ship a 1-step or 2-step raw-waveform drum model on CPU, this 1D-UNet teacher + DDIM-50 trajectory is the natural starting point. Consistency models / progressive distillation literature should be applied to *this exact architecture* before we go inventing alternatives.
- **An architecture template.** Downsampling factors 2-2-3-5-5 with FiLM(σ) and RFF embedding is a reasonable default for short-sample raw-waveform networks at 44.1 kHz. The factor-300 receptive-field assumption is worth recording.
- **A practical reminder:** schedule choice interacts with NFE budget. Any benchmark we run should sweep schedule × NFE jointly.

## Open questions for the repo

- What is the actual CPU cost of one forward pass through this U-Net at 21 000 samples, single-batch? Should measure before doing anything else.
- Does the same architecture stay competitive at 1–2 s lengths (×4 input length), or does the factor-300 downsampling chain need to be re-thought?
- Can this be distilled to ≤4 NFE with consistency-model-style training while keeping FAD ≤ 2? The schedule sensitivity at low NFE suggests it's not free.
- How does FAD ≈ 1 here compare to GAN baselines (DrumGAN, Nistal et al. 2020) on the same dataset? Paper claims it "closes the gap" but doesn't put the GAN number in a table.
