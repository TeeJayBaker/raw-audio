## TL;DR (project-relevant)

LinDiff is the only published precedent that runs the full **linear patchify → transformer trunk → linear unpatchify → post-conv smoother** recipe on **raw waveform** (22.05 kHz, 64-sample patches, 256-dim transformer, 4 layers / 4 heads). It also matches every choice this project is converging on: `x₀`-prediction under a linear interpolant (= rectified flow with Euler steps), Time-Adaptive LayerNorm (a Sandwich-style time conditioning, not AdaLN-zero), mel cross-attention, an explicit Post-Conv module motivated as a *boundary smoother* for patch artefacts, and an implicit-diffusion + GAN finishing move that pushes RTF to 0.013 on RTX 3090 at 3 steps (MOS 4.12). The repo's `configs/backbone/waveform_transformer.yaml` is essentially "LinDiff at 48 kHz with bigger trunk and smaller patches" — this is the closest prior art in the wiki.

## Why this paper matters here

Until LinDiff was ingested, the patched-ViT family in the wiki was image-only (JiT, pMF, EPG, ELIT) and the audio papers were either 1-D conv U-Nets (CRASH, WaveFM) or STFT-domain ConvNeXt (Vocos, RFWave, Flow2GAN, ComVo, WaveNeXt). LinDiff fills the empty cell: **a transformer trunk operating directly on raw waveform patches, with a post-trunk conv smoother and rectified-flow-shape training**. Everything in this paper ports to the project's `waveform_transformer` backbone with minimal translation.

It also gives the **canonical citation for the Post-Conv-as-boundary-smoother argument** — the paper states the motivation explicitly (see §3.2) and shows the spectrogram with horizontal-line harmonic artefacts when the Post-Conv is ablated.

## Training objective: linear-interpolant `x₀`-prediction = rectified flow

The forward "diffusion" is a linear interpolant between target audio `a₀` and Gaussian noise `a_T`:

```
a_t^rev = (1 − t/T) · a_T + (t/T) · a₀         (Eq. 6 — reverse-process parameterisation, t ∈ [0, T])
```

with constant velocity `v_d = a_T − a₀` (Eq. 5). The paper credits **rectified flow (Liu et al. 2022, ref [16])** as the inspiration.

**Parameterisation:** instead of predicting the velocity directly, LinDiff parameterises an *implicit denoiser* (DiffGAN-style, ref [24]). The network `f_θ(a_t^rev, c, t)` predicts the **clean target `a_T^rev` (= `a₀`)** — i.e. `x₀`-prediction — and the velocity is recovered via

```
v(a_t^rev, c, t) = f_θ(a_t^rev, c, t) − a_T     (Eq. 9)
```

(`a_T` is the running noisy sample; the equation is the "v from x" linear-interpolant identity.) The Euler update at inference becomes:

```
a_{t+1}^rev = a_t^rev + (a_T^rev_pred − a₀) · (1/T)        (Eq. 12)
```

i.e. a standard rectified-flow Euler step rewritten in the implicit-denoiser parameterisation.

**Diffusion loss (Eq. 13):** plain MSE on the predicted clean waveform.

```
L_diff = MSE(wav_gt, a_T^rev)
```

This is the same `x_1`-prediction-from-FM rule that Flow2GAN and WaveFM independently rediscover (see [methods](../wiki/concepts/methods.md) FM-on-audio rule book). LinDiff is the **earliest** audio paper in the wiki making this choice and the first to do it with a transformer trunk.

**Auxiliary losses (Eq. 14, 17):**

- `L_s` — multi-resolution STFT MSE (4 random `(window, hop, n_fft)` pairs, one picked per step).
- `L_adv` — non-saturating GAN on three discriminators applied to `a_T^rev` (i.e. the predicted clean audio at every intermediate step): one spectral discriminator + HiFi-GAN's MSD + HiFi-GAN's MPD. Discriminator updated every 5 training steps, adversarial loss weighted 0.2 to balance.

## Architecture

Three modules in sequence; the figure in §3.2 makes the topology obvious.

### 1. Patchify (linear projection)

- Input: raw waveform `a_t ∈ ℝ^{N}` at 22.05 kHz, plus mel condition `c` (80-band, hop 12.5 ms, win 50 ms, n_fft 1024).
- **Patch size 64 samples** (= 2.90 ms at 22.05 kHz; 256 tokens carry 0.74 s, 3600 tokens carry 10.44 s — the paper's training clip ceiling).
- Linear projection `Linear(64 → 256)` to patch embeddings.
- Ablation (Tab. 4): patch=64 gives MOS 4.18; patch=128 → 3.65; patch=256 → 3.34. **Smaller patches strictly win on quality, lose on compute** — paper picks 64 as the sweet spot. Lossy compression at larger patches; below 64 not tested.

### 2. Audio Transformer (trunk)

- **4 layers, 4 heads, hidden 256, MLP via Conv1d.** This is *small* for a transformer (cf. JiT-B/16 at 12 layers / 12 heads / 768 dim, ~131 M params), reflecting the audio-data scarcity vs ImageNet.
- Each block (Fig. 1b):
  1. `TA-LN` → Self-Attention → residual add
  2. `TA-LN` → Cross-Attention against mel hidden features (linearly projected to 256-dim, audio is query, mel is K/V) → residual add
  3. `TA-LN` → MLP (Conv1d) → residual add
- "Channel Flatten" at the end reshapes back to the time axis, then a "Local Conv" — these are the *unpatchify*: reshape `(B, N_tokens, 256)` → `(B, 64·channels?, N_tokens)` style, with a final Conv1d to project to waveform channels. (The paper's terminology is light; the Figure shows `MLP → Channel Flatten → Local Conv` as the end-of-trunk stack feeding the Post-Conv.)

### 3. Time-Adaptive Layer Norm (TA-LN)

The conditioning mechanism. **Differs from AdaLN-zero in three ways**:

```
TALN(x, t_emb) = g(t_emb) · LN(x) + b(t_emb)     (Eq. 11)
```

1. **No gating residual.** AdaLN-zero scales the *residual branch* by a learned `α(t_emb)` initialised to 0 (so the block starts as identity). TA-LN replaces the LayerNorm's affine `(γ, β)` directly with `(g(t_emb), b(t_emb))` — same shape as adaLN but no zero-init residual gate.
2. **Step embedding pipeline:** sinusoidal positional encoding (128-d) of `t` → linear → diffusion-step embedding `t_emb`.
3. **Mel conditioning is separate.** Time goes through TA-LN; mel goes through cross-attention. AdaLN-style audio papers (e.g. RFWave) usually fold both into AdaLN.

This is closest to "AdaLN without the zero residual" or equivalently "Sandwich LN with affine driven by `t_emb`" — call it AdaLN-affine. The lack of a zero-init gate matters for training stability at depth; LinDiff's 4-layer trunk is shallow enough not to need it, but the project's `waveform_transformer.yaml` runs depth=12 and uses AdaLN-zero — keep AdaLN-zero, this is one place to *not* port verbatim.

### 4. Post-Conv (boundary smoother)

Paper's own motivation (§3.2, verbatim): *"Due to partitioning the input sequence into small patches, the resolution of the model is limited and the output sequence obtained through AiT losses much high-frequency information. However, as the human ear is sensitive to speech, we here add a Post Convolution module to process the details of the output."*

The Figure 1(c) shows the exact structure:

```
Input waveform (B, 1, N) from transformer
  ↓ Conv1d
  ↓ Leaky ReLU
  ↓ Time-Aware Location-Variable Convolution (TA-LVC)  ← conditioned on (mel c, time t)
  ↓ Conv1d
  ↓ Leaky ReLU
  ↓ Conv1d
  ↓ Output waveform (B, 1, N)
```

**32 channels** in the Post-Conv hidden width (paper §4.1 model configuration: "The Post-Conv module uses Conv1d and Time-Aware Location-Variable Convolution with 32 channels").

**Exact kernel sizes and dilations are NOT given in the paper.** The paper points to ref [6] (FastDiff, Huang et al. 2022, arXiv:2204.09934) for the LVC module structure. FastDiff in turn cites LVCNet (Zeng et al. 2021, ref [26] in LinDiff) — these specify the LVC mechanism but not the hyperparameters LinDiff uses.

What LinDiff *does* tell us:

- The LVC layer is conditioned on **both `c` (mel) and `t` (time step)** — the "Time-Aware" prefix is LinDiff's contribution; FastDiff's LVC is mel-only.
- The Conv1d layers in Post-Conv are plain Conv1d (no dilation discussion); width 32.
- Ablation (Tab. 4, Fig. 3): removing Post-Conv at 3 steps drops MOS 4.12 → 3.84 and introduces horizontal-line harmonic-noise artefacts in the spectrogram. **This is the explicit "boundary smoother" demonstration** — without Post-Conv, the patch boundaries at every 64-sample interval are visible in the spectrogram as a horizontal periodicity. This is the diagnostic the project's `conv_smoother` head is meant to address.

**The Location-Variable Convolution (LVCNet) mechanism**, for reference: a small kernel-predictor MLP takes the conditioning `c` (and in LinDiff's case `t` too) and produces a **per-time-step kernel** — different convolution weights at different positions along the waveform, conditional on the local mel frame. This is the audio analogue of dynamic / hypernet convolutions. The kernel predictor is trained end-to-end. Compared to FiLM (which only scales/shifts channels), LVC also varies the receptive-field weights along time.

### Discriminator stack (for the GAN-finetune stage)

Three discriminators on the predicted clean waveform `a_T^rev`:

1. Spectral discriminator (the paper says "one of the discriminators examines the samples from a spectral perspective" — no further detail).
2. Multi-Scale Discriminator (HiFi-GAN MSD).
3. Multi-Period Discriminator (HiFi-GAN MPD).

Updated every 5 training steps; generator adversarial loss weight 0.2.

## Training pipeline (three-stage)

The algorithm (Algorithm 1) trains in three stages:

1. **Stage 1 (10k steps):** diffusion loss only on short audio clips. Pure rectified-flow `x₀`-prediction.
2. **Stage 2:** add adversarial training; still short clips; both gen and discriminator updated each step.
3. **Stage 3 (long clips):** discriminator updated every 5 steps; generator gets `L_diff + L_s + 0.2 · L_adv`. Long clips = up to 3600 tokens = 3600·64/22050 ≈ 10.44 s.

Total 200k Adam steps (`β₁=0.9, β₂=0.98, ε=10⁻⁹`), batch 16, on 4× RTX 3090 GPUs. LJSpeech (24 h single-speaker) and LibriTTS (585 h multi-speaker) at 22.05 kHz mono, 16-bit.

## How this maps onto the project's `waveform_transformer` config

The config currently reads (paraphrased):

| Item | LinDiff (22 kHz) | `waveform_transformer.yaml` (48 kHz) |
|---|---|---|
| sample rate | 22 050 | 48 000 |
| patch size (samples) | 64 (2.90 ms; tokens at 344 / s) | 512 (10.67 ms; tokens at 93.75 / s) |
| linear in_proj | `Linear(64 → 256)` | `Conv1d(1 → 512, k=512, s=512)` (equivalent to a 512-d linear projection) |
| trunk dim | 256 | 512 |
| trunk depth | 4 | 12 |
| trunk heads | 4 | 8 |
| MLP in block | Conv1d | (transformer FFN) |
| time conditioning | TA-LN (AdaLN-affine) | AdaLN-zero |
| mel conditioning | cross-attention | — (no mel input in the config) |
| unpatchify | "Channel Flatten" → "Local Conv" | linear (ConvTranspose1d k=patch_size) |
| post-conv head | TA-LVC + plain Conv1d, 32 ch | `ConvSmootherHead`, hidden 32, 3 layers, k=13 |
| prediction target | x₀ (clean waveform) | x₁ (FM convention; same target) |
| interpolant | linear (rectified flow) | linear (FM) |
| GAN finetune | yes (MSD + MPD + spectral) | not active in baseline FM |

**Key alignments:**

- **Same interpolant, same parameterisation, same loss family.** The project's FM-with-`x₁`-prediction is identical to LinDiff's linear-diffusion-with-`x₀`-prediction up to naming (the paper calls `a₀` the clean target and `a_T` the noise; FM convention is opposite). Eq. 12 is a rectified-flow Euler step.
- **Same Post-Conv role.** `ConvSmootherHead` in the repo is the project's analogue of LinDiff's Post-Conv module — both are explicitly motivated as a boundary smoother for transformer patch artefacts.
- **Mel-cross-attention is the load-bearing conditioning channel.** The project doesn't yet have a conditional waveform-transformer config wired up; when adding one, the LinDiff topology (cross-attention against mel hidden features, audio as query) is the published precedent.

**Key differences and what to think about:**

- **Patch density.** LinDiff at 64 samples / 22.05 kHz = 344 tokens / s. The project's 512 / 48 kHz = 94 tokens / s — **3.7× fewer tokens per second of audio**. This is a much more aggressive patch ratio. LinDiff's ablation shows monotonic quality loss as patch size grows from 64 → 128 → 256 (MOS 4.18 → 3.65 → 3.34); the project sits beyond patch=256 in token-density terms. The post-conv smoother has more boundary smoothing to do, and the trunk has less time-resolution detail to work with. Worth a sweep at 256 / 128 / 64 samples once the baseline trains.
- **Trunk depth and width.** Project's 12×512×8 trunk is roughly `(12·512²·4)/(4·256²·4) ≈ 12×` the FLOPs/token of LinDiff's. Whether this extra capacity compensates for the lower token density is the open empirical question for the architecture.
- **TA-LN vs AdaLN-zero.** LinDiff uses TA-LN at 4 layers; AdaLN-zero only matters at depth where the zero-init residual gate keeps the network well-conditioned. At depth 12, AdaLN-zero is the safer default; don't port TA-LN.
- **No mel conditioning yet.** The project's `waveform_transformer.yaml` is unconditional; LinDiff is a mel-conditioned vocoder. Direct quality comparison only makes sense once a conditional variant exists.
- **Post-Conv specifics.** The project's `ConvSmootherHead` uses 3 plain Conv1d layers at kernel=13. LinDiff uses 3 Conv1d layers + 1 Time-Aware LVC layer at unspecified kernels. **The TA-LVC is the structural difference** — a kernel-predictor MLP that produces position-varying convolution weights conditional on `(c, t)`. Worth ablating: replace one of the project's Conv1d with a TA-LVC layer and see if the ablation gap (MOS 4.18 → 3.84 when LVC is dropped from LinDiff's Post-Conv) reproduces.

## Strengths

- **Earliest waveform-transformer in the literature** with full method ablation (patch size, Post-Conv, adversarial). Establishes the recipe template.
- **Clean rectified-flow framing** months before "flow matching" papers dominated audio. Eq. 12 is RF-Euler in disguise.
- **Explicit boundary-artefact diagnostic.** Fig. 3 shows the harmonic-line pattern from patch boundaries without Post-Conv — the visual that justifies the conv smoother family.
- **Three-discriminator stack** (spectral + MSD + MPD) carried forward from HiFi-GAN; replicated by every modern audio GAN.

## Limitations

- **MOS 4.12 at 3 steps is competitive but not SOTA** — beaten by WaveNet (AR), and on the diversity metric LinDiff still lags WaveNet. The transformer trunk + small embedding space hurts multi-speaker zero-shot generation (Tab. 3 LibriTTS).
- **Quadratic attention cost.** Paper limits training to 3600 tokens ≈ 10.44 s. For longer audio the transformer is unaffordable; the project's 1–10 s sweet spot fits, but >10 s needs an architectural change (latent interface à la ELIT, or a non-attention bottleneck).
- **No CPU benchmark.** Only RTX 3090 RTF reported. The 0.013 RTF at 3 steps is GPU-only.
- **Post-Conv hyperparameters are not fully specified** in the paper text — kernel sizes for the Conv1d layers and the TA-LVC kernel-predictor MLP shape are left implicit, referring to FastDiff (ref [6]) for the LVC details.
- **Three-stage training pipeline is a lot of moving parts** — 200k Adam steps across three stages, on 4× 3090. Not a budget-friendly recipe.

## Open questions for this project

- **Does the patch=64 / 22.05 kHz sweet spot translate to 48 kHz?** The natural-scale port is patch=140 (≈64·48000/22050), giving the same 2.90 ms patch duration and 344 tokens/s. The current config uses patch=512 → 3.7× fewer tokens. A sweep on (patch, trunk depth, trunk width) at matched compute would map the project's local equivalent of LinDiff's Tab. 4.
- **Does the TA-LVC layer add a measurable quality boost over plain Conv1d Post-Conv** at the project's audio resolutions, or does it become structurally unnecessary with a deeper trunk?
- **Replace `ConvSmootherHead`'s middle Conv1d with TA-LVC.** Minimal code change; one ablation against the existing baseline.
- **The Post-Conv ablation reproduces?** Train `waveform_transformer.yaml` with and without `ConvSmootherHead`; check whether removal reproduces LinDiff's horizontal-line harmonic artefact in the spectrogram.
- **CPU RTF.** LinDiff has no CPU number. The project's `waveform_transformer.yaml` at depth=12 / dim=512 / patch=512 / 48 kHz is much bigger per token; expected CPU cost is dominated by the transformer attention (`O(tokens² · dim)`) and FFN (`O(tokens · dim²)`). At 1 s @ 48 kHz / patch=512 → 94 tokens, attention is ~`94² · 512 ≈ 4.5 M` MACs / head / layer — small enough that CPU is plausible. **Worth measuring; would be the first published CPU number for a waveform-transformer.**
