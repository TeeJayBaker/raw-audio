# TL;DR — why this matters for raw-audio CPU gen

Theoretical companion to **Drifting (2602.04770)**. One identity does everything: under a Gaussian kernel, the drifting field is *literally* a score difference on Gaussian-smoothed distributions, `V_{p,q}^{(σ)}(x) = σ² ∇_x log(p_σ / q_σ)(x)`. From this single fact the paper closes the three open questions of the original Drifting paper and supplies a kernel-design program. For us, the practical takeaways for raw-waveform one-step audio are:

1. **Kernel choice matters far more than the parent paper made clear.** The Gaussian kernel suffers an *exponential* high-frequency convergence bottleneck (Landau damping); the Laplacian gets only polynomial slowdown. For raw audio — which is dominated by high-frequency content — a Gaussian-kernel drift will be the worst possible choice, and the empirical preference for Laplacian in the parent paper now has a sharp explanation.
2. **An exponential bandwidth-annealing schedule** `σ(t) = σ₀ e^{-rt}` converts a worst-case `exp(O(K_max²))` convergence time into `O(log K_max)` while preserving the Gaussian's identifiability guarantee. This is a free training-time improvement, not an inference change — costs us nothing on CPU.
3. **Stop-gradient is not optional.** It is the JKO frozen-field discretization of a Wasserstein gradient flow. Removing it makes the loss minimisable by *drift collapse* (`||V|| → 0` without `q → p`). Sliced-Wasserstein vs `||V||` decorrelate completely without SG (Fig 4c). Don't even consider removing it in an audio replication.
4. **A general recipe for new drifts:** any "regular" discrepancy `F[q]` with `F[q] ≥ 0` and `F[q] = 0 ⇔ q = p` yields a valid drift `V(x) = -∇ δF/δq (x)` that inherits all JKO guarantees. They demonstrate with a Sinkhorn-divergence drift; on a checkerboard distribution it converges comparably to the Laplacian-kernel drift. **This is what unlocks audio-perceptual drifts** — we can plug in any audio-aware divergence with smooth particle-level gradients (e.g. Sinkhorn on Mel features, or a Sobolev divergence) and inherit the same convergence theory.
5. **Identifiability** (`V ≡ 0 ⇒ p = q`) is proved cleanly via Fourier injectivity of Gaussian convolution. Closes the Prop 3.1 hole in the parent paper.

Experiments here are deliberately small (1D/2D toys, 3-layer MLPs, ~3000 steps); the spectral / variational analysis is the contribution. No ImageNet, no audio, no CPU numbers. So the value to us is **diagnostic + prescriptive**: it tells us *why* to prefer Laplacian over Gaussian for audio and *how* to construct an audio-aware drift.

# The core identity (Theorem 4.1)

Under the Gaussian kernel `φ_σ(z) = (2πσ²)^{-d/2} exp(-||z||²/(2σ²))`, with `p_σ := p * φ_σ` and `q_σ := q * φ_σ`:

```
V_{p,q}^{(σ)}(x) = σ² ∇_x log[ p_σ(x) / q_σ(x) ]
```

Proof sketch (App. A.1): differentiate the kernel, `(y − x) φ_σ(x−y) = σ² ∇_x φ_σ(x−y)`. Substitute into the numerator of the attractive drift `V_p⁺`, pull the gradient out by dominated convergence; the result divided by `p_σ` is `σ² ∇ log p_σ`. Same for `V_q⁻`. Difference gives the identity.

Two-line consequence: **drifting is score matching in disguise**, but the score is computed *non-parametrically on the fly* via Parzen-window kernel-density estimates over the current generator batch, rather than being learned by a separate network. There's nothing to learn for the score; the network is the *sampler*, not the score-field model.

Independently established by Weber 2024 from a different starting point (top-down KL-minimizing flow); also concurrent with Lai et al. 2603.07514 and Li & Zhu 2602.20463 — same identity, different angles. None of those provide the spectral / Landau-damping / annealing analysis that is the actual contribution here.

# What the identity buys us — three open questions, all resolved

## 1. Identifiability — `V ≡ 0 ⇒ p = q`

Vanishing drift means `∇ log(p_σ / q_σ) = 0`, so `p_σ = C q_σ`; normalisation forces `C = 1`, so `p_σ = q_σ`. In Fourier, `p̂ · e^{-σ²||ξ||²/2} = q̂ · e^{-σ²||ξ||²/2}`. The Gaussian factor is strictly positive, so `p̂ = q̂`, hence `p = q`. **Proved**, for *any* σ > 0 (Prop 5.1). Parent paper had only proved the easy direction (`p = q ⇒ V = 0`).

## 2. Landau damping — why Laplacian beats Gaussian

Linearise the McKean-Vlasov dynamics `∂_t q + ∇ · (q V_{p,q}) = 0` around the equilibrium `q = p + δ`. Under local homogeneity of `p_σ`, the linearised PDE on the smoothed perturbation `δ_σ = φ_σ * δ` is:

```
∂_t δ(x, t) = σ² Δ(δ_σ(x, t))           (Gaussian kernel, Eq. 9)
∂_t δ̂(ξ, t) = -λ_κ(ξ) δ̂(ξ, t)            (general kernel κ, Theorem 5.2)
λ_κ(ξ) = c_κ |ξ|² κ̂(ξ)
```

Each Fourier mode decays independently at rate `λ_κ(ξ) ∝ |ξ|² κ̂(ξ)` — exactly the **Landau-damping** dispersion relation from collisionless plasma kinetic theory. The kernel plays the role of the medium; its spectrum directly controls mode-resolved convergence times.

Plugging in the two kernels of interest:

| Kernel | `κ̂(ξ)` | Decay rate `λ(ξ)` | Convergence time to reach `K_max` |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gaussian `φ_σ` | `e^{-σ²|ξ|²/2}` | `σ² |ξ|² e^{-σ²|ξ|²/2}` | `T_Gauss ≈ (log(1/ε) / (σ²K_max²)) · exp(σ²K_max² / 2)` — **exponential in K_max²** |
| Laplacian `e^{-|x|/τ}` | `(1 + τ²|ξ|²)^{-(d+1)/2}` | `c_κ |ξ|² (1+τ²|ξ|²)^{-(d+1)/2}` | `T_exp ∝ log(1/ε) τ^{d+1} K_max^{d-1}` — **polynomial in K_max** |

(Corollary 5.4.) The Gaussian kernel of bandwidth σ behaves as a **low-pass filter with cutoff `|ξ| ~ 1/σ`**: modes above the cutoff are exponentially suppressed in the convergence rate. This is intrinsic to the kernelised particle dynamics, **not** the spectral bias of neural networks — it survives even with a perfect function approximator.

**For audio this is the central finding.** Raw waveform has substantial mass at high spatial frequencies (mid/high-frequency audio bands → high `|ξ|` in 1D sample space). With a Gaussian kernel, those modes are *exponentially* slow to converge. The Laplacian's polynomial scaling is the only practical choice — confirming the parent paper's empirical preference with a mechanism.

## 3. Stop-gradient — JKO frozen-field discretization (§5.3)

Define the smoothed-KL energy:

```
F_σ[q] := σ² KL(q_σ ‖ p_σ) = σ² ∫ q_σ log(q_σ/p_σ) dx
```

Prop 5.5: `F_σ` is lower semicontinuous, has first variation `δF_σ/δq(x) = σ²(φ_σ * log(q_σ/p_σ))(x) + C`, and its Wasserstein gradient field `v_σ[q] := -∇(δF_σ/δq)` equals `-V_{p,q}^{(σ)}` up to a smoothing-induced error `O(σ² ||∇² log(q_σ/p_σ)||_∞)`. **So drifting is the Wasserstein gradient flow of `F_σ`.**

The JKO scheme discretizes such a flow implicitly:
```
q_{n+1}^τ = argmin_{q ∈ P₂} { F_σ[q] + (1/2τ) W_2²(q, q_n^τ) }
```
This is intractable because the velocity depends on the *unknown* minimiser `q_{n+1}^τ`. The **frozen-field explicit Euler** replaces it with the current iterate:
```
S_n(x) := x + τ v_σ[q_n^τ](x),    q̃_{n+1} := (S_n)# q_n^τ
```
introducing an error `W_2(q̃_{n+1}, q_{n+1}^τ) = O(τ^{3/2})` (App. E.3). Fitting this with a parametric generator gives exactly:
```
L(θ) = E_ε [ || G_θ(ε) − sg[ G_{θ_n}(ε) + τ v_σ[q_{θ_n}](G_{θ_n}(ε)) ] ||² ]    (Eq. 16)
```
which **is the drifting loss with `η = τ`**. The stop-gradient is the frozen-field bracket. Theorem 5.6: removing SG yields the coupled loss `L_coupled = τ² ‖v_σ[q_θ]‖²_{L²(q_θ)}`, whose gradient contains a *distribution-feedback term* `(D_q v)∇_θ q_θ` — minimisable by reducing `‖V‖` without transporting any mass toward `p` ("drift collapse"). Empirically (Fig 4): with SG, `‖V‖` and sliced-W are correlated `r > 0.95`; without SG, `‖V‖ → 10⁻⁸` while SW stays at 0.389.

This is structurally identical to: target networks in DQN, momentum encoders in MoCo/BYOL, EMA teachers in semi-supervised learning. The paper hypothesises the JKO-frozen-field principle may explain why **all** of those self-referential targets need stop-gradient — a striking unification.

# Practical contributions

## Exponential bandwidth annealing (§6.2)

Schedule:
```
σ(t) = σ₀ e^{-rt},  held at σ_min after T_ann
```

The optimal rate window `σ²|k|² = 2` (where `λ_Gauss(k)` peaks) sweeps continuously across frequencies, activating each mode at its maximum convergence rate. Theorem 6.2:
```
T_{ε,anneal} ≲ (1/r) log(σ₀/σ_min) + (1/λ_min(K_max)) log(1/ε)
            = O(log K_max)         [choosing σ_min = √2/K_max]
```
versus `exp(O(K_max²))` for fixed-bandwidth Gaussian. **Logarithmic in K_max**, retaining Gaussian identifiability. Validated numerically in Fig 2a-c: at `k=20`, exponential schedule beats linear by ~6× and cosine by orders of magnitude in spectral error decay (Fig 2c, App F).

**Takeaway for audio:** if we use a Gaussian-kernel drift at all, the bandwidth schedule should be exponentially-decaying not constant. For Laplacian we don't strictly need it (polynomial is already fine) but a multi-bandwidth Laplacian (`Σ_τ V^{(τ)}`) is the parent paper's recipe and remains a reasonable default.

## Principled drift construction (§6.3)

Any functional `F[q]` on `P₂(Ω)` satisfying:
- (a) lower semicontinuity in `W_2`,
- (b) existence and smoothness of `δF/δq` (in `C^∞(Ω)`),
- (c) positivity `F[q] ≥ 0` with `F[q] = 0 ⇔ q = p`,

yields a valid drift `V(x) = -∇_x (δF/δq)(x)` that inherits the entire JKO-frozen-field-stop-gradient chain. This **supersedes the anti-symmetry requirement** of the parent paper — condition (c) alone is sufficient for the descent to identify the data distribution.

**Feasibility demo: Sinkhorn-divergence drift.** With `S_ε(q,p) = OT_ε(q,p) − ½ OT_ε(q,q) − ½ OT_ε(p,p)` (debiased entropic OT, Feydy et al. 2018):
```
V_SK(x) ∝ T_{q→p}(x) − T_{q→q}(x)
```
where `T_{q→p}, T_{q→q}` are barycentric projections of the entropic optimal couplings — an attractive term toward `p` and a self-repulsion term mirroring the `V⁺ − V⁻` structure, but arising from OT geometry rather than kernel mean-shift. On 2D checkerboard converges to SW 1.42×10⁻², comparable to the Laplacian-kernel drift (2.07×10⁻²) (Fig 5).

**This is the doorway for audio-perceptual drifts.** Anything we know how to turn into a smooth, particle-differentiable, positive-definite divergence becomes a drift with convergence theory attached. Candidates: Sinkhorn on Mel/MFCC features, Sliced-Wasserstein on AudioMAE features, Sobolev/MMD-with-perceptual-kernel.

# Architecture / experiments

None at scale. All experiments use **3-layer MLPs with hidden dim 256, Adam lr 1e-3, batch 2048, 50k steps, 1D/2D synthetic targets**:
- Score-matching verification: 4-mode Gaussian mixture, N=50k samples. Empirical mean-shift drift vs analytical `σ² ∇ log(p_σ/q_σ)` agree to mean ℓ₂ error `4.9×10⁻³` at σ=0.3 (Fig 1, App I.1).
- Spectral convergence: tracked modes `k = 1..20` of a small perturbation; per-mode decay rates match Theorem 5.2 across Gaussian, Laplacian, annealed Gaussian (Fig 2).
- Stop-gradient on Swiss roll + checkerboard: jointly tracks `‖V‖` and SW. With SG, `r > 0.95` correlation; without SG, drift collapse demonstrated (Figs 3, 4).
- Sinkhorn drift vs Laplacian drift on checkerboard (Fig 5).

# Inference cost

**No new inference cost.** Everything here is a training-time analysis of the parent drifting recipe. At inference it remains 1-NFE forward of `f_θ`. The annealing schedule changes training-time σ but doesn't touch the inference graph. The Sinkhorn-divergence drift adds per-step training cost (running Sinkhorn iterations on the mini-batch), but still single-step at inference.

# How this translates to raw audio

The parent paper's audio translation discussion (`papers/2602.04770.md`) is the right starting point — none of it is invalidated. What this theory companion *adds* for raw audio:

## 1. Kernel choice is now a hard constraint, not a hyperparameter

Audio has substantial energy at high frequencies. The Gaussian kernel's `σ²|ξ|² e^{-σ²|ξ|²/2}` decay rate caps how fast any mode above `|ξ| ~ 1/σ` can train, exponentially. For a 22.05 kHz waveform mode at, say, 5 kHz to be "in the band", we need σ small enough that `σ · (2π · 5000) ~ 1` → σ ~ 30 μs in time-axis units. At that bandwidth `K_max² σ²` is small for the targeted band but the Gaussian's `T_ε ∝ exp(σ² K_max² / 2)` constant prefactor still kicks the convergence time up. **Use the Laplacian kernel for any direct-waveform drifting experiment.** The polynomial `τ^{d+1} K_max^{d-1}` scaling is the only kernel that touches high frequencies in reasonable wall-clock training time.

In φ-space (which is where the parent paper actually trains): the relevant "frequencies" are now coordinates in the encoder's feature space, not raw audio frequencies. The argument still applies but at lower effective dimensionality. The Laplacian preference remains a safe default.

## 2. The Sinkhorn-drift template unlocks audio-perceptual training objectives

The parent paper's drift is kernel-based; this paper proves that anything smooth + positive + zero-iff-`p=q` is admissible. So we can build:

- **Sinkhorn-on-Mel drift.** `F[q] = S_ε(q, p)` computed on Mel-feature representations of batch samples. Inherits JKO guarantees. Per-step cost is one Sinkhorn iteration on the mini-batch.
- **Sliced-Wasserstein-on-φ drift.** `F[q] = SW(φ(q), φ(p))` with φ an audio encoder. Same template, cheaper than full Sinkhorn.
- **Multi-scale Laplacian + Sinkhorn combo.** Stack drifts; each one is a valid gradient flow on its own functional; the sum is the flow of the sum.

This sidesteps the parent paper's empirical hyperparameter sweep over kernels and gives us a *principled* path to audio-aware drifts.

## 3. Stop-gradient is non-negotiable

The implication for code: any audio replication of drifting must use stop-grad exactly as specified. The drift-collapse failure mode (loss → 0 with no distributional movement) is a real failure mode that will look like "the model is training" if you only watch loss. Track a sliced-Wasserstein on a held-out batch in parallel — that's the actual progress signal. (App I.3.)

## 4. Anneal the bandwidth

If we use a Gaussian kernel (e.g. for simple toy experiments or because some φ-distance happens to be Gaussian-like), schedule σ exponentially. For Laplacian, multi-bandwidth (the parent paper's `τ ∈ {0.02, 0.05, 0.2}` summed) plays a similar role of activating different frequency bands.

## 5. CPU inference is unchanged

Nothing here changes inference latency. The training-side improvements may reduce wall-clock training time for any audio-Drifting experiment (faster spectral convergence → fewer steps to good FAD) but the headline 1-NFE inference target is unaffected.

# Strengths

- **Closes all three of Drifting's open theoretical questions** with one identity. Identifiability, kernel choice, stop-gradient — all derived, not conjectured.
- **Mechanistic explanation of kernel preference.** Not "we grid-searched and Laplacian won"; Landau damping says *why*, with explicit dispersion relations.
- **Free training-side speedup** via exponential annealing — `O(log K_max)` vs `exp(O(K_max²))`.
- **Generalises the recipe.** The `V = -∇ δF/δq` template removes the anti-symmetry constraint and turns drift design into divergence design. Sinkhorn-drift demo proves the template is not vacuous.
- **Unifies stop-gradient across self-referential targets** (DQN, MoCo, BYOL) under one variational principle, which is a real conceptual win even if it's a "remark" in this paper.

# Limitations

- **No high-d validation.** The annealing schedule is proven on 1D modes and validated on 1D/2D toys. Whether `exp → log` actually plays out on ImageNet-scale (or audio-scale) is the most pressing open empirical question — the paper says so explicitly.
- **Linearisation regime.** The spectral analysis holds for `δ ≪ p` and local homogeneity. Early training is far from equilibrium and outside the analysis.
- **Local-homogeneity approximation.** Theorem 5.2 needs `p_σ ≈ const` locally, which is fine for smooth `p` away from mode boundaries but breaks at mode interfaces of e.g. multi-modal data — exactly the interesting regions.
- **No experiments on the parent paper's recipe at scale.** Integrating exponential annealing into the full Deng et al. ImageNet pipeline is listed as future work, not done here.

# Open questions for this project

1. **Direct test of kernel choice on 1D waveform.** Set up a tiny 1D-drifting experiment on a synthetic harmonic / drum-like signal corpus, with Gaussian vs Laplacian kernel at matched bandwidths. Plot per-frequency-band convergence times. We expect to *see* Landau damping for the Gaussian in the high-frequency bins.
2. **Build a Sinkhorn-on-audio-φ drift.** Smallest experiment: SK divergence on Mel-spectrogram features of mini-batches. Compare convergence to a Laplacian-kernel drift on the same φ.
3. **Annealing schedule in φ-space.** Parent paper uses three fixed τ values summed. Does an *exponential* schedule on a single Laplacian (`τ(t) = τ_0 e^{-rt}`) match or beat the multi-bandwidth sum, at lower training cost?
4. **Stop-grad diagnostic for any future drift variant.** Always run paired SW-vs-‖V‖ correlation — if it decorrelates, something's broken in the JKO discretization (Fig 4 protocol).
5. **Multi-scale drift as a kernel-spectrum design tool.** Parent paper's `V^{multi}_{p,q} = Σ_σ V^{(σ)}` is now interpretable as choosing the *effective kernel spectrum* — picking σ values to cover the spectrum of audio frequencies we care about. We could design τ values to flatten the dispersion relation `λ(ξ) ∝ |ξ|² κ̂(ξ)` over the audio band of interest.
