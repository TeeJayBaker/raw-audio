# TL;DR — why this matters for raw-audio CPU gen

A **genuinely off-tree training paradigm** for the from-scratch one-/few-step zoo. Every other member of that zoo (MeanFlow, iMF, SoFlow, pMF, IMM, Drifting, consistency-from-scratch, score-distillation, FD-loss) keeps some piece of the *time-conditional / trajectory-following* structure of diffusion/FM — schedules, time embeddings, fixed integration horizons. **EqM throws all of that out**: train a single time-invariant gradient field `f_θ(x)` that is the gradient of an implicit energy landscape with local minima on the data manifold; sample by *gradient descent* on that landscape. No `t` input, no schedule, no fixed NFE. Reports **FID 1.90 on ImageNet 256×256** — beats DiT-XL/2 (2.27), SiT-XL/2 (2.06), and competes with the strongest from-scratch 1-NFE methods at multi-step. For this project's CPU thesis, the load-bearing implication is **sampling-as-optimization**: per-sample adaptive compute (stop when the gradient norm drops below a threshold), free choice of step size, drop-in Nesterov acceleration, and **coarse-then-fine early-stopping is mathematically natural** rather than a hack. EqM reports cutting total sampling compute to **40% of the fixed-step baseline** at comparable FID via the adaptive-compute mechanism alone. Image-domain, no audio benchmark, no CPU benchmark — but it is the **only published recipe in this wiki where the inference budget is per-sample-adaptive and not fixed at training time**, and that property is the right shape for an interactive CPU audio UI.

# What problem does it solve

The from-scratch 1-/few-step family (MeanFlow, iMF, SoFlow, pMF, IMM, Drifting) all reformulate the time-conditional dynamics of diffusion/FM to either collapse multiple denoising steps into one or to bake the trajectory into a single training objective. **They all still inherit the non-equilibrium framing** — the model is conditioned on `t` (or sampled with a fixed-horizon integrator), and at inference the user must commit to an NFE budget chosen at training time.

EqM asks: can we just drop the time conditioning entirely? Naive approach (Sun et al. 2025, *Is Noise Conditioning Necessary for Denoising Generative Models?*) shows that simply removing `t` from a Flow Matching objective **degrades generation quality**. EqM diagnoses this as an incompatibility between the FM target (a time-varying velocity field) and the equilibrium framing (a single time-invariant field), and constructs a new target family — gradients of an implicit energy landscape — that is *intrinsically* time-invariant and converges to the data manifold via gradient descent.

The result is a method that:

1. **Has no `t` input**, no schedule, no fixed NFE — sample with any optimizer (vanilla GD, Nesterov AGM, even ODE integrators as a special case).
2. **Supports per-sample adaptive compute** — stop when `‖∇E(x)‖₂ < g_min`; uses up to 60% less total compute than fixed-step sampling at comparable FID.
3. **Is a competitive EBM** at scale, with the byproduct properties EBMs are valued for: OOD detection from the energy value, sample composition by adding energy landscapes, partially-noised-image denoising.

# Method — the part that distinguishes it from neighbours

## The training target: time-invariant gradient of an implicit energy

EqM replaces FM's time-conditional velocity target `x - ε` (which varies along the path) with a **single time-invariant field** that points "downhill" toward the data manifold from anywhere in the corruption neighbourhood and **vanishes at the manifold**.

Define the forward corruption `x_γ = γ x + (1 − γ) ε` for `γ ∈ [0, 1]` uniformly (note: `γ` is **not** seen by the network — it's purely a training-time interpolation factor, the analogue of FM's `t` but never an input). The target gradient at `x_γ` is:

```
target(x_γ) = (ε − x) · c(γ)
```

where the **direction** `(ε − x)` always points from data toward noise (the gradient of the implicit energy at that interpolated point should descend back toward data, hence the loss minimises `f - target` with this direction; the gradient `−f` *descends* `x` toward data), and the **magnitude** `c(γ)` controls how steep the landscape is at distance `γ` from data. The training objective:

```
L_EqM = E_{x, ε, γ} || f_θ(x_γ) − (ε − x) c(γ) ||²        (Eq. 3)
```

The key design constraint is `c(1) = 0`: at `γ = 1` (clean data), the target gradient vanishes — real samples are stationary points of the implicit energy. Compare with FM, which requires `∫₀¹ c(γ) dγ = 1` for the velocity field to integrate to a valid transport map. **The constraints are different, and that is the whole technical thesis of the paper.**

When `c(γ) = 1`, EqM reduces exactly to the *negation* of FM's velocity target — so EqM and FM are not the same field in any limit; they sit on different sides of the "should the field vanish at data?" choice.

Three concrete choices for `c(γ)` are tested:

- **Linear:** `c_linear(γ) = 1 − γ`. Cleanest; produces a single basin around each data point.
- **Truncated decay:** `c_trunc(γ) = 1` for `γ ≤ a`, then linearly to 0 over `[a, 1]`. The flat region keeps the gradient magnitude constant far from data, so descent moves at a consistent speed when noisy. **Best choice empirically (`a = 0.8`).**
- **Piecewise:** `c_piece(γ) = b − (b−1)γ/a` for `γ ≤ a`, then `(1−γ)/(1−a)` — starts higher than 1 close to noise. Slightly worse than truncated decay.

A global **gradient multiplier** `λ` scales the whole field; tuned to **λ = 4** as default.

## Implicit vs explicit energy

The default model `f_θ(x)` is an *implicit* gradient field — no scalar energy is constructed; the network output is the gradient. An **explicit energy** variant `EqM-E` parameterises a scalar `g(x) → ℝ`, trains `∇g(x_γ) = (ε − x) c(γ)`. Two ways to derive `g` from an existing `f`:

- **Dot product:** `g(x) = x · f(x)` — gives `∇g = f(x) + x ∇f(x)`. Best empirically.
- **Squared L2:** `g(x) = -½‖f(x)‖²` — gives `∇g = −f(x) ∇f(x)`. Sensitive to initialisation; needs warm-start from a pretrained EqM model.

For pure generation, the implicit variant suffices and is cheaper. The explicit variant is required for the byproduct properties (OOD detection by reading off `g(x)`, sample composition by summing two scalar energies).

## Sampling — gradient descent on the learned landscape

This is where the paradigm fundamentally departs. Sampling is an *optimization* of `E(x)` starting from noise `x_0 ~ N(0, I)`.

**Vanilla gradient descent (GD):**

```
x_{k+1} = x_k − η ∇E(x_k) = x_k − η f_θ(x_k)
```

`η` is a free hyperparameter. **Not coupled to a schedule** — there is no schedule.

**Nesterov-Accelerated Gradient (NAG-GD):**

```
x_{k+1} = x_k − η f_θ(x_k + μ (x_k − x_{k−1}))
```

with momentum factor `μ`. **Best empirical sampler** (table 2: FM-Euler 2.10 → EqM-NAG-GD 1.90 at the same model size). Notably, **NAG is a stock optimization technique from 1983** applied unchanged — borrowing decades of optimization literature is now a sampler-design avenue.

**Adaptive-compute sampling:** loop until `‖f_θ(x_k)‖₂ < g_min`. Per-sample step count varies. Reported: histogram of per-sample step counts on a 1024-sample test produces a distribution from ~50 to ~150 steps with mean such that **total compute = 40% of the fixed 250-step baseline**, FID 33.79 (vs 32.85 fixed). Different samples need different compute, and *the model decides per sample* via the gradient norm.

**ODE samplers as a special case:** Appendix D shows that diffusion's standard Euler-ODE update is a particular `η`-schedule on top of the EqM gradient field — so any ODE sampler can be reused on an EqM model, but GD/NAG-GD with adjustable `η` strictly subsume them.

## Architecture

**No architectural innovation.** Adopts SiT-XL/2 verbatim from Ma et al. 2024 (the same backbone used by SiT, iMF, SoFlow). To eliminate `t` conditioning, **simply set the `t` input to 0** at both train and inference time — no architectural change. This is deliberate: the paper's claim is the *objective* is what matters, not the network.

Sizes (Table 7):

| B/2 | 130 M | 12 | 768 | 2 | 12 |

## Theoretical guarantees

Three statements (informal):

1. **Statement 1 (Learned gradient at ground-truth samples):** under perfect training with `c(1) = 0`, in high-d the limit gives `‖f_θ(x^(i))‖₂ ≈ 0` for any training sample — data points are exact stationary points of the learned gradient.
2. **Statement 2 (Property of local minima):** every local minimum `x̂` of the learned energy satisfies `P(x̂ ∈ X) ≈ 1` — local minima are almost surely training samples. (Proof: in high-d the Gaussian mass at the *exact* training points concentrates at `γ = 1` exponentially fast; combined with `c(1) = 0` this forces minima to coincide with data.)
3. **Statement 3 (Convergence of GD-based sampling):** if the energy is L-smooth and `η ∈ [0, 1/L]`, GD reaches `min_k ‖f(x_k)‖² ≤ 2(E(x_0) − E_inf) / (η K)`.

Together these justify why "gradient descent on the EqM field converges to the data manifold" is not just empirical — it has the same flavour as a standard optimisation theorem.

# Training objective recap

```
L_EqM = E_{x ~ D, ε ~ N(0,I), γ ~ U(0,1)} || f_θ(γ x + (1-γ) ε) − (ε − x) · c(γ) ||²
```

with `c(γ) = λ · c_trunc(γ; a=0.8)` and `λ = 4` as defaults.

Sample with NAG-GD:

```
x_{k+1} = x_k − η · f_θ(x_k + μ (x_k − x_{k−1}))
```

`η = 0.0017` (XL/2), `μ = 0.3`, ~250 steps for fixed schedule, or adaptive until `‖f‖₂ < g_min = 10`.

# Inference cost

**No fixed NFE.** This is the structural departure that matters for our CPU thesis.

- Fixed-step: comparable to FM in their experiments (~250 steps for the headline FID 1.90). Per-step cost = a forward through SiT-XL/2 ≈ 119 GFLOPs (SiT pricing); 250 steps × 119 = ~30 TFLOPs per image.
- **Adaptive-compute:** ~40% of the fixed budget at FID 33.79 (B/2, the small ablation model) vs 32.85 fixed; on XL/2 the gain is not directly tabulated but the paper claims **"up to 60% reduction"**.

**The interesting cost-side observation:** with NAG-GD the sampler converges *visibly faster than FM Euler at the same step size* (Fig. 4 shows EqM produces realistic samples at intermediate steps where FM is still pure noise). This is the **"sampler enters basin of attraction earlier"** property of gradient descent over a well-shaped landscape vs Euler integration of a velocity field — the descent is monotonically reducing energy, while Euler must traverse the full path even if the structure forms early.

Per-sample step count varies — Fig. 8 shows a histogram peaking around 100 steps but with a long tail to 150+; the model is implicitly performing a "this sample needs more compute" decision.

**Scalability (Fig. 3):** EqM outperforms FM **at every tested scale** across (epochs, param count, patch size) — not a regime-specific win. Across S/B/L/XL, EqM stays uniformly below FM's FID curve.

**Sampling-process visualisation (Fig. 4):** at the same step size `η = 0.004`, EqM produces recognizable image content several steps *before* FM does — visual evidence of the "faster basin entry" claim.

**Step-size robustness (Fig. 7):** FM has a single sharp optimum in `η`; EqM's FID is roughly flat over a wide range. This is consistent with the optimization view: gradient descent on a smooth landscape tolerates many step sizes; ODE integration of a velocity field needs the exact `η = 1/N`.

**Ablations (Tables 3–5):**

- `c_trunc` with `a = 0.8` is best (FID 38.34 on B/2 at 80 epochs). Linear `c(γ) = 1 − γ` and piecewise are close but worse.
- `λ = 4` improves FID significantly over `λ = 1`.
- The EqM target **only helps in the noise-unconditional case** (Table 4: noise-conditional + `c_trunc` is 41.89, *worse* than noise-unconditional + `c_trunc` 32.85). This is consistent with the paper's central thesis — `c_trunc` is the right target for an equilibrium landscape, not for a time-varying velocity.
- For the explicit-energy variant, dot-product `g(x) = x · f(x)` beats squared-L2 `g(x) = -½‖f(x)‖²` (FID 73.40 vs 75.53 vs 57.54 for implicit). Explicit energy *underperforms* implicit for raw generation; it earns its keep on byproduct properties.

**Negative result on CIFAR-10 (Table 8):** EqM (FID 3.36) beats noise-unconditional FM (3.96) but loses to standard FM (2.09). The authors attribute this to extensive over-optimisation of the FM CIFAR-10 baseline (noise schedule, sampler scheduling). On ImageNet — where neither method's hyperparameters are over-tuned — EqM wins clearly.

# Unique properties (the EBM revival angle)

EqM is a competitive EBM, which buys properties FM doesn't have:

- **Partially-noised image denoising (Fig. 10):** start sampling from a moderately-noised image rather than pure noise. EqM's FID *improves* as the starting noise level drops (the corruption is now closer to a local minimum); FM's FID *degrades* because FM expects a specific noise level at start.
- **OOD detection (Table 6):** dot-product EqM-E achieves the best average AUROC across SVHN/Textures/constant tested as OOD vs CIFAR-10 ID — beats IGEBM (the previous best EBM on this benchmark), suggesting EqM-E really has learned a useful scalar energy landscape rather than just a generative gradient.
- **Composition (Fig. 11):** sum two class-conditional gradients (`f_panda + f_valley`) and run GD — yields images that combine both concepts. This is exactly the EBM-composition property (`E_total = E_1 + E_2` ⇒ joint sampling) that diffusion was shown to support only approximately and at higher cost (Du et al. 2023). EqM gets it for free.

# How this translates to raw audio

This is an image-domain paper with no audio experiments and no CPU benchmark. Three independent threads to think about.

## 1. Does the equilibrium objective transfer to 1-D audio?

The objective is dimension-agnostic. `(ε − x) · c(γ)` is defined for any data dimensionality; the corruption `x_γ = γ x + (1 − γ) ε` is identical to FM's interpolant. The only thing audio-specific would be the *kernel* of the implicit energy — but EqM never instantiates one explicitly, it only enforces the gradient. So in principle this drops onto a 1-D conv U-Net or 1-D ViT directly.

**Question worth asking:** does the equilibrium property hold for high-dimensional, signed, oscillatory data the way it does for natural images? The Statement-1/2 derivation depends on high-d Gaussian concentration around training points being so sharp that "any local minimum is a training sample" with probability ~1. For ImageNet `d ≈ 196 000` (256×256×3). For 1 s at 22.05 kHz mono, `d = 22 050` — an order of magnitude smaller. The concentration argument *weakens* in lower dimensions. **Whether the "ground-truth samples are stationary points" property survives at audio dimensionality is the first empirical question.**

## 2. Sampling-as-optimization and CPU adaptive compute

This is the most directly relevant feature of EqM for the project. The CPU-inference target is currently constrained by a fixed NFE chosen at training time (or, in 1-NFE methods, by a fixed forward pass). **EqM is the only method in the wiki where the inference budget is per-sample-adaptive and not fixed in advance.** Implications:

- **Coarse-then-fine on CPU:** start with large `η`, take a few coarse steps, then drop `η` near the minimum. Mathematically natural (it's just optimization with a decaying learning rate); for FM/diffusion this is a heuristic with no convergence story. For audio, the "coarse structure first, fine details later" decomposition has a perceptual interpretation — coarse = envelope/onset, fine = timbral detail. Right shape for an interactive UX where the user can hear an intermediate output and abort.
- **Per-sample early stopping:** stop when `‖f_θ(x)‖₂ < g_min`. On audio this would mean different clips get different compute — simple/percussive samples might converge in ~50 steps, complex/sustained samples in ~150. Currently no audio method offers this; everything is fixed-NFE.
- **Nesterov / Adam / other optimizers as free upgrades:** the sampler is *just* an optimizer. Anything that accelerates gradient descent on a smooth landscape applies. Worth testing on audio: Nesterov is decisive on ImageNet (FID 1.93 → 1.90 by adding momentum). The optimization literature is large; this opens an inference-side design space FM/diffusion doesn't.

**However:** EqM uses ~250 steps for the headline result, and even the adaptive-compute mode lands at ~100. **EqM is not a 1-NFE method.** It's a *flexible-NFE* method. The CPU question is: can it be made few-step on audio? The answer depends on (a) how aggressive `η` can be without overshooting and (b) whether the basin geometry permits 4–8 step descent on the audio manifold. **Untested.**

## 3. Phase, stereo, perceptual concerns

The implicit energy is a scalar over all of `ℝᴰ`; nothing in the formulation distinguishes phase from amplitude or mono from stereo. The standard audio failure modes apply:

- **Phase-blind landscape:** if the network is biased toward magnitude features (via a mel-domain loss or STFT-magnitude prior), the energy landscape will have flat phase directions and produce phase-incoherent samples. **MR-STFT regression alone is the wrong shape — needs a phase-sensitive component or training in waveform space directly.**
- **Stereo:** drift field is in `ℝᴰ`, `D = T × C` for `C` channels. Nothing blocks stereo; gradient descent on the joint landscape should respect inter-channel structure if training data is stereo. Empirical question whether the basins remain well-separated.
- **Long-clip scaling:** 10 s @ 22.05 kHz = 220 500 samples = roughly ImageNet 256² dimensionality. Concentration arguments back in play. 0.1 s @ 22.05 kHz = 2 205 — very low dim; the geometric concentration argument may break down.

## 4. What this stacks with

EqM is structurally orthogonal to several recipes already in the wiki:

- **Patch-size-scaling at fixed token count (pMF / EPG):** EqM uses standard SiT-XL/2 with 2×2 patches. Applying patch-size scaling for variable-length audio is purely architectural and stacks straight onto EqM.
- **Frequency-axis multi-band batching (RFWave):** the equilibrium objective is dimension-agnostic; if subbands are batched into a single shared backbone, the EqM loss is computed per-subband and summed identically to how RFWave does it for FM.
- **FD-loss post-training (2604.28190):** EqM produces a fixed-NFE multi-step generator. Post-training that base with FD-loss to push it down to 1-NFE is the same pattern that works for iMF and pMF. **But:** this throws away EqM's adaptive-compute mechanism, which is the headline property. **The interesting question is whether you can train a *single-step EqM* directly, not post-train.** No precedent.
- **GAN fine-tune (Flow2GAN, PeriodWave-Turbo):** orthogonal in principle. The FM-pretrain-then-GAN-finetune recipe could replace FM with EqM. Untested.

## 5. Where this lands relative to other from-scratch families

| | EqM | Drifting | iMF/SoFlow/pMF | IMM | EBM (classical) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Trains a time-conditional field? | **No** | No | Yes (or fastforward t input) | Yes (s, r, t) | No |
| Needs `t` / schedule at inference? | **No** | No | No (1-NFE) | Yes (s, t pair) | No |
| Needs a teacher? | No | No | No | No | No |
| Needs a frozen φ? | **No** | Yes | No | No | No |
| NFE at inference | flexible (adaptive) | 1 | 1 | 2-8 | many (Langevin) |
| Has scalar energy? | optional (EqM-E) | no (gradient field) | no | no | yes |
| Supports composition / OOD? | **Yes (EqM-E)** | no | no | no | yes |
| FID @ ImageNet-256 | 1.90 (many-step) | 1.54 (1-step latent) | 1.72 / 2.22 (1-step) | 1.99 @ 8-step | hard to scale |
| Train stability | "easy to train" | needs careful φ | needs network-indep target | needs `M ≥ 2` | famously unstable |

EqM occupies the **only cell** in this comparison with "no teacher, no `t` conditioning, no φ requirement, supports per-sample adaptive compute, has scalar energy as bonus property". This is genuinely new territory.

It is **not** competitive at 1-NFE — the headline 1.90 is multi-step (~250 NFE adaptive, vs Drifting's 1.54 at 1 NFE). The trade is: give up the strict 1-NFE win, get flexible inference budget + EBM properties.

# Strengths

- **Genuinely new training paradigm.** Discards the time-conditional dynamics structure that every other method in the wiki preserves at some level.
- **EBM revival at competitive scale.** First credible EBM-flavoured method at ImageNet 256² FID parity with diffusion/FM. Sidesteps classical EBM training instability (no contrastive divergence, no Langevin sampling during training).
- **Sampling-as-optimization.** Free choice of sampler from the optimization literature; Nesterov, Adam, etc. all drop in. Per-sample adaptive compute via gradient-norm thresholding.
- **Step-size robustness.** Flat FID over a wide `η` range (Fig. 7). FM has a single sharp optimum. For deployment this matters: less tuning sensitivity, easier defaults.
- **Implicit energy = composition, OOD detection, partial-noise denoising for free.** All three are byproducts not requiring extra training.
- **Trivial implementation.** Two changes to a FM training loop: drop `t` conditioning, multiply target by `c(γ)`. No new modules, no JVP, no kernel, no φ, no SSL stage, no teacher.

# Limitations / things to test

- **Not 1-NFE.** Headline FID needs ~250 GD steps; adaptive sampling cuts to ~100. The strict 1-NFE methods (Drifting, iMF, pMF) beat EqM at single-step. For our CPU thesis, **EqM is a few-step / many-step method with adaptive budget, not a 1-step method.**
- **CIFAR-10 regression.** EqM loses to FM on CIFAR-10 (3.36 vs 2.09). The authors attribute this to FM's CIFAR-10 hyperparameters being over-tuned; the more honest reading is "EqM may be regime-specific" — works well at ImageNet-256 scale where neither method is over-tuned, may not at smaller scales. For audio at 1 s @ 22 kHz (`d ≈ 22 k`, smaller than ImageNet's 196 k), this is a real concern.
- **High-dimensional concentration argument.** Statements 1 and 2 lean on high-d Gaussian concentration. The argument weakens at lower dimensions. Audio's `d` sits in between CIFAR (`d = 3072`) and ImageNet (`d ≈ 196 000`).
- **No CPU benchmark.** Adaptive compute is reported only as "~40% of fixed-step compute"; the absolute wall-clock per sample is not given. For our project, the **CPU wall-clock at audio sequence lengths is the headline number we'd need.**
- **No comparison to from-scratch 1-NFE methods.** The paper compares EqM to multi-step DiT/SiT/VDM++ but not to iMF, SoFlow, pMF, or Drifting. At 1-NFE EqM is presumably much worse than those; the paper doesn't quantify.
- **Truncated decay `c_trunc` is hyperparameter-sensitive.** `a` and `λ` both matter (Table 3). Audio-domain values will need re-tuning.

# Open questions for this project

1. **Smallest few-step EqM on audio.** Concrete experiment: train a 1-D conv U-Net (CRASH-style) or small 1-D ViT with the EqM objective at 1 s @ 22 kHz, 4–16 sampling steps with NAG. Compare CPU wall-clock to a same-size FM baseline at matched FAD.
2. **Adaptive-compute on audio.** Per-sample step count distribution: do simple (percussive) clips converge faster than complex (sustained) ones? If yes, this is a deployable property — interactive UI can preview after `‖f‖₂ < g_min`, then refine.
3. **Does the equilibrium property survive at audio dimensionality?** Statement 2 (local minima are training samples with `P ≈ 1`) is asymptotic in `d`. Empirical test: train EqM on a small audio corpus, run GD from random starts, check the nearest-neighbour distance from converged points to training set. On ImageNet the nearest neighbours are *not* training samples (Fig. 5) — model generalises. Does that generalisation pattern hold on audio?
4. **Sampler choice on audio.** NAG is decisive on ImageNet. Does it stay decisive on 1-D audio, or do other optimizers (Adam, RMSProp, LBFGS) work better? Audio basins may have different curvature structure than image basins.
5. **Coarse-then-fine `η` schedule for audio.** Decaying-`η` GD is the natural audio analogue of low-pass-then-high-pass synthesis. Test whether large `η` early gives plausible envelopes (onset, attack-decay) and small `η` late refines timbre. If yes, the schedule has an intuitive perceptual mapping.
6. **EqM + GAN fine-tune.** Flow2GAN's recipe is FM-pretrain + GAN-fine-tune at low NFE. Replace FM with EqM. Does the equilibrium structure survive GAN refinement, and does adaptive compute still work after the fine-tune?
7. **EqM + FD-loss post-training.** Does FD-loss post-training preserve the gradient-norm-based stopping criterion, or does it warp the landscape so that `‖f‖₂` is no longer a useful proximity-to-data signal?
8. **Phase coherence.** Train EqM on raw waveform vs on STFT-coefficients. Compare phase artefacts at converged samples. The equilibrium property is meant to hold for whatever data representation you feed it; whether raw waveform's high-frequency components produce well-shaped basins is open.
9. **Composition for audio.** EqM-E's two-energy summation generates "panda + valley" coherent images. The audio analogue: train two class-conditional EqM-E models (e.g. "kick" + "snare"), sum gradients, generate. Does the result sound like a layered kick+snare, or like incoherent noise? Direct test of whether the EBM-composition property transfers.
10. **OOD detection on audio.** EqM-E's energy value separates SVHN/Textures from CIFAR-10 ID. Audio analogue: train on music, evaluate AUROC on speech / environmental sounds. If it works, EqM-E gives a built-in OOD filter for generated samples — useful for QA in a generative audio pipeline.
