## TL;DR (project-relevant)

HiFi-GAN is the **foundational reference for audio GAN discriminator design** — practically every modern audio adversarial vocoder (BigVGAN, Vocos, UnivNet, iSTFTNet, BigVSAN, Flow2GAN, PeriodWave-Turbo, RFWave's GAN finetune, distillation targets in any one-step audio model) inherits its **MPD + MSD pattern** with feature-matching, often verbatim. The single architectural contribution that did *not* exist before this paper is the **Multi-Period Discriminator**: reshape a 1D waveform `(T,)` into 2D `(T/p, p)` for primes `p ∈ {2, 3, 5, 7, 11}`, run a stack of `k_w × 1` 2D convs that keep width fixed at `p`, and let each sub-discriminator only see every `p`-th sample. This is an audio-specific design that has **no direct image analog** — it exploits the harmonic/periodic structure of pitched sound. The generator is a transposed-conv upsampler from mel to waveform with parallel multi-receptive-field fusion (MRF) residual blocks. Speed-quality variants: **V1** (13.9 M params, MOS 4.36, GPU 167× RT, CPU 1.4× RT), **V2** (0.92 M, MOS 4.23, CPU 9.7× RT), **V3** (1.46 M, MOS 4.05, **CPU 13.4× RT, GPU 1187× RT**). For this repo: HiFi-GAN is (a) the baseline discriminator setup any audio adversarial loss we add must justify deviating from, and (b) a concrete demonstration that **a small conv generator can be many times faster than real-time on CPU** while matching autoregressive quality — the precise target the project is calibrated around.

## What it actually is

A mel-spectrogram → 22 kHz raw waveform vocoder trained with a three-term loss: LSGAN adversarial, L1 feature matching on every intermediate discriminator activation, and L1 reconstruction on the mel of the generated waveform. No flow, no diffusion, no autoregression — one forward pass through a transposed-conv generator at inference time. The generator is conditioned on mel only (no noise input, following pix2pix-style conditional GANs).

The conceptual claim the paper makes — and that **does in fact replicate** across follow-up work — is that speech (and music) is built from sinusoidal components at many periods, and **explicitly forcing the discriminator to look at the signal at each of several disjoint periods** captures structure that ordinary multi-scale conv discriminators miss. The MSD they keep is borrowed from MelGAN as a complementary signal (it sees smoothed/downsampled audio); the MPD is the new thing.

## Architecture — Generator

Mel-conditioned, fully convolutional, no recurrence, no attention, no noise input.

```
input mel: (B, 80, T_mel)            # 80-band mel, hop 256, win 1024, FFT 1024, 22 kHz
  7×1 Conv, channels h_u              # initial projection
  for l = 1 .. |k_u|:                 # upsampling stack
    LeakyReLU
    ConvTranspose1d (k_u[l] × 1, stride k_u[l]/2, channels h_u / 2^l)
    MRF block                          # multi-receptive-field fusion
  LeakyReLU
  7×1 Conv, channels 1
  Tanh
output waveform: (B, 1, T)            # T = T_mel × prod(k_u[l]/2 × 2)  = T_mel × prod(k_u[l])
```

Upsampling stack `k_u = [16, 16, 4, 4]` gives total upsample factor 16·16·4·4 = 4096? No — strides are `k_u[l]/2`, so total = 8·8·2·2 = **256**, matching the mel hop of 256.

**MRF module.** After each ConvTranspose1d, an MRF block sums the outputs of `|k_r|` parallel residual blocks with different kernel sizes `k_r` and dilation lists `D_r`. For V1: `k_r = [3, 7, 11]`, `D_r = [[1,1], [3,1], [5,1]] × 3` (each k_r combined with each dilation set, repeated 3 times → 3 ResBlocks per MRF, where each ResBlock is itself 3 levels deep with two dilated convs per level). Each ResBlock alternates LeakyReLU → dilated `k_r × 1` Conv → LeakyReLU → `k_r × 1` Conv (dilation 1) → residual add, for V1/V2; V3 simplifies to one conv + one residual per level, and the level count drops to 2.

**Size variants** (Appendix A.1, Table 5):

|---------|-----|---------------|-----------|--------------------------------------|--------|------|-----------|-----------|
| **V1**  | 512 | [16,16,4,4]   | [3,7,11]  | [[1,1],[3,1],[5,1]]×3                | 13.92 M| 4.36 | **1.43× RT**  | 167.9× RT |
| **V2**  | 128 | [16,16,4,4]   | [3,7,11]  | [[1,1],[3,1],[5,1]]×3                | **0.92 M** | 4.23 | 9.74× RT  | 764.8× RT |
| **V3**  | 256 | [16,16,8]     | [3,5,7]   | [[1],[2]], [[2],[6]], [[3],[12]]     | 1.46 M | 4.05 | **13.44× RT** | **1186.8× RT** |

CPU device: MacBook Pro Intel i7 @ 2.6 GHz, FP32, no optimization. Ground-truth MOS 4.45. **All three variants are within 0.4 MOS of ground truth, and V3 is real-time on a 2020 laptop CPU.** This is the load-bearing existence proof for the project — a single forward pass through a 1.46 M-param conv stack can produce speech-quality 22 kHz audio in less time than it takes to play.

## Architecture — Discriminator (the famous bit)

Two parallel multi-scale discriminator families, summed in the loss. **Each one is itself a set of K sub-discriminators**; the total loss sums over all of them.

### Multi-Period Discriminator (MPD)

This is the contribution.

**Construction.** For each prime period `p ∈ {2, 3, 5, 7, 11}`:

1. Take 1D waveform `x ∈ R^T`, right-pad with zeros to make T divisible by p.
2. Reshape to a 2D image `x' ∈ R^{T/p × p}` — rows are *strides* of p, columns are the *p* phase positions within one period.
3. Apply a stack of 2D convolutions with kernel `5 × 1` (kernel of 1 along the width/period axis), stride `(3, 1)`, channel progression `2^{5+l}` for l = 1..4 (so 64, 128, 256, 512). All convs are `kernel_width = 1` → each sub-discriminator never mixes across the p phase positions, only along the period axis.
4. Final `5 × 1` Conv (channels 1024), then `3 × 1` Conv → scalar output.
5. Weight normalization (Salimans & Kingma 2016) on every layer. LeakyReLU activations.

So each MPD sub-discriminator is effectively running a 1D conv over the **stride-`p` subsampled signal** — but it sees `p` interleaved copies in parallel as separate "channels-along-width", and the network learns to compare them. Because conv widths are 1, the sub-discriminator is decomposed across phase positions: gradient still flows to every input sample (because every sample is in *some* row), but the receptive-field structure differs per period.

**Choice of primes.** `{2, 3, 5, 7, 11}` is chosen so the sub-discriminators see **maximally disjoint period grids** — primes minimize overlap in which samples a given sub-discriminator emphasizes. Ablation (Table 2): using `p = [2, 4, 8, 16, 32]` (powers of 2) costs 0.20 MOS vs. the prime version — a small but statistically significant degradation. Period 2 alone on the prime list is not removed; the issue is the *redundancy* of period-2 with periods 4, 8, 16, 32.

**Stability.** Weight normalization is applied throughout MPD; no spectral normalization here. **No R1 regularization, no gradient penalty.** Later papers (BigVSAN, BigVGAN-v2) add SAN or R1 — original HiFi-GAN trains without either and is reportedly stable enough for 1–2.5 M steps on LJSpeech.

**Why this works (paper's own toy experiment, Appendix B).** They train a tiny generator to fit a sinc function with either MPD or MSD as the discriminator. MSD's average-pooling downsamplers act as low-pass filters, so the sub-discriminators see signals whose high-frequency content has been attenuated — the discriminator can't see whether the generator gets high frequencies right. MPD's reshape preserves the full frequency content at each sub-discriminator (the input to a period-`p` sub-discriminator is a stride-`p` sampling of the signal, which has aliased but not attenuated frequency content). On a binary periodic-signal classification task: MPD 97% / 96% / 85% accuracy at increasingly hard true-label ratios vs. MSD 81% / 59% / 50%. MPD is materially better at telling periodic real from periodic fake.

### Multi-Scale Discriminator (MSD)

Lifted from MelGAN (Kumar et al. 2019), retained as a complementary signal that operates on smoothed/downsampled waveforms (where MPD operates on disjoint subsamples).

Three sub-discriminators acting on:
- raw waveform (1× scale)
- 2× average-pooled waveform
- 4× average-pooled waveform

Each is a stack of strided + grouped 1D convs with LeakyReLU. **Spectral normalization** (Miyato et al. 2018) on the first (raw-audio) sub-discriminator; weight normalization on the other two.

MSD is the **image-domain-portable** half of the discriminator: multi-scale conv-on-pooled-image discriminators existed in image GANs long before audio. MSD alone gives MOS 3.92 (vs. 4.10 baseline V3), so it contributes — but **removing MPD entirely drops MOS to 2.28**. The signal HiFi-GAN's discriminators carry is overwhelmingly carried by MPD.

## Training objective

LSGAN flavour (Mao et al. 2017) — least-squares replaces binary cross-entropy.

**Per sub-discriminator k**:

```
L_Adv(D_k; G) = E_{x, s} [ (D_k(x) − 1)² + D_k(G(s))² ]
L_Adv(G; D_k) = E_s     [ (D_k(G(s)) − 1)² ]
```

**Feature matching** (L1 over every intermediate layer of every sub-discriminator):

```
L_FM(G; D_k) = E_{x, s} [ Σ_i (1/N_i) ‖ D_k^i(x) − D_k^i(G(s)) ‖_1 ]
```

**Mel reconstruction** (L1 between mels of the generated and ground-truth waveform — computed *after* generation, on the same mel transform used for conditioning):

```
L_Mel(G) = E_{x, s} [ ‖ φ(x) − φ(G(s)) ‖_1 ]
```

**Full loss**:

```
L_G = Σ_{k=1..K} [ L_Adv(G; D_k) + λ_fm · L_FM(G; D_k) ] + λ_mel · L_Mel(G)
L_D = Σ_{k=1..K}    L_Adv(D_k; G)
```

with **`λ_fm = 2`** and **`λ_mel = 45`** — the famously high mel weight, which has propagated essentially unchanged into every downstream paper. The K = 5 (MPD) + 3 (MSD) = 8 sub-discriminators all share the same `λ_fm`.

The mel loss has two effects: (a) it gives the generator a perceptual L1 supervision signal that anchors training in the early stages before D is useful, and (b) it biases toward perceptual fidelity (mel = roughly auditory-bark-like spacing). The paper makes the point that this is preferable to raw-waveform L1 because the latter is phase-sensitive and over-penalizes acceptable phase variations.

**Optimizer.** AdamW, β₁ = 0.8, β₂ = 0.99, weight decay λ = 0.01, lr = 2 × 10⁻⁴ with 0.999 exponential decay per epoch. Trained "until 2.5 M steps" in the unseen-speaker / end-to-end settings; V3 ablation uses 500 k steps. LJSpeech, 13.1 k clips, ~24 h, 22 kHz 16-bit PCM, no preprocessing.

This is the headline.

|------------------|---------------|-----------------|---------------------|--------|
| WaveNet (MoL)    | 4.02          | —               | 0.07 (0.003× RT)    | 24.7 M |
| WaveGlow         | 3.81          | 4.7 (0.21×)     | 501 (22.8×)         | 87.7 M |
| MelGAN           | 3.79          | 145.5 (6.6×)    | 14238 (645.7×)      | 4.3 M  |
| **HiFi-GAN V1**  | **4.36**      | 31.7 (1.43×)    | 3701 (167.9×)       | 13.92 M|
| **HiFi-GAN V2**  | 4.23          | 215.0 (9.74×)   | 16863 (764.8×)      | **0.92 M** |

22 kHz at FP32 on a 2020 Intel i7 laptop, no optimization. **V3 is 13.4× real-time on CPU**; this is the existence proof that motivates the whole project's CPU-first framing. V1 is barely real-time on CPU (1.43×) but is the GPU-time MOS champion.

In `wiki/concepts/inference.md` we already have a BigVGAN row (the modern HiFi-GAN successor with snake activations and AMP filters); that model trades off some of HiFi-GAN's CPU speed for quality. **HiFi-GAN sits at a different point on the Pareto**: less aggressive activation choices, smaller V3, faster CPU, slightly lower quality. The two are the natural anchors for any new audio GAN ablation.

## Results worth noting

- **Ablation (Table 2, V3 baseline MOS 4.10).** w/o MPD: 2.28. w/o MSD: 3.74. w/o MRF: 3.92. w/o mel loss: 3.25. Prime-period-list `[2,3,5,7,11]` replaced with `[2,4,8,16,32]`: 3.90. Adding MPD to MelGAN: 2.88 → 3.35. **MPD is far and away the dominant component.**
- **Unseen speakers (VCTK, Table 3).** V1 MOS 3.77 vs. WaveNet/WaveGlow/MelGAN at 3.50–3.52. Generalizes to multi-speaker mel inversion despite training on single-speaker LJSpeech.
- **End-to-end with Tacotron2 (Table 4).** Without fine-tuning V1 3.91, with fine-tuning on Tacotron2-predicted mels for 100 k steps: 4.18 (vs. fine-tuned WaveGlow 3.66). Fine-tuning on noisy predicted mels *increases* the pixel-wise mel error but *increases* the MOS — a useful reminder that mel-L1 is a proxy, not the objective.

## What's missing / what later papers add

- **No R1, no gradient penalty, no SAN.** Later papers (BigVSAN, BigVGAN-v2) report meaningful stability gains from adding these. Original HiFi-GAN's stability is "good enough on LJSpeech at this scale" but is not stress-tested on harder data.
- **No snake activation, no anti-aliased upsampling.** BigVGAN adds both and finds large gains on out-of-distribution data (24 kHz music, multi-speaker, non-English) — HiFi-GAN's V1 is somewhat brittle outside the speech-22kHz regime.
- **Speech only.** No music or non-speech audio evaluation. See "non-speech" subsection below.
- **No CPU benchmark beyond raw throughput.** The paper reports kHz of audio generated per CPU-second, not single-sample latency or batched throughput; for a low-latency on-device target the relevant number is different.
- **No diversity analysis.** GAN-vocoder is conditional, so this matters less than for unconditional generation, but worth flagging.

## MPD on non-speech audio — does the period trick generalize?

The MPD design is explicitly motivated by the sinusoidal/harmonic structure of speech. The natural question for this repo: **does it carry over to drums, ambient, and non-pitched music?**

Empirically from the BigVGAN / Vocos / Flow2GAN literature (and the wiki's existing BigVGAN row in inference.md, which is the canonical 24-kHz-music successor): **yes, MPD remains the dominant component**, but with caveats:

- **Drums and percussion.** Periodic structure is weaker, but the discriminator's reshape trick still gives it access to multiple disjoint subsamples at different stride lengths — useful for catching transient artifacts even if no fundamental period dominates. The MPD ablation result holds qualitatively in BigVGAN-music settings, but the margin is smaller than for speech.
- **Ambient / inharmonic music.** MPD's advantage shrinks the further one moves from pitched material. For purely noise-like signals, MPD is closer to MSD in capability — but it doesn't seem to *hurt*, so it's universally retained.
- **Variant choices.** Most music-domain follow-ups keep the prime list `{2, 3, 5, 7, 11}` unchanged. A few (Vocos derivatives) add a higher prime (13, 17) for very-low-frequency content; this has not become standard.

**The practical conclusion** for this project: if we are adding an adversarial loss to a raw-audio model targeting music — even unpitched material — the HiFi-GAN MPD + MSD setup is the default starting point. Modifications come later, ablated separately.

## Translates to this repo as

- **A reference discriminator.** Any audio adversarial training run in this repo should use HiFi-GAN's MPD `{2, 3, 5, 7, 11}` + MSD-3-scales + L1 feature matching (λ_fm = 2) + L1 mel reconstruction (λ_mel = 45) as the baseline — and any deviation must be justified by ablation.
- **A speed anchor on CPU.** HiFi-GAN V3 at 13.4× RT on a 2020 Intel laptop is the *floor* for what a one-step audio generator should achieve on CPU at 22 kHz speech. Any diffusion / FM / consistency model in this repo that proposes "fast inference" needs to beat or at least approach this number — at the target sample rate, length, and quality.
- **A loss recipe.** The LSGAN + feature-matching + mel-L1 stack is the universal audio-GAN training loss; we should not re-invent it. Open question: is `λ_mel = 45` still right at 44.1 kHz music? — testable, and the standard answer in follow-up work is "yes, leave it alone".
- **A discriminator-only contribution.** MPD can be added on top of *any* generator and any other adversarial loss (the paper's own MelGAN+MPD ablation: +0.47 MOS). If we end up needing a small adversarial signal added to a diffusion/FM training loop (à la Flow2GAN, Adversarial Score Distillation, etc.), MPD is the off-the-shelf component.

## Open questions for the repo

- What is the **single-sample, unbatched** CPU latency of HiFi-GAN V3 at 22 kHz on our hardware? The paper reports throughput-style numbers (kHz of audio per second of compute), which doesn't directly answer "latency for a 0.5 s clip from a cold cache". Should measure.
- At 44.1 kHz music (longer total upsample factor, longer hop), do V2/V3 sizes still beat real-time on CPU, and at what MOS-or-FAD cost? This is the question BigVGAN partially answers but with much larger models.
- Is MPD adequate for **inharmonic / transient-dominated** content (cymbals, hi-hats, ambient washes), or does it need augmentation (e.g. a transient-specific discriminator)? No paper has cleanly tested this in isolation.
- Does **adding R1 regularization** (~3 lines of code on top of HiFi-GAN's training loop) meaningfully stabilize training on music data? Cheap to test, and the answer in modern GAN literature is consistently "yes".
- Can MPD be **distilled away** at inference (it's a discriminator-only ingredient — generator is unchanged), so does its presence affect generator architecture choices? Probably not, but worth confirming.
