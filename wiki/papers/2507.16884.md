# TL;DR — why this matters for raw-audio CPU gen

SplitMeanFlow is **the cleanest production-grade JVP-free MeanFlow recipe in the wiki**, and the *only* MeanFlow-family paper whose headline result is a deployed speech-synthesis system (ByteDance Doubao TTS, 1–2 NFE, 20× speedup over the 10-NFE Flow Matching baseline + CFG). Its method is a return-to-first-principles: replace MeanFlow's differential identity `u = v − (t−r)·du/dt` with the **purely algebraic Interval Splitting Consistency** `(t−r)·u(z_t, r, t) = (s−r)·u(z_s, r, s) + (t−s)·u(z_t, s, t)` (Eq. 10), which is the immediate consequence of the additivity of definite integrals applied to the integral definition of average velocity. No derivatives anywhere — three forward passes through `u_θ` plus simple algebra produces the regression target. The paper proves that the MeanFlow differential identity is the `s → t` limit of this algebraic identity, so SplitMeanFlow is a **strict generalisation** of MeanFlow rather than a heuristic alternative. For this project the load-bearing facts are: (i) JVP eliminated → FlashAttention / FSDP / any hardware backend that doesn't support `torch.func.jvp` becomes usable for fastforward training, (ii) **already validated as a 1–2-NFE generator on a Seed-TTS-scale acoustic diffusion model deployed at industrial scale on speech audio**, (iii) the training recipe is teacher-distillation-based (warm-start + boundary anchor from a pretrained FM teacher), which matches the realistic pipeline for any pretrained raw-waveform FM teacher we might end up with.

# What problem does it solve

The MeanFlow family parameterises an **average velocity field** `u(z_t, r, t) := (1/(t−r)) ∫_r^t v(z_τ, τ) dτ` so that one-step inference is exactly `z_0 = z_1 − u_θ(z_1, 0, 1)` — no ODE integration needed at inference. The integral is intractable during training, and three independent reformulations have now appeared, each with a different way of avoiding the integral:

| Method | Identity used | Cost at training |
|---|---|---|
| **MeanFlow** (Geng 2025) | Differential: `u = v − (t−r)·du/dt` | One forward + **JVP** for `du/dt` |
| **SoFlow** (2512.15657) | Taylor finite-difference solution consistency | Two forwards (FD over `l ∈ (s,t)`) — JVP-free |
| **SplitMeanFlow** (this paper) | Algebraic: `(t−r)·u(z_t,r,t) = (s−r)·u(z_s,r,s) + (t−s)·u(z_t,s,t)` | Three forwards + algebra — JVP-free |

MeanFlow's JVP is two distinct pains:

1. **Compute overhead** — JVP roughly doubles forward-only cost; the regression target's gradient flows through a second-order computation graph.
2. **Hardware/software compatibility** — `torch.func.jvp` does **not** compose with FlashAttention / memory-efficient SDPA kernels (SoFlow Tab. 5: MeanFlow falls back to math attention, ~50 GB and 2.39 it/s vs SoFlow at 35 GB and 2.94 it/s on identical DiT). FSDP and TPU/NPU backends are similarly affected. For a 1B+ TTS diffusion model on production hardware, this is the limiting factor.

SplitMeanFlow's claim: **the integral's algebraic structure (additivity over sub-intervals) is itself enough to anchor training — you never need the derivative.** The differential identity is just one limit of a more general algebraic relation.

# Method

## (1) The Interval Splitting Consistency identity (Eq. 10)

Start from the definition `u(z_t, r, t) := (1/(t−r)) ∫_r^t v(z_τ, τ) dτ`, so the **displacement** over the interval is `D(r, t) := (t−r)·u(z_t, r, t) = ∫_r^t v(z_τ, τ) dτ`. The additivity of definite integrals gives, for any `r ≤ s ≤ t`,

```
∫_r^t v dτ = ∫_r^s v dτ + ∫_s^t v dτ
```

Substitute the displacement definition on each piece:

```
(t − r) · u(z_t, r, t) = (s − r) · u(z_s, r, s) + (t − s) · u(z_t, s, t).         (Eq. 10)
```

Divide by `t − r` and let `λ := (t−s)/(t−r) ∈ [0,1]` (so `s = (1−λ)t + λr`):

```
u(z_t, r, t) = (1 − λ) · u(z_s, r, s) + λ · u(z_t, s, t).                          (Eq. 11)
```

"Average velocity over `[r,t]` is a length-weighted convex combination of the average velocities over `[r,s]` and `[s,t]`." Algebraic. No differentiation. Holds **for every `s ∈ [r, t]`**, not only at the midpoint (this distinguishes it from Shortcut Models, which use only `s = (r+t)/2` and discrete `d` — see §4.3.1).

## (2) Training algorithm (Algorithm 1, JVP-free, three forward passes)

For each batch:

1. Sample `r, t` with `0 ≤ r ≤ t ≤ 1`; sample `λ ~ U(0, 1)`; set `s = (1−λ)t + λr`.
2. Sample `ε ~ N(0, I)`; construct path point `z_t = (1−t)·x + t·ε`.
3. Forward 1: `u_2 = u_θ(z_t, s, t)`.
4. Construct intermediate state on the trajectory: `z_s = z_t − (t−s)·u_2`.
5. Forward 2: `u_1 = u_θ(z_s, r, s)`.
6. Build target: `target = (1−λ)·u_1 + λ·u_2`.
7. Forward 3 (gradient path): predict `u_θ(z_t, r, t)`; loss `L = ‖u_θ(z_t, r, t) − sg(target)‖²`.
8. Backward through forward 3 only.

The stop-gradient on the target is critical (same role as in MeanFlow / SoFlow / consistency models — avoids trivial degenerate solutions). The boundary condition `u(z_t, t, t) = v(z_t, t)` must be enforced separately (see two-stage recipe below) to prevent the model from collapsing to `u ≡ 0`.

## (3) MeanFlow is the `s → t` limit (Theorem, §4.3.1)

The paper proves that taking `s → t` in Eq. 10 (after rearranging) recovers the MeanFlow differential identity:

```
((t−r) u(z_t,r,t) − (s−r) u(z_s,r,s)) / (t − s) → g'(t)
```

where `g(t) := (t−r)·u(z_t, r, t)`. The LHS is the difference quotient of `g`; its limit is `g'(t)`. The RHS limit is `v(z_t, t)`. Combining gives `g'(t) = u + (t−r)·du/dt = v`, which rearranges to the MeanFlow identity `u = v − (t−r)·du/dt`.

**Implication:** MeanFlow is **literally the infinitesimal-split (`s → t`) case** of SplitMeanFlow. The algebraic identity holds at any finite `s`, the differential identity only at the limit. Shortcut Models (Frans et al. 2024) are a discrete special case at `s = (r+t)/2` only.

## (4) Two-stage training with a teacher (§5.1) — the production recipe

The paper notes SplitMeanFlow can be trained from scratch but found that a **two-stage distillation pipeline** converges much faster and to higher quality for industrial-scale models:

**Stage 1:** Train a standard Flow Matching teacher `M_teacher` with the conditional FM loss — this is just the pretrained TTS diffusion model that already exists at any production shop.

**Stage 2 — distill into a SplitMeanFlow student `M_student`:**

- Initialise student weights from `M_teacher` (warm start).
- For each batch: a fraction `p ≥ 0.5` of samples set `r = t` (boundary anchor) — supervise `u_θ(z_t, t, t) ← v_teacher(z_t, t)` using the teacher's one-step velocity prediction.
- The remaining `1 − p` fraction uses the Interval Splitting Consistency loss (Eq. 11) on student-only forwards.
- CFG dropout on student set to 0; teacher generates `v` at a fixed CFG scale, baking guidance into the student.

The flow ratio `p` is the practical knob: high `p` keeps the student grounded in the teacher's velocity field and stops the self-consistency objective from drifting to trivial solutions. This is structurally the same trick MeanFlow uses (boundary mixing) and is unavoidable for any fastforward family — the consistency identity alone has a `u ≡ 0` solution.

## (5) JVP-free engineering benefits (§4.3.2)

The paper's three engineering claims, in their order:

1. **Simpler implementation** — only standard forward and backward passes; no `torch.func.jvp` or manual primal/tangent plumbing.
2. **Numerical stability** — backpropagating through a JVP requires high numerical precision (the second-order graph amplifies fp16/bf16 error); SplitMeanFlow's algebraic-then-MSE structure is single-order.
3. **Hardware compatibility** — the algebraic recipe runs unmodified on any backend that supports forward + backward (FlashAttention, FSDP, custom inference accelerators, TPU/NPU). This is the load-bearing point for production deployment.

# Architecture and scale

The paper deliberately does **not** introduce a new architecture. The student is the **Seed-TTS framework's acoustic diffusion model** — one of four modules in a large-scale autoregressive TTS pipeline (speech tokenizer + token LM + token-conditioned diffusion model + acoustic vocoder). The "diffusion model" in Seed-TTS is the acoustic-detail generator conditioned on speech tokens; it dominates inference latency (>50% of total even at 10 diffusion steps). No specific parameter count, sequence length, or layer breakdown is reported — Seed-TTS itself ([2406.02430]) is the reference.

The student inherits the teacher's architecture verbatim; the SplitMeanFlow change is only at the training-loss level (plus the `r, s, t` time inputs to the network).

# Inference cost — the headline

Two benchmarks (Tables 1 and 2), both on **Seed-TTS** tasks:

**Seed-TTS_SFT (supervised fine-tune; in-domain TTS):**

| Method | Steps | CFG | SIM ↑ | WER ↓ | CMOS vs. baseline ↑ |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Flow Matching (teacher) | 10 | Y | 0.787 | 0.0551 | 0 |
| DMD | 2 | N | 0.787 | 0.0561 | −0.04 |
| **SplitMeanFlow** | **2** | **N** | **0.789** | 0.0561 | **−0.01** |

**Seed-TTS_ICL (in-context learning; few-shot prosody/voice cloning):**

| Method | Steps | CFG | SIM ↑ | WER ↓ | CMOS vs. baseline ↑ |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Flow Matching (teacher) | 10 | Y | 0.686 | 0.0286 | 0 |
| SplitMeanFlow | 2 | N | 0.681 | 0.0297 | 0 |
| **SplitMeanFlow** | **1** | **N** | **0.685** | **0.0286** | **0** |

The 1-step result on ICL is the strongest: **identical WER (0.0286) and statistically tied SIM (0.685 vs 0.686) and CMOS (0) to the 10-step + CFG teacher**, with CFG dropped entirely. That's a **20× FLOP reduction** (10 steps with CFG = 20 NFE → 1 step no CFG = 1 NFE).

The 2-step SFT result beats DMD ([2405.14867]) under the same step budget on SIM (0.789 vs 0.787) and CMOS (−0.01 vs −0.04). DMD is a score-distillation + GAN hybrid that requires a separate fake-score network and TTUR; SplitMeanFlow is loss-only and doesn't need a discriminator.

**Production deployment.** The paper states the 1-2 NFE SplitMeanFlow models "have been successfully deployed in large-scale speech synthesis products (such as Doubao), demonstrating its practical value and robustness" — Doubao being ByteDance's consumer AI assistant. **No CPU benchmark is reported**, and no GFLOPs/sample number. The implicit inference-time win is the relative 20× speedup over the 10-NFE FM + CFG baseline; absolute CPU latency is not in the paper.

# Position in the JVP-free MeanFlow landscape

The wiki previously framed the JVP problem as "two answers" (iMF keeps it stop-gradded; SoFlow eliminates it via Taylor FD). **SplitMeanFlow is a third independent answer**, and arguably the conceptually cleanest of the three — neither stop-gradded JVP nor finite-difference approximation, but a pure algebraic identity that holds exactly for any `s`. Adjacent to TiM's DDE (central FD on the time derivative of `f_θ`) but different in kind: DDE approximates a derivative; SplitMeanFlow never has one.

| Axis | MeanFlow | iMF | SoFlow | **SplitMeanFlow** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Parameterisation | Avg velocity `u_θ` | Avg velocity `u_θ` | Solution `f_θ` | Avg velocity `u_θ` |
| Identity type | Differential | Differential (stop-grad'd) | Taylor FD | **Algebraic (exact, finite-interval)** |
| JVP at train time? | Yes | Stop-gradded | No | **No** |
| Forward passes per step | 1 + JVP | 1 + JVP | 2 | **3** |
| Boundary condition | Implicit | Implicit | Explicit `s=t` | **Explicit (`r=t` anchor at fraction `p`)** |
| FlashAttn / FSDP compat? | No | No | Yes | **Yes** |
| Training type | From scratch | From scratch | From scratch | **Teacher-distillation (two-stage)** |
| 1-NFE result | 3.43 FID (XL/2, ImageNet-256) | 1.72 FID | 2.96 FID | 0.685 SIM / 0.0286 WER (Seed-TTS_ICL — audio, not ImageNet) |
| Deployed? | No | No | No | **Yes — Doubao TTS** |

SplitMeanFlow trades two things to get its properties: (i) higher per-step training cost (3 forwards vs MeanFlow's 1 + JVP — but JVP roughly doubles cost, so the wall-clock comparison is closer than 3× and *much* better when FlashAttention is unlocked); (ii) heavier dependence on a strong teacher — the paper recommends Stage-2 distillation rather than from-scratch for industrial-scale models. The first is a feature on production hardware; the second is realistic given that any audio-deployment context already has a pretrained FM model.

# How this translates to raw audio

This paper *is* an audio paper — the experiments are on Seed-TTS's acoustic diffusion model. But Seed-TTS operates on **acoustic tokens / mel-frame representations**, not raw waveform samples. The recipe ports to raw-sample-space audio modulo one assumption: that we have a pretrained FM teacher in waveform space to distill from.

What's directly portable:

- **The Interval Splitting Consistency loss (Eq. 11)** is dimension-, transport-, and architecture-agnostic. It applies to any linear-interpolant FM (`z_t = (1−t)x + tε`), which covers the standard waveform FM training recipe.
- **The two-stage training pipeline** (FM teacher → SplitMeanFlow student with boundary anchor and warm start) is the realistic recipe for any waveform model where we *already* have a pretrained FM (CRASH-style, RFWave-style, Flow2GAN's FM-pretrain stage). The teacher provides the boundary `u(z_t, t, t) = v(z_t, t)` supervision; the student does Interval Splitting Consistency on the rest.
- **The flow ratio `p ≥ 0.5`** is the most important hyperparameter. Direct port.
- **CFG handling** — turn off CFG dropout on the student; bake teacher's CFG scale into the supervision. For unconditional waveform generation, CFG is irrelevant and this simplifies further.

What's deployment-relevant for CPU inference:

- **1-NFE TTS on Doubao is the production existence proof for "MeanFlow without JVP works at scale on speech audio"** — the missing data point for the from-scratch fastforward literature, which is otherwise all image and all GPU.
- No CPU benchmark in the paper, but the deployment context (consumer mobile app + cloud serving) means 1-NFE *acoustic-diffusion* inference is fast enough for production at unspecified hardware. Whether the same recipe at full raw-waveform resolution hits the CPU real-time bar is unanswered — but the architecture stays whatever the teacher was, and the inference NFE drops by 5–20×, so the relative CPU win is a direct multiplier on whatever the teacher's CPU latency was.

What we still don't know about audio:

- **CPU latency, GFLOPs/sample, real-time factor** — none reported. Mandatory before claiming a viable CPU recipe.
- **Raw-waveform (sample-space) results** — Seed-TTS works on a learned acoustic representation, not 22-kHz raw samples. The training recipe should transfer cleanly to a CRASH-style 1-D conv U-Net, but the high-frequency / phase-sensitive structure of raw audio may stress the few-step generator differently than acoustic features do.
- **From-scratch ablation** — the paper says training from scratch is possible but distillation converges faster. No from-scratch numbers reported, so we can't tell how much of the deployed quality comes from the teacher's prior vs. the SplitMeanFlow objective itself.
- **`λ ~ U(0,1)` sweep** — the paper picks uniform; whether `λ → 0` or `λ → 1` biased distributions help for shorter / longer audio is not ablated.

# Strengths

- **Simplest JVP-free MeanFlow recipe of the three.** Algebraic identity, three forwards, MSE loss. Nothing else.
- **Strict generalisation of MeanFlow** (proven). MeanFlow falls out as the `s → t` limit; any MeanFlow setup can be upgraded by widening `s` away from `t`.
- **Production-deployed** at industrial scale on speech audio — the only paper in the MeanFlow / iMF / SoFlow / pMF cluster with this property. Strong evidence the recipe is stable and converges on real data at real scale.
- **Realistic two-stage recipe.** Doesn't require teacher-free training and explicitly leverages an existing FM checkpoint, which is the realistic starting point for any audio pipeline.
- **Hardware-portable.** FlashAttention, FSDP, custom inference accelerators all "just work".

# Limitations and open questions

- **No CPU benchmark.** No GFLOPs/sample, no real-time factor, no parameter count. The deployment claim is wall-clock relative to FM baseline only.
- **No from-scratch results.** Cannot separate "SplitMeanFlow objective is good" from "Seed-TTS teacher is good and SplitMeanFlow distills it cleanly". The latter is a weaker claim.
- **No direct comparison to MeanFlow / iMF / SoFlow on the same audio task.** DMD is the only baseline besides FM; the MeanFlow family is not even cited. We can't tell whether the algebraic identity is *better* than the differential one at matched compute, only that it's JVP-free.
- **Per-step training cost is ~3× vs MeanFlow's 1-forward-plus-JVP** — the wall-clock comparison depends on whether FlashAttention is in play. The paper doesn't quantify the training-throughput tradeoff.
- **No `λ ~ U(0,1)` sweep, no `p` sweep, no architecture/condition ablations.** A short paper with one method and one experiment set.
- **No mode-coverage / diversity analysis.** TTS metrics (WER, SIM) are content-fidelity metrics; the diversity-loss failure mode that bites other 1-NFE methods isn't tested.
- **Sample-space (raw waveform) untested.** Acoustic-feature TTS is closer to a learned-latent than to raw audio.

# Things to test in this repo

1. **Reproduce on a small raw-waveform FM teacher.** CRASH-style 1-D conv U-Net trained on drum one-shots → distill with SplitMeanFlow `p = 0.5`, `λ ~ U(0,1)`. Report 1-NFE / 2-NFE FAD, MIND, CPU xRT.
2. **Direct A/B against iMF and SoFlow on the same 1-D backbone.** Same teacher, same compute budget, same data — does the JVP-free algebraic objective match the JVP-stop-gradded iMF v-loss, and does it match the Taylor-FD SoFlow at matched FlashAttention compatibility?
3. **From-scratch SplitMeanFlow on waveform.** Skip the teacher entirely; train with `p = 0` (no boundary anchor — let the consistency identity stand alone), then with `p = 0.5` using an FM auxiliary loss instead of teacher-supplied `v`. Does the algebraic identity converge from random init on audio, or does it require teacher distillation?
4. **`p` and `λ` sweeps.** Production paper picks `p ≥ 0.5` and `λ ~ U(0, 1)` without justification; both are likely audio-dependent.
5. **Hardware comparison.** SplitMeanFlow training on a backbone with FlashAttention enabled vs. MeanFlow training on the same backbone with math attention (forced by JVP). Measure GPU memory, training throughput, samples per dollar.
6. **Compose with DDE (TiM).** SplitMeanFlow's three-forward target construction doesn't need DDE — but if the algebraic identity were generalised to mixed splits at multiple `s` values, central-FD-style targets might be more efficient. Open structural question.
7. **CPU inference at 1-NFE on a deployable audio backbone.** The Doubao deployment is a wall-clock argument, not a per-sample-CPU number. Whatever audio backbone we settle on, the headline metric for this project is **CPU xRT at 1-NFE post-SplitMeanFlow distillation**.
