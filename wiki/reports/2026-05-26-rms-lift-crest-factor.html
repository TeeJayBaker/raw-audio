RMS lifting clips one-shot transients

2026-05-26 · WavFlow amplitude lift on percussive/synth one-shots · n = 20,000 files (samples_from_mars/one_shots)

Question. WavFlow's amplitude lift x_lift = s_a · clamp((r_*/rms)·x, −1, 1) rescales the FM target toward the unit-Gaussian prior. The clamp hard-limits anything whose peak exceeds 1/r_* × RMS — a crest-factor ceiling of 20·log10(1/r_*) dB. WavFlow's default r_*=0.33 sets that ceiling at 9.6 dB. Does that clip the attack transients of drum/synth one-shots?

Measured crest-factor distribution

Per-clip crest factor (peak / RMS, RMS computed exactly as the trainer does, over all channels & time): median 13.6 dB, mean 13.8 dB, p90 19.7 dB, p99 26.0 dB, max 41.6 dB. The typical one-shot sits ~4 dB above the default ceiling.

What each r_* clips

The clamp hits the highest-amplitude samples — for a percussive hit, the attack transient that defines its identity. "Mean clipped" looks small precisely because the attack is a few samples, but those are the perceptually load-bearing ones. Lifted RMS stays ≈1.0 in every row (s_a = 1/r_*); only the headroom changes.

Decision. Set rms_target = 0.1, lift_scale = 10.0 (20 dB ceiling ≈ dataset p90): median file untouched, only the punchiest ~9% clip their single loudest peak. Inheriting WavFlow's 0.33 would flat-top the attack on 80% of samples — actively harmful for this domain. The two lift knobs must be matched to this dataset's crest distribution, not WavFlow's low-crest 8 s content; r_* · s_a = 1 preserves the prior match regardless. With clipping removed, the lift becomes cheap enough to A/B fairly against no-lift.

Reproduce: python scripts/crest_factor_check.py <root>. Related fix: the lift's clamp/s_a ordering was corrected in trainer.py (s_a · clamp(…), not clamp(s_a · …)) so the lifted target reaches unit RMS rather than being re-clipped to ±1. The hard clamp was then replaced with tanh so the residual top-percentile peaks compress smoothly instead of flat-topping (near-linear at r_*=0.1, so the body is still just unit-RMS-normalised).
